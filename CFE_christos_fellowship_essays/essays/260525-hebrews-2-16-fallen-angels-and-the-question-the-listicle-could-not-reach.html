Hebrews 2:16, the Fallen Angels, and the Question the Listicle Could Not Reach: A Fellowship Essay on Why Humans Can Be Forgiven When Angels Cannot

By: Thomas Lee Abshier, ND

Date: May 25, 2026

Fellowship Discussion Essay | May 25, 2026

Occasion. A piece arrived in circulation this week through MSN.com — a syndicated article from a site called Angelynum, titled 10 Reasons God did not Forgive Fallen Angels. The piece is recognizably what its host page is: SEO-optimized inspirational content, the kind of listicle that performs well in algorithmic feeds and accumulates traffic on the strength of provocative headline plus tidy numbered structure. The host site, Angelynum, is by its broader catalog primarily a publisher of angel-number, manifestation, and spiritual-signs material — the kind of pop-spirituality content that markets angels as positive presences one consults for guidance and good fortune. The piece on fallen angels therefore sits somewhat at the dark end of the host's normal catalog, but the genre constraints remain the same: ten items, short paragraphs, repeated framing, no biblical citations, no engagement with the theological tradition, no Christology.

I want to engage this piece for two reasons. The first is that the underlying theological question — why does the classical Christian tradition hold that the fallen angels are not redeemed? — is one of the deepest and most theologically illuminating questions in the Christian deposit, and the listicle handles it badly enough that the fellowship can do something useful by handling it well. The second is that the genre itself is worth examining. The form of the SEO-listicle, applied to a topic of this depth, is a corrosion of theological seriousness. The fellowship should be aware of how the form distorts the content, both as a discipline of our own reading and as a discipline for those of us who occasionally write online and may be tempted by the click-economy's incentives.

This essay does three things. It identifies what the listicle gets approximately right within the bounds of the genre. It identifies what the listicle misses — most centrally, the Christological hinge that the classical tradition treats as the actual answer to the question. And it addresses the deeper question the article does not ask: why CAN humans be forgiven when angels cannot? The answer to that question is not in the listicle. The answer to that question is the gospel.

I. The genre problem

Before the theology, the form. The MSN listicle as a genre is the natural product of three converging incentives. The algorithm rewards headlines that promise enumerated answers. The reader's attention rewards short bullet-style paragraphs. The publisher's revenue model rewards page-views, scroll-depth, and time-on-page, which numbered lists deliver more reliably than discursive prose. The result is a form that is structurally suited to one kind of content (consumer-product comparisons, travel destinations, quick-tip articles) and structurally unsuited to another kind of content (theology, philosophy, history, anything where the depth of the topic resists segmentation into ten interchangeable bullets).

The form has three predictable failure modes when applied to deep topics:

Redundancy. A topic with three or four substantive points must be padded to ten for the headline's promise. The padding is accomplished by restating the same underlying claim in slightly different words across multiple numbered items. The reader, scanning, perceives ten distinct reasons; the careful reader, on close reading, finds three or four reasons each repeated several times. The Angelynum piece illustrates this exactly. Of its ten reasons, items 1, 3, 4, 7, and 9 all reduce to the same underlying claim — they rebelled willfully with full knowledge and refused to repent. Items 2, 6, and 8 reduce to they caused harm by influencing humans toward evil. Items 5 and 10 are variations on their punishment is final and serves as a warning. The actual substantive content is approximately four points, dressed up as ten.

Surface plausibility without depth. Each bullet sounds reasonable, because each bullet is short enough that the reader cannot easily identify what it is not saying. The Angelynum piece, considered bullet by bullet, says nothing flagrantly wrong. Considered as a treatment of its stated subject, the piece omits everything that would actually answer the question it claims to address. The reader leaves the article feeling informed, having received no information at the level the topic requires.

Absence of sourcing. The listicle form is allergic to citation. Footnotes break the scan-friendly flow. Discussion of primary sources requires paragraphs the form cannot accommodate. The Angelynum piece gestures vaguely at the Bible, particularly in Genesis and other Jewish texts (item 2) and at religious texts (multiple items) and at some religious scholars (item 4), but does not name a single biblical passage, a single theologian, a single canonical or extracanonical source. The reader has no way to follow up, no way to check, no way to evaluate. This is not laziness on the writer's part; it is a structural feature of the form. The form does not permit citation, and so the form cannot be theology.

The fellowship's relation to this kind of content should be clear. We do not have to treat the listicle as if it were a serious work simply because it appears on a major host site. We do not have to engage it on its own terms. The right relation is the relation Thomas Aquinas had toward the looser teachers of his time: take whatever is true in what they say, give them credit for what they get right, identify carefully what they have omitted, and then do the actual work the topic deserves. The actual work is what follows.

II. What the Angelynum piece gets approximately right

Set the genre problem aside for a moment. Within its limits, what does the piece get right?

The piece affirms the broadly traditional Christian doctrine that the fallen angels are not redeemed. This is, on the surface, in line with the mainstream of Christian theology across most of its history. The relevant New Testament passages — 2 Peter 2:4 (God spared not the angels that sinned, but cast them down to hell, and delivered them into chains of darkness, to be reserved unto judgment), Jude 6 (the angels which kept not their first estate, but left their own habitation, he hath reserved in everlasting chains under darkness unto the judgment of the great day), Matthew 25:41 (everlasting fire, prepared for the devil and his angels), and Revelation 12:7-9 (Michael's war with the dragon and his angels, who are cast down) — all affirm a settled, final state for the fallen angels that does not contemplate restoration. Augustine in The City of God (Book XI), Aquinas in the Summa (I, qq. 63-64), John Damascene in De Fide Orthodoxa, and the broader patristic and medieval tradition treat the angels' fall as permanent and irreversible. The Council of Constantinople II (553) condemned the Origenist doctrine of apokatastasis — the eventual restoration of all rational creatures including demons — as heretical. The classical Christian tradition, on this question, is settled.

So when the Angelynum piece says, in various ways across its ten items, that the fallen angels are not redeemed, that their state is final, that their rebellion was willful and complete, that their refusal to repent is structurally different from human repentance, and that their punishment serves as a warning to creation about the seriousness of rebellion against God — the piece is, within the bounds of these very general claims, in continuity with the mainstream of the tradition. The piece does not invent its broad framework. It restates, with greatly reduced precision, what the tradition has held for nearly two thousand years.

This matters for fairness. The Angelynum piece is not a heresy. It is a thin, redundant, badly-sourced, structurally inadequate restatement of an orthodox claim. The thinness is the problem; the orthodoxy is not. The fellowship can credit what is approximately right while attending to what is missing.

III. The redundancy: ten reasons that are really four

For clarity, let me restate the piece's ten items in their actual underlying form. Items 1, 3, 4, 7, and 9 all assert variations on the same claim: that the fallen angels rebelled with full knowledge, willfully, in pride, refusing to repent, in defiance of God's authority. This is one claim, repeated five times. Items 2, 6, and 8 assert variations on a second claim: that the fallen angels caused harm to humanity by influencing humans toward sin, violence, lust, and pride. This is one claim, repeated three times. Item 5 asserts that the angelic sin is by its nature permanent and irreversible. Item 10 asserts that the punishment serves a pedagogical function for other rational creatures.

So the piece's actual substantive content is approximately:

  1. Angels rebelled with full knowledge and refuse to repent.
  2. Their rebellion harmed humanity.
  3. Their sin is permanent by nature.
  4. Their punishment serves as a warning.

Four points. This is not in itself bad theology — each of the four points has standing in the classical tradition. But the article's stretched-out listicle form obscures the relationship among the four points, and more critically, it conceals what the four points do not say.

What do they not say? They do not say why the angelic sin is permanent and the human sin is not. They do not say why human repentance is structurally possible when angelic repentance is not. They do not say what it is about angels — as opposed to humans — that produces this difference. They do not say anything about the Incarnation. They do not name the Christological hinge that the classical tradition treats as the actual answer to the question they pose.

The question the article asks is: why does God not forgive the fallen angels? The answer the article gives is, in essence, because their sin was serious enough to deserve permanent damnation. But this answer simply pushes the question back one step. Human sins are also serious enough to deserve permanent damnation. Why are human sins forgivable, then? The article does not ask. The article cannot ask, because the listicle form does not accommodate the question.

We will ask it.

IV. The Christological hinge: Hebrews 2:16

The single most important passage in the New Testament on this question is in Hebrews 2:16, which the King James Version renders:

For verily he took not on him the nature of angels; but he took on him the seed of Abraham.

The Greek is more compact and more strikingly direct: οὐ γὰρ δήπου ἀγγέλων ἐπιλαμβάνεται, ἀλλὰ σπέρματος Ἀβραὰμ ἐπιλαμβάνεται — for not, surely, of angels does he take hold; but of the seed of Abraham does he take hold. The verb ἐπιλαμβάνεται (epilambanetai) means to take hold of, to grasp, to lay claim to — in this context, to assume a nature, to become incarnate within a nature.

The claim of Hebrews 2:16 is that the Incarnation of the Son was of the seed of Abraham — that the Son took on human nature, not angelic nature. He became a man, not an angel. He united himself with the human race, not with the angelic order. This is the load-bearing fact that the classical tradition treats as the actual answer to the question of why fallen angels are not redeemed and humans are.

The fourth-century theologian Gregory of Nazianzus articulated the principle that the medieval and Reformation traditions have followed: τὸ γὰρ ἀπρόσληπτον, ἀθεράπευτον, what is not assumed is not healed. The Greek formulation is one of the most condensed and precise statements in the whole Christian theological deposit. What is not assumed is not healed. Christ heals human nature by taking on human nature. He does not heal angelic nature because he does not take on angelic nature. Therefore the redemption made available through the Incarnation is available to humans by structural participation in the Incarnate Son's human nature; it is not available to angels, because there is no Incarnate Son in angelic nature for them to participate in.

This is the answer the listicle could not reach. The fallen angels are not unforgiven because their sin was too serious; the fallen angels are unforgiven because the Son did not become an angel for them. The structural mechanism by which the human race is redeemed is the assumption of human nature by the Second Person of the Trinity. There is no parallel assumption in angelic nature. The Incarnation was for us, not for them. He took not on him the nature of angels; but he took on him the seed of Abraham.

This reading is not idiosyncratic. It is the reading of the patristic consensus, the medieval scholastic consensus, the Reformation consensus, and the modern Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, Reformed, Lutheran, and broadly evangelical consensus. The Incarnation-specific-to-humans is the load-bearing fact. Everything else in the doctrine of the angels' permanent fall depends on it.

V. Why this is the answer rather than the moralistic answer

It is worth pausing to mark the difference between the answer the listicle gives and the answer the classical tradition gives. The listicle's answer is moralistic: the angels are unforgiven because their sin was severe enough to merit unforgiveness. The classical tradition's answer is structural: the angels are unforgiven because the structural mechanism by which forgiveness is mediated (the Incarnation) was effected in human nature, not angelic nature.

The difference matters for several reasons.

The moralistic answer is theologically unstable. If the angels are unforgiven because their sin was severe, then the implication is that severity of sin is the determining factor. But human sin can also be severe. Hitler's sin was severe. The medieval inquisitor's sin was severe. The drug cartel's sin is severe. The sex trafficker's sin is severe. Yet the tradition holds that all of these human sins are, in principle, forgivable through the mediation of Christ. The severity-criterion cannot be the actual criterion, because severity does not track with the human/angelic distinction in the way the moralistic framing would require.

The structural answer is theologically stable. If the angels are unforgiven because the Incarnation was effected in human nature only, then the distinction between forgivable human sin and unforgivable angelic sin tracks with the actual structural fact of the gospel: that the Son became man, and that participation in the redemption he effected is available through the human nature he assumed. The moralistic question (was the sin severe enough?) is replaced by the gospel question (is the sinner human, and is the redemption-channel available to him through the Incarnate Christ?). The answer for humans is yes — for every human, regardless of the severity of his particular sins. The answer for angels is no — not because their particular sins are too severe, but because the redemption-channel is not structurally available to angelic nature.

The structural answer is good news for us. This is the part that the listicle entirely fails to communicate. The reason the fallen angels are not redeemed and we are is not that we are morally better than they are. The reason is that the Son took on our nature. The asymmetry between humans and angels in the matter of forgiveness is the central good news of the Christian gospel. He took not on him the nature of angels; but he took on him the seed of Abraham is, for every human being who reads it, the announcement that the redemption-channel is open to him personally — not because he deserves it, but because the Son became what he is. The listicle ends with the moralistic warning that mercy is not automatic when someone knowingly and intentionally rejects God. The Christological hinge ends with the announcement that mercy is structurally available, through the Incarnate Son, to every human being who comes to him. The two endings are not the same. One is law. The other is gospel.

VI. The classical theological account: Augustine and Aquinas

Let me develop the classical account a little further, because it bears on how the fellowship should understand what the article gestures at but does not explain.

Augustine, in The City of God Book XI, develops the fall of the angels in connection with the fall of man, but distinguishes them sharply. The angels, in Augustine's account, were created with intellectual natures that grasped truth immediately and completely. Their wills, having exercised themselves in choice, exercised themselves with full knowledge of what they were choosing. The fallen angels, having turned from God, turned with their whole being and at the moment of their fall — there was no slow erosion, no partial knowledge, no mixed motives, no later opportunity for the kind of slow reconsideration that humans engage in over decades of lived experience. The angelic fall, for Augustine, was complete in a single act of will, and that complete-in-a-single-act-of-will quality is what makes it irrevocable.

Aquinas, in Summa Theologiae I, qq. 63-64, develops this further. The angelic intellect, in Aquinas, does not deliberate the way the human intellect does. The human intellect proceeds discursively — from premise to conclusion, with the possibility of revising premises in light of new information, with the further possibility of changing one's mind. The angelic intellect, being a higher kind of intellect, grasps its conclusions in a single intuitive act. Once an angel has grasped a conclusion and committed his will to it, there is no later deliberative process to revise it. The conclusion is held in its full clarity and with full will. The fallen angel does not change his mind because there is no temporal-deliberative structure to his mind for change to occur in. The fallen angel is in the eternal state of having chosen rebellion with full knowledge, and that state is the state he is in.

This is not a punishment imposed from outside. It is the natural consequence of the kind of being an angel is. The angel does not regret his sin the way a human regrets his sins, because he does not have the temporal-revisional structure in which regret operates. He is fixed in his choice — not because God arbitrarily fixed him, but because his nature as an intellectual being of the angelic kind is to commit his will completely, and his will was committed to rebellion at the moment of his fall.

The human is structurally different. The human intellect is discursive. The human will is composite — bound up with embodiment, with affections, with memory, with the temporal-revisional process by which human beings move through their lives. Humans can change their minds. Humans can come to see what they did not see before. Humans can experience the slow movements of conscience over years that produce, in the end, a genuine metanoia — a turning of the whole person toward God. The structure of human repentance is possible because the structure of human cognition and will permits it.

This is the structural answer underneath the Christological hinge. The Incarnation was effected in human nature because human nature is the kind of nature in which repentance is structurally possible. The angels' nature does not have repentance as a structural possibility, because the angel commits his will completely in a single act and does not deliberate over time. The Son became man because man is the creature who can come back. The Son did not become an angel because the angel, having gone, is gone in the only way the angel can go.

This is harder than the listicle's flat they refused to repent. The classical position is that the fallen angels could not repent in the way humans repent, because the structure of angelic cognition and will does not have repentance as a possibility. Refused and could not are not synonyms. The listicle uses the language of refusal because the language of refusal preserves the moralistic framing. The classical tradition uses the language of structural impossibility because the structural impossibility is what is actually the case.

VII. The speculative elements: where the tradition is less settled

The classical tradition's account of the fall of the angels has its settled core (the angels are not redeemed; the Son took on human nature, not angelic nature; the Incarnation is the structural ground of redemption) and its more speculative periphery. The listicle, with characteristic flatness, treats the whole thing as if it were equally settled. The fellowship should know where the tradition is firm and where it is less firm.

The reason for the angelic rebellion. The traditional answer is pride — Lucifer's I will ascend into heaven, I will exalt my throne above the stars of God (Isaiah 14:13), read with Ezekiel 28:11-19 as referring typologically to a fallen angelic being beyond the king of Tyre in the immediate context. This interpretation goes back to early Christian readings of those passages. It is not, however, universally accepted; some modern biblical scholars argue that Isaiah 14 is addressed to a human king of Babylon and Ezekiel 28 to a human king of Tyre, with no necessary angelic referent. The classical Christian reading remains the dominant Christian reading, but it depends on a typological/Christological hermeneutic that not all serious interpreters accept. The Angelynum listicle treats they wanted power for themselves (item 4) as flat fact. It is in the tradition, but it depends on a particular reading of contested biblical passages.

The role of fallen angels in human sin. The classical tradition affirms that the fallen angels seek to lead humans into sin (Ephesians 6:12, 1 Peter 5:8, the temptation accounts in the Gospels). The further claim that the bene elohim (sons of God) in Genesis 6:1-4 are fallen angels who took human wives and produced the Nephilim is a real but contested interpretation. The Watcher tradition draws this out at length in 1 Enoch (a non-canonical book of significant influence in Second Temple Judaism and the early church), and 2 Peter and Jude both appear to allude to it. But other historic Christian readings of Genesis 6 (the Sethite-Cainite reading, where the sons of God are the descendants of Seth intermarrying with the descendants of Cain) have substantial standing as well. The Angelynum listicle treats item 2 — they corrupted humanity, teaching them forbidden knowledge — as if the Watcher interpretation were the settled reading. It is one reading among several. Serious fellowship discussion of Genesis 6 needs to account for the others.

The pedagogical-warning function. The listicle's item 10, that the punishment of the fallen angels serves as a warning to other rational creatures, is in the tradition (Jude 6 says they are reserved unto the judgment of the great day, which has pedagogical weight) but is not the primary purpose of their state. Their state is the natural consequence of their being and choice, not primarily a pedagogical performance for the benefit of others. The pedagogical effect is real but secondary.

The question of universal restoration. The Council of Constantinople II (553) condemned Origen's apokatastasis — the doctrine that all rational creatures, including the demons, would eventually be restored to God. This condemnation has held in the mainstream of Catholic and Orthodox theology. Some modern theologians (David Bentley Hart, for example, in his That All Shall Be Saved) have raised the universalist question again in connection with humans, though most have not extended the universalist argument to the angels. The classical position remains the settled position. But fellowship members reading widely in contemporary theology may encounter the apokatastasis discussion and should know where it sits in the tradition: condemned in the sixth century, raised again in the twenty-first, mainstream theology continues to hold the classical position, the question is not entirely closed in the broader contemporary discussion.

VIII. The pastoral implications for the fellowship

What does the fellowship take from this engagement?

First, the discipline of theological depth. A topic of this magnitude — the fall of the angels, the structure of redemption, the relationship of the Incarnation to human salvation — cannot be addressed in ten SEO-padded bullets. The listicle form is corrosive to the content. The fellowship's reading of theological material on the internet should include an attentional discipline: is this form adequate to this topic? If the answer is no, the material can be set aside, however clickable the headline.

Second, the Christological hinge. He took not on him the nature of angels; but he took on him the seed of Abraham (Hebrews 2:16) is one of the most important sentences in the Christian deposit on the question of who is redeemed and why. The fellowship should know it. It is the answer to questions the secular world is not asking but that come up wherever serious religious conversation goes deep enough — questions about the fairness of redemption, the structure of grace, the goodness of God in distinguishing among creatures. The answer is the Incarnation. The Son became man. He did not become an angel. The redemption channel is structurally available to humans, through the Incarnate Son, in a way that it is not structurally available to angels.

Third, the gospel-versus-law distinction. The listicle ends with law: mercy is not automatic when someone knowingly and intentionally rejects God. This is true as far as it goes. But it is not the gospel. The gospel is the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, full of grace and truth (John 1:14). The gospel is that the Son took on what we are so that we could share in what he is. The fellowship should not let the listicle's law-ending be the last word on the topic. The last word on the topic is grace — for those of us who, by no virtue of our own, are members of the species the Son became.

Fourth, the seriousness of human repentance. The structural availability of repentance to humans is not a license for casualness about it. The fallen angels did not have repentance as a structural possibility; humans do. The exercise of that possibility is the central work of the human spiritual life. Repent ye therefore, and be converted, that your sins may be blotted out, when the times of refreshing shall come from the presence of the Lord (Acts 3:19). The fellowship's working life is the cultivation of habits of repentance — the daily, weekly, yearly returning to God in the discursive-deliberative way that human cognition makes possible. We have what the angels did not have. We owe it to the One who took on our nature to use what he gave us.

Fifth, awareness of the broader genre context. Angelynum and sites like it are not just isolated publishers of theological listicles. They are part of a much larger pop-spiritual content economy that markets angels as positive consultative presences (the angel-number content, the manifestation content, the spiritual-signs content) and occasionally produces darker pieces (this fallen-angel listicle) for variety and for engagement metrics. The genre is not theologically careful, and the fellowship should be aware that material reaching us through major host sites (MSN, Yahoo, AOL, the various aggregators) often originates in publishers whose primary content is not in serious continuity with the Christian theological tradition, even when individual pieces gesture in vaguely orthodox directions. Reading well includes knowing what you are reading.

IX. Closing

The Angelynum piece asks: why did God not forgive the fallen angels? The answer the piece gives is, in essence: because their sin was too serious. The answer the classical Christian tradition gives is different and deeper: because the Son took on the seed of Abraham, not the nature of angels; because what is not assumed is not healed; because the structural mechanism of redemption is the assumption of human nature by the Incarnate Word, and that assumption was effected in our nature, not in theirs.

The answer is the gospel, and the gospel — for those of us who are human — is the best news a creature can hear. We can be forgiven. The angels who fell are in the state they are in. The humans who fell are not, because the Son became one of us, and what he became, he healed.

Forasmuch then as the children are partakers of flesh and blood, he also himself likewise took part of the same; that through death he might destroy him that had the power of death, that is, the devil; and deliver them who through fear of death were all their lifetime subject to bondage. For verily he took not on him the nature of angels; but he took on him the seed of Abraham. Wherefore in all things it behoved him to be made like unto his brethren, that he might be a merciful and faithful high priest in things pertaining to God, to make reconciliation for the sins of the people.

— Hebrews 2:14-17

— Thomas
