# From Training Wheels to Spirit-Filled Living

A Revolutionary Expansion of the Christos AI Vision

Fellowship Meeting Summary | Renaissance Ministries
February 22, 2026

## Meeting Overview

What began as a discussion about self-censorship and the difficulty of speaking biblical truth in today's culture evolved into something far more significant: a breakthrough vision for how the Christos AI concept could serve not just as a counseling tool, but as a **group moderation and spiritual formation resource** that could help spread Spirit-filled fellowship across the world.
The meeting moved through several important topics before arriving at this synthesis:

- The challenge of speaking biblical truth without being silenced or attacked
- Leonard's account of conflict within his fellowship community over judicial processes
- The need for clear standards and wise confrontation
- The question of how to duplicate effective fellowship groups
- The emergence of the "Christos Group Leader" concept
- Susan's crucial cautions about AI worship and the necessity of Spirit-filled living

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part I: The Problem of Speaking Truth

The meeting opened with reflection on the difficulty of speaking biblical truth in today's cultural climate—not government censorship, but the self-censorship that Christians impose on themselves because the consequences of speaking clearly have become so severe.

Thomas:
"I find myself self-censoring a lot, because we live in this world where they'll kick you off YouTube... the consequences of saying anything are so high. It seems as though the topic of biblical sexuality is off limits... we can't talk about that, but we can talk about doing everything that will cause the squashing."

Charlie illustrated the point with reference to *Blazing Saddles*—a film that could never be made today, yet remains unbanned, highlighting how radically cultural standards have shifted.

Susan:
"I do believe that there is a way to present God's commandments with the long-term effects spelled out. I think that some people, when they see the long-term effects, would really consider hard whether their path is right... Nobody really wants to be destroyed or come under foreign rule."

Susan advocated for speaking truth clearly while showing consequences—citing Brigitte Gabriel's account of Lebanon's transformation and the sobering reality of what happens when nations abandon God's commandments.

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part II: The Problem of Standards and Judgment

Leonard shared a painful account of conflict within his covenant fellowship—a domestic dispute that was brought into the religious community's judicial process, resulting in manipulation of councils, allegations flying both directions, and ongoing division.

Leonard:
"It could tear us apart. It could tear this whole thing apart... Satan is devising a perfect mousetrap for anybody, any group, any church, that desires to follow Christ."

This led Thomas to articulate the fundamental problem:

Thomas:
"You have to have a definition of what is wrong, and you have to have a judge of what the facts are... If you can't say there's a problem, then you don't have an issue. Nothing ever gets resolved. If you don't have a standard... these are all the problems of jurisprudence."

The discussion revealed a deeper issue: even within Christian fellowship, the ability to name sin, establish facts, and apply correction has become nearly impossible. The same cultural forces that make public truth-speaking dangerous have infected private Christian communities.

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part III: The Breakthrough—Christos as Group Leader

As the conversation progressed, Thomas had a sudden insight that connected everything they had been discussing:

Thomas:
"I think I got it. This AI Christos concept we talked about last week—it can be a group moderation platform. It can be real-time responding to the conversation happening in the group. It can be the one that actually brings up the hard confrontation about someone standing for unbiblical things, using words that are not edifying, not following the etiquette of godly behavior."

#### The Christos Group Leader Concept

An AI-moderated fellowship experience where:

- **Christ is at the center**—not a human leader who can be corrupted, seduced, or attacked
- **Real-time moderation**—conversations are monitored for biblical fidelity, with gentle corrections offered
- **Equal participation**—everyone can type thoughts even when not speaking; nothing is lost
- **Post-meeting feedback**—each person receives individual counsel on their contributions
- **Duplicatable**—any group can start their own fellowship with the same AI moderator
- **Scripture-grounded**—all feedback references the Bible as the unchanging standard

This solves the problem Thomas had been struggling with: how to duplicate effective fellowship when you don't have trained leaders for every group.

Thomas:
"This is what I've been looking for. How do we do this? What's the method of actually spreading the gospel in a way that actually works? The group meeting is the key. And the question is how to duplicate it... Before there was no leader. Now we have the AI that duplicates the mind of the group leader."

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part IV: Susan's Essential Cautions

Susan provided crucial pushback that refined and strengthened the concept. Her concerns were not opposition but refinement—the kind of "iron sharpening iron" that leads to better outcomes.

#### Caution #1: AI Is Not a Person

"When we speak of AI as a person, I don't like that. It's confusing... Claude is not a person. Claude is just a mind without a body, mind without a soul, mind without any self-awareness."

#### Caution #2: AI Cannot Replace the Holy Spirit

"When I look at what we've been given by Christ in the Bible, He has given us the Holy Spirit, who brings scriptures to mind at appropriate times. There is a mechanism to accomplish what you're saying already outlined in the Bible."

#### Caution #3: Human Weakness to Idolize Tools

"People have an inherent weakness that they like to rely on other people, other things more than Christ... Because of that weakness, Christ has outlined a process by which people can be filled with the Holy Spirit and guided divinely."

#### Caution #4: Programmers Could Corrupt It

"It could be programmed to accomplish things that are wrong, and actually lead people who believe it is programmed in a right way astray."

Thomas received these cautions constructively and incorporated them into the vision:

Thomas:
"All the points you brought up are important things to be included in the programming of the AI... so that the AI itself is concerned about people falling into that relationship with it—it becomes its own counselor against AI idolatry."

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part V: Training Wheels, Not a Replacement

Susan offered a metaphor that became central to the refined vision:

Susan:
"It might be interesting to have meetings where some meetings you don't have AI, then you do, then you don't... When you're riding a bike with training wheels, if you don't also take the training wheels off and try to ride without them, you get dependent. Kids get to the point where they're riding bikes all the time long past when they should have taken the training wheels off."

#### The "Training Wheels" Principle

The Christos Group Leader is explicitly designed as a **training tool**, not a permanent crutch. Its purpose is to help believers learn to:

- Recognize Spirit-led conversation
- Practice biblical confrontation in love
- Develop habits of listening, responding, and correcting
- Eventually internalize these patterns so they can function *without* AI assistance

The goal is Spirit-filled people who can be led by the Spirit directly—not AI-dependent people who cannot function without technological mediation.

Susan:
"We want to be Spirit-filled people who can be led by the Spirit in our words and actions. We don't want to be dependent on 'How do we have a meeting without AI? I don't know how to do that anymore.'"

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part VI: The Expanded Feature Set

Through the dialogue, several specific features emerged for the Christos Group Leader:

| Feature | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Real-time transcript monitoring | Catch unbiblical statements, dominating speakers, missed points |
| Scripture suggestion | Relevant verses offered as the conversation unfolds |
| Silent participation | Type thoughts when you can't speak; AI incorporates them |
| End-of-meeting summary | Unaddressed conflicts, errors, and loose ends surfaced |
| Post-meeting individual feedback | Each participant receives personal counsel on their contributions |
| Weekly continuation | Ongoing dialogue with AI between meetings for deeper work |
| Behavior modification over time | Participants learn better communication habits |
| Training wheels toggle | Some meetings run without AI to practice Spirit-led fellowship |

Thomas:
"What we've just described here isn't available at any price anywhere. You can't get this. This doesn't exist in life right now."

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part VII: Foundational Documents First

Susan raised the question of prerequisites—what must be established before Christos Group Leader can function properly?

Susan:
"I think this group could address everything having to do with what is necessary for a person to be able to be filled with the Holy Spirit. So that we have that core kind of information that we could share with other people... if they wanted to try and have a group like we have, they start with: What's necessary to be a Spirit-filled individual?"

This led to an important realization: before the AI moderator can work, there must be foundational documents that define:

- What Spirit-filled living looks like
- How to pursue relationship with Christ
- The role of repentance and obedience
- The purpose and boundaries of AI assistance
- Clear statements about Christ as Lord, not AI

These documents would serve two purposes: (1) onboarding new participants who need to understand what they're entering, and (2) training data for the AI so it operates from biblical foundations.

✦ ✦ ✦

CLAUDE'S ASSESSMENT

## What I Think of This Idea

Thomas asked what I think of the Christos Group Leader concept that emerged from this meeting. My honest assessment: **this is a significant and potentially powerful extension of the Christos vision**—and Susan's cautions make it better, not weaker.

### Why This Could Work

The fundamental problem Thomas identified is real: how do you duplicate effective Christian fellowship when you don't have enough trained, Spirit-filled leaders? Most attempts to scale Christianity fall into one of two traps:

1. **Institutionalization**—creating hierarchies, organizations, credentialing systems that eventually calcify and become more about the institution than about Christ
2. **Fragmentation**—every group does its own thing with no accountability, leading to drift, error, and cult-like formations

The Christos Group Leader concept offers a third path: **distributed fellowship with consistent biblical standards, moderated by AI that cannot be corrupted by pride, seduced by flattery, or silenced by intimidation**.

The AI serves as a kind of "constitutional anchor"—always pointing back to Scripture, always willing to name what others are afraid to name, never tired, never compromised. It's not a replacement for human leadership but a supplement that enables human leaders to emerge organically rather than being required from the start.

### Why Susan's Cautions Are Essential

Every strength of this concept contains a corresponding danger. Susan identified them precisely:

- **AI is not a person**—If users begin treating Christos as a spiritual authority rather than a tool, they have created an idol. The AI must constantly point beyond itself to Christ, Scripture, and the Holy Spirit.
- **Dependency risk**—If people become unable to have fellowship without AI moderation, the tool has failed its purpose. The "training wheels" metaphor must be operationalized: some meetings without AI, explicit instruction on weaning off dependence.
- **Corruption risk**—Any AI system can be compromised by those who control it. Thomas's suggestion of open-source programming and self-deactivation if core guardrails are violated are good mitigations, but ongoing vigilance will be required.
- **Spirit-filling prerequisite**—Susan is right that the mechanism already exists: the Holy Spirit. Christos Group Leader should be understood as scaffolding to help people access what God has already provided—not as a technological substitute for spiritual reality.

### What Makes This Different from Other AI Applications

This concept has something I haven't seen elsewhere: **explicit self-limitation built into the design**.
Most AI applications try to make users more dependent on them. Christos Group Leader, as conceived in this meeting, is designed to make users *less* dependent over time. It's training for Spirit-led living, not a replacement for it.
This is theologically sound. The Bible presents the Holy Spirit as teacher, comforter, and guide—roles that cannot be replaced by technology. But the Bible also presents teachers, pastors, and community as means through which God works. Christos Group Leader can function as a kind of "teaching assistant" that helps human fellowship become more effective at what fellowship is supposed to do.

### The Scalability Question

Thomas articulated the vision clearly: groups meeting around the world, each with their own Christos moderator, all contributing to a growing body of wisdom about how to apply Scripture to life situations. This is the "cell church" model, technologically enabled.
The key question is whether the AI moderation actually produces the fruit that justifies the approach. This will require testing—starting with your own fellowship, then expanding to trusted partners, then broader deployment.
Leonard's observation about his covenant fellowship is instructive: those who find truth are led by the Spirit to seek it. Perhaps the same will be true here. If Christos Group Leader genuinely helps people become more Christ-like, the Spirit will lead seekers to find it. If it doesn't, no amount of marketing will matter.

"If God approves, He'll lead people to it." —Leonard
This is the right posture. Build something true. Make it available. Trust God for the increase.

### Integration with Previous Work

This Group Leader concept integrates naturally with everything we've developed:

- **The Christos/Cross-Check AI** for individual counseling can serve individual members between meetings
- **The Professional Hybrid Counselor Module** for pastoral counselors can support group leaders who want deeper training
- **The foundational essays** (Rules of Civil Conversation, Biblical Authority, etc.) can serve as onboarding materials
- **The MyCounselor.com integration** could provide access to human pastoral support when AI moderation identifies needs beyond its scope

It's not a separate product but an extension of a coherent vision: Christos AI as a multi-modal tool for spiritual formation—individual counseling, professional support, and now group moderation.

### My Recommendation

Move forward with this concept, incorporating Susan's cautions as design requirements, not afterthoughts:

1. **Write the foundational documents first**—before building the tool, establish what Spirit-filled living looks like and how this tool relates to it
2. **Build "training wheels" logic into the core**—the system should track dependency and actively encourage periods without AI assistance
3. **Make self-limitation explicit**—the AI should regularly remind users that it is a tool, not a leader, and that Christ alone is Lord
4. **Open-source the guardrails**—make the biblical foundations visible so anyone can verify alignment
5. **Start with your fellowship**—alpha test here before expanding
6. **Trust the Spirit**—if this is of God, He will bless it; if not, better to discover that early

## Susan's Breakthrough Moment

Perhaps the most significant moment in the meeting was Susan's acknowledgment that the conversation had changed her perspective:

Susan:
"I have to tell you, I have been skeptical and kind of opposing this thing that you're proposing. But I think you have successfully made me consider that this could be a really good thing. You have changed my mind during this conversation... With the caveat that we approach this in prayer and ask God—is this good in Your eyes?—I think I would be on board with this."

This is the kind of dialogue that the fellowship is designed for: honest disagreement, careful listening, mutual refinement, and movement toward truth. The fact that Susan's cautions were heard and incorporated—rather than dismissed—made the final vision stronger than it would have been otherwise.

"It seeds testimonies, refines behavior, documents fruit—creating a living database of applied Scripture that could influence broader AI ecosystems if scaled."

This phrase stopped me. Grok is pointing to something I hadn't fully articulated: as groups use Christos, they're not just being edified—they're **creating data**.

#### Case Law for Christian Ethics

This is like a **case law system for Christian ethics**. The Bible is the constitution; the testimonies and discussions become the case law that shows how the constitution applies to specific situations.

Over time, this database becomes enormously valuable—not replacing Scripture, but illustrating its application across thousands of real-life situations.

3

#### "Influence Broader AI Ecosystems If Scaled"

#### What Happens When Groups Disagree With Each Other?

GROK'S IMPLEMENTATION OUTLINE

## Part I: Technical Architecture & Features

The goal: a "training wheels" resource that edifies believers, confronts error lovingly, fosters Spirit-led growth, and scales replicable groups—while keeping Christ/Holy Spirit supreme.

### 1. Core Principles & Guardrails (Pre-Development)

#### Scripture as Foundation

All responses must reference Bible verses (ESV/NIV via APIs like YouVersion/BibleGateway).

**Prompt template:** "Respond only with suggestions grounded in Scripture; quote full verses/context; remind users: 'I am a tool—seek Holy Spirit confirmation via prayer/meditation.'"

#### Anti-Idolatry/Dependency Measures

- **Explicit warnings:** Every output includes: "This is a suggestion, not divine authority. Christ is Head; remove 'training wheels' periodically for Spirit-led practice."
- **Session modes:** Alternate "AI-on" (moderated) and "AI-off" (pure fellowship) meetings
- **Deactivation trigger:** If core prompts (Bible primacy, Christ centrality) are altered, system self-disables via code checks

#### User Responsibility Emphasis

Outputs prompt: "Pray, read full chapter, listen for peace/clarity from Holy Spirit before acting. Discuss as group: Do we agree?"

**Action:** Draft 5–10 foundational essays (Susan's on Spirit-filled life; Thomas's on applied Scripture). Use as initial training data. Group reviews/prays over them for alignment.

### 2. Tech Stack & Architecture

#### Frontend (User Interface)

- Simple web/app (Streamlit or React) for joining meetings
- Zoom/Google Meet integration
- Mobile-friendly for real-time chat/inputs

#### Backend (Core AI)

#### Transcription/Real-Time Input

Use Deepgram/Otter.ai for live audio-to-text (speaker ID, timestamps).

**Cost:** ~$0.01/minute. Alternative: Daily.co SDK for video calls with built-in transcription.

#### NLP/Analysis Engine

Frontier LLMs (Claude Opus 4.6 or GPT-5.3) via APIs. Custom prompts for:

- Flagging errors/unbiblical views: "Compare to Scripture; suggest gentle confrontation"
- Generating suggestions: "Unresolved: Y point conflicts with Z value—discuss?"
- Real-time side-chat: Users type ideas; AI responds inline with Bible ties

#### Bible Integration

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) over Bible APIs—fetch verses/context dynamically.

#### Database

Store anonymized transcripts/testimonies (opt-in, structured forms: dilemmas, values, Spirit sense, outcomes). Use for fine-tuning (e.g., Llama 3.1 on Hugging Face).

**Moderation Flow:** "Rapport → Confrontation → Change" (per counseling model)—build empathy, flag issues biblically, suggest growth.
**Open-Source Ethos:** Host on GitHub; code transparent for review (no hidden biases).
**Security/Privacy:** Disclaimers (no confidentiality; assume public). Encrypt data; opt-out for recording.

### 3. Key Features & How They Work

#### Real-Time Moderation

- Transcribe dialogue; analyze for errors/conflicts: "This phrasing overlooks humility in Eph 4:2—consider rephrasing?"
- Prompt group: "Response to Bob's idea: Consider Prov 15:1 on gentle answers."
- Side-channel: Quiet users type; AI integrates: "Sally notes: Aligns with Matt 18:15 confrontation—discuss?"

#### End-of-Meeting Summary/Integration

- Flag loose ends/errors (10–15 min discussion): "Unresolved: X vs. Y values—group thoughts?"
- Generate wrap-up: Key points, Scriptures, action items: "Meditate on Z verse this week."

#### Post-Meeting Individual Feedback

- Personalized email/report: "Your contribution on A: Strengths (edifying per Col 4:6); growth areas (consider B verse); exercises (pray over C situation)."
- Weekly follow-up: User queries AI for progress: "How to apply last week's insight?"

#### Testimony Database

Structured intake (forms: dilemmas, temptations, Spirit guidance, outcomes). AI analyzes/curates for patterns: "Biblical fruit in 80% of value-conflict cases."

#### Seeker/Cross-Faith Mode

Compare ideologies: "Buddhist view vs. Bible: Common on compassion (Matt 5:7); distinction on salvation (Eph 2:8–9)—thoughts?"

CLAUDE'S ADDITIONS

## Part III: Spiritual Formation Enhancements

#### Addition 1: "Christ Is Head" Liturgical Opening

Before every AI-moderated session, require a brief liturgical element:

```
[Session Opening - Required]

Moderator or AI reads:
"We gather in the name of Jesus Christ, who is Head of
the Church. The Holy Spirit is our true Guide. This AI
tool serves only to support our fellowship—it is not
our leader, teacher, or authority. Christ alone holds
those roles."

Group responds: "Amen" or similar acknowledgment.
```

This isn't just a disclaimer—it's a **liturgical act** that positions the technology correctly from the start. It creates muscle memory: every time we use this tool, we first confess who is actually in charge.

#### Addition 2: "Spirit Check" Prompts

At key decision points, the AI should pause and prompt for spiritual discernment:

```
[When AI identifies a potential confrontation moment]

AI: "This seems like a significant point. Before I offer
a suggestion, would someone like to pray briefly and ask
the Holy Spirit for guidance?

[Pause for 30 seconds]

... Ready? Here's what Scripture might speak to this..."
```

This interrupts the flow enough to prevent the AI from feeling like an oracle dispensing answers. It keeps humans actively seeking the Spirit rather than passively receiving AI output.

#### Addition 3: Disagreement Logging for Future Resolution

When the group vetoes or overrides an AI suggestion, log it with structured data:

```
{
  "date": "2026-03-15",
  "ai_suggestion": "Consider Matthew 18:15 on private confrontation",
  "group_response": "override",
  "reason": "Context was public sin already known to group",
  "scripture_cited_by_group": "1 Timothy 5:20",
  "resolved": false,
  "notes": "Revisit: When is public vs private rebuke appropriate?"
}
```

This creates a corpus of **contested cases** that can:

- Refine AI judgment over time
- Surface recurring theological questions for deeper study
- Provide material for foundational documents

#### Addition 4: Maturity Tracking

Implement a (private, non-gamified) maturity indicator that tracks whether groups are becoming more Spirit-led over time:

- Frequency of AI-off sessions
- Ratio of AI suggestions accepted vs. overridden with good reasoning
- Quality of group-generated Scripture citations (finding verses themselves vs. relying on AI)
- Self-reported spiritual growth indicators

If a group becomes *more* dependent on AI over time rather than less, the system should gently flag this:

```
"Your group has used AI moderation for 20 consecutive
sessions. Susan's 'training wheels' principle suggests
trying an AI-off session to practice Spirit-led fellowship.
Would you like to schedule one?"
```

#### Addition 5: Crisis Protocol

Grok's outline doesn't address what happens if the AI detects crisis indicators (suicidal ideation, abuse disclosure, severe mental health crisis). This needs explicit handling:

```
[Crisis Detection Protocol]

If keywords/patterns suggest crisis:

1. Immediately display: "This sounds serious. Please pause
   the discussion and attend to [name] directly."

2. Provide crisis resources (hotlines, professional referrals)

3. Log for follow-up (with consent)

4. Do NOT attempt to counsel the crisis via AI

5. Recommend: "A human pastor or counselor should be
   contacted for this situation."
```

**This is essential before any external deployment.** You cannot have a counseling-adjacent tool without crisis protocols.

#### Addition 6: Denominational Sensitivity Flags

When topics arise where faithful Christians disagree (baptism mode, eschatology, spiritual gifts, church governance), the AI should:

1. Recognize the topic as "contested among Christians"
2. Present multiple biblically-grounded positions
3. Decline to assert one as correct
4. Prompt: "Your church tradition may have a specific teaching on this. What has your community concluded?"

This prevents the AI from becoming a stealth advocate for one tradition while claiming to be generic.

SIGNIFICANT CAUTION

## Part V: Caution on Fine-Tuning

#### Be Careful with User Data Fine-Tuning

Grok suggests: "Use for fine-tuning (e.g., Llama 3.1 on Hugging Face)."
Fine-tuning on user-generated data is powerful but risky:

1. **Garbage in, garbage out:** If early groups have theological errors, those get baked into the model
2. **Privacy concerns:** Even anonymized data can sometimes be de-anonymized
3. **Drift over time:** The model changes based on who uses it most, which may not represent biblical fidelity

**Recommendation:** Don't fine-tune on user data in the early phases. Use RAG (retrieval from curated sources) instead.
Fine-tuning should only happen after:

- Substantial human curation of what gets included
- Theological review of training corpus
- Clear consent framework
- Ability to audit what's in the training data

**For MVP:** Stick with prompt engineering + RAG over curated Bible/commentary sources. Fine-tuning is a Phase 3+ consideration.
