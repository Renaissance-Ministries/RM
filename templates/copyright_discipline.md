# Copyright Discipline — Renaissance Ministries

**Location:** `templates/copyright_discipline.md`
**Purpose:** A loadable working rule for Claude sessions producing content in Thomas's name — fellowship essays, newsletter issues, addenda, sermon transcripts, and any material destined for the Christos AI training corpus. Pair this file with `authors_voice.md` (for *how* Thomas writes) and the Theological Grammar (for *what* he believes); this file covers *how to engage other people's work* without infringing copyright and while honoring the source.
**Status:** Initial version. April 2026.

---

## How to use this file

If you are a Claude session about to draft content that engages another person's lecture, interview, podcast, book, article, video, or other published work:

1. Read this file **before** you draft.
2. Hold the two-part posture (§1) in mind — copyright is not just defensive, it is generous.
3. Apply the working rules (§2) to every source you engage.
4. Apply the attribution-and-traffic discipline (§3) so the original creator benefits from being cited.
5. Run your draft against the audit checklist (§4) before shipping.

This file exists so that as Renaissance Ministries content scales — fellowship essays, newsletter issues, the eventual Christos AI training corpus — the body of work has zero copyright infections and a uniformly generous posture toward sources. The discipline you build into early content compounds into trustworthy infrastructure later.

## 1. The two-part posture

Copyright discipline at Renaissance Ministries has two simultaneous aims, and both must be present in every piece:

### Part 1 — Defensive: Do no legal harm

Do not reproduce more of someone else's copyrighted work than fair use permits. Do not let original phrasing slip in unmarked. Do not summarize so thoroughly that the reader has no remaining reason to consume the original. Do not republish under our banner what belongs to another publisher.

This is the floor. It is not optional.

### Part 2 — Generous: Send traffic, give credit, build relationship

When we engage another author's work, we are receiving a benefit from them — their thinking has informed ours, their phrasing has prompted ours, their conversation has occasioned ours. The right response is to *give back*. Specifically:

- **Name the author.** Not "a recent podcast" — "Marc Halawa's interview with President Michael Stanley on PragerU."
- **Link the source.** Always. Every time. Even if it interrupts the prose.
- **Tell the reader why it's worth their time.** A brief commendation costs nothing and dignifies the source.
- **Frame our engagement as *with* the source, not *against* it** — even when we disagree. We are joining a conversation, not capturing territory.

The defensive posture protects us legally. The generous posture protects our witness. A Christian commentary that quietly extracts material from secular and other-faith creators without sending readers back to those creators is not behaving as a good neighbor — and the watching world notices.

## 2. Working rules

### Rule 1 — Public domain is freely available

Quote freely from:

- **The King James Bible** (and any other pre-1928 translation in the U.S. public domain)
- **Pre-1928 published works** in the U.S. (the Founders, the Reformers, the Church Fathers in older translations, classical texts, etc.)
- **U.S. government publications** (founding documents, congressional records, Supreme Court opinions)
- **Anything explicitly placed in the public domain or under Creative Commons licenses that permit the use** — verify the license, then quote according to its terms

Attribution is still required, but length is not constrained.

### Rule 2 — Living authors and contemporary media: paraphrase by default

For any copyrighted contemporary source (books, articles, lectures, podcasts, interviews, YouTube videos, social media posts, news reporting, scholarly papers since 1928):

- **Paraphrase as the default.** State the author's claim in your own words, attributed to them by name.
- **One direct quote per source, maximum.** After you have used one verbatim quote from a given source, that source is closed for further direct quotation in the same piece.
- **Keep the one quote under fifteen words.** Cut to the load-bearing phrase. If the specific phrasing is the evidence (e.g., a CEO's exact words during testimony, a thinker's signature formulation), the quote is justified. If you could say it as well in your own words, you should.
- **Never string together multiple short quotes from the same source** to evade the one-quote rule. Two seven-word quotes from the same article still count as two quotes, which is one too many.
- **Do not reconstruct the source's structure.** Paraphrasing the article paragraph by paragraph, even in your own words, can constitute infringement if the reader receives the substance of the original through your summary. Engage *selectively* — pick the points that serve your argument, leave the rest for the reader to discover.

### Rule 3 — Songs, poems, haikus, prayers, and short complete works

Never reproduce these in whole or in significant part, regardless of length. Even one stanza of a copyrighted hymn, one verse of a contemporary poem, or one couplet of a copyrighted song lyric is too much. Discuss the work, name its themes, explain its significance — but do not reproduce it. Send readers to the original.

### Rule 4 — Images, video, audio

We do not embed copyrighted images, video clips, or audio in Renaissance Ministries content unless we hold the rights or the use is clearly licensed (e.g., embed codes from the platform of origin, fair-use thumbnails for review purposes). When in doubt, link rather than embed.

### Rule 5 — Transcripts and lecture material

Transcripts of lectures, interviews, sermons, and podcasts are copyrighted contemporary content even when freely posted online. Treat them under Rule 2. The fact that a video is on YouTube does not make its transcript public domain. The fact that someone has uploaded a transcript to a forum does not make it usable.

### Rule 6 — Our own prior work

Quote freely from previously published Renaissance Ministries content (fellowship essays, sermons, the Theological Grammar, the Conscious Point Physics papers, prior newsletters). Always attribute the source ("In the April 18 essay on the Kingdom of God…") and link it. This is good for SEO, builds the corpus's internal coherence, and helps readers find the broader argument.

### Rule 7 — When in doubt, paraphrase and link

You will not get sued for paraphrasing too much. You can get sued for quoting too much. When the question is "should I quote this directly or paraphrase?", default to paraphrase. The link does the work the quote would have done — and it sends the reader to the source.

## 3. Attribution and traffic discipline

This is the generous half of the posture. Every engagement with another creator's work should leave the reader knowing:

1. **Who** the original creator is (by name)
2. **Where** the original work can be found (a link or citation that resolves)
3. **What kind** of work it is (interview, lecture, book, article, podcast)
4. **Why** it's worth engaging (a brief commendation or characterization, even when we disagree)

### Standard inline attribution

For media engaged in a fellowship essay or newsletter piece, the first reference to the source should establish all four elements. Subsequent references can be shorter.

**Example (good):**

> PragerU has just released a long, friendly conversation between Marc Halawa and Michael Stanley, the LDS stake president of Santa Clarita. [Link]

This names the platform (PragerU), the host (Halawa), the guest and his role (Stanley, stake president), the genre (conversation/interview), and the recency (just released). The link does the rest. A reader who wants to verify our characterization can do so in one click. A reader who decides our engagement is interesting can watch the interview themselves — and PragerU gets the view.

**Example (insufficient):**

> A recent Mormon interview made some interesting claims…

This is the kind of attribution to avoid. It conceals more than it reveals. It denies the reader the ability to check our work. And it withholds the traffic the original creator earned by doing the work in the first place.

### Citations at the end of the piece

For longer essays, a "Sources" or "References" section at the end is good practice. List:

- **Books** by author, title, publisher, year, and (where applicable) a link to the publisher or a reputable bookseller.
- **Articles** by author, title, publication, date, and direct URL.
- **Lectures, interviews, podcasts, videos** by speaker(s), host, platform, date, and direct URL.
- **Internal Renaissance Ministries references** by title, date, and link to the post on renaissance-ministries.com.

This serves the reader, dignifies the sources, and creates a trail that the Christos AI training corpus can later use for verification and cross-reference.

### Sending traffic to interlocutors in the fellowship

When a fellowship essay is responding to or engaging a member of the fellowship — Michael, Susan, Charlie, Leonard, Armond, Isak, Margo, John, or others — name them, characterize their position fairly, and (where they have public work of their own) link to it. Michael Sherman's NowAll.us, John Howard's writings, members' own published work — these are all worth surfacing for readers, with permission from the member.

This is iron sharpening iron, made visible.

### A note on adversarial engagement

We sometimes engage sources we strongly disagree with — Islamist preachers, secular relativists, prosperity-gospel teachers, false prophets, ideological opponents. The attribution-and-traffic discipline still applies, but with judgment:

- **Name them and characterize their position fairly** (steelman before you strike — see `authors_voice.md` Move 3).
- **Link to primary sources** so readers can verify our characterization.
- **Do not link to platforms whose primary purpose is recruiting to evil** (jihadist propaganda outlets, child-exploitation forums, terror-cell websites) — describe and cite without sending traffic.
- **Distinguish "this is what they say" from "this is where to go to be discipled by them"** — readers verifying are welcome; readers being recruited are not.

The default is link. The exception is when the link itself becomes a vector for harm.

## 4. Audit checklist

Before shipping any piece for Thomas's review, run the draft against these:

1. **Have I quoted any source verbatim more than once?** If yes, paraphrase the secondary instances.
2. **Is any single direct quote longer than fifteen words?** If yes, cut to the load-bearing phrase or paraphrase.
3. **Have I named every source by author and platform?** Generic references ("a recent article," "someone said") are not acceptable.
4. **Have I linked every source that has a public URL?** Inline or in a closing references section, but somewhere.
5. **Have I reconstructed the structure of any source?** If my essay walks through someone else's argument paragraph by paragraph in my own words, it may be infringing even without verbatim quotes. Engage selectively.
6. **Have I given the reader enough to find the source themselves?** A reader who wants to verify or go deeper should be able to in one click.
7. **Have I sent traffic where traffic is due?** Even when I disagree with the source, the original creator did the work; the reader benefits from being able to consume it.
8. **Have I quoted Scripture in KJV?** (For Renaissance Ministries content, this is the default — see `authors_voice.md` §1.)
9. **Are my own prior Renaissance Ministries pieces linked when referenced?** Internal coherence matters.
10. **Have I included a "Sources" or "References" section at the end of any longer piece?** For essays of substantial length, this is best practice.

## 5. Things this discipline is NOT

Watch for these failure modes:

- **Excessive paraphrase that becomes summary-displacement.** Even paraphrased, a comprehensive walk-through of someone else's argument can constitute infringement and certainly displaces the original. Engage selectively, not exhaustively.
- **"Inspired by" without attribution.** If a lecture or article *prompted* the essay, name it — even if no direct quotation appears. The reader benefits from knowing the conversation we are joining.
- **Citation-free aphorisms from contemporary thinkers.** "As someone once said…" is not citation. If we do not know the source, we do not use the line. If we do know the source, we name and link it.
- **Defensive crouch.** Refusing to engage another creator's work because of copyright fear is the opposite failure from infringement. We are commentators; commentary requires engagement; engagement is protected. Paraphrase, attribute, link, and proceed with confidence.
- **Performative humility.** Constantly hedging ("I haven't read the whole book, but…") is not the same as careful citation. Cite what you have engaged. Let the reader judge whether the engagement is sufficient.

## 6. Why this matters for the Christos AI corpus

Renaissance Ministries content is being assembled, in part, as a training corpus for the Christos AI — a multi-module system that will produce content under the Renaissance Ministries banner long after any individual session ends.

If the source corpus contains copyright infringements, the AI will produce more of them. If the source corpus contains generous, well-attributed, traffic-sending engagements with other creators' work, the AI will produce more of those.

The discipline you build into a fellowship essay in April 2026 propagates into thousands of pieces of AI-generated content over the coming years. The corpus is a multiplier — for good or for ill.

This is one of the rare cases where doing the right thing in the small case is *also* doing the strategically essential thing in the large case. We do not get a second chance to clean up a corrupted corpus once it has been trained.

Get it right at the source.
