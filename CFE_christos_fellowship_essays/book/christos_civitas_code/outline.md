# The Christos Civitas Code — Detailed Outline

**Working subtitle:** *How to Hold Firm Convictions and Love Captured Friends in a Fractured Cultural Moment*

**Target length:** 50,000–65,000 words across eight chapters

**Audience:** Conservative Christians who are tired of being told their convictions are themselves the problem but who are also embarrassed by the contempt-culture that has come to characterize too much conservative Christian discourse, and who are looking for a third way. Secondary audience: thoughtful natural-law conservatives reaching for similar conclusions through general revelation; younger believers trying to live faithfully in a culture that treats orthodox Christianity as morally beyond the pale; pastors and fellowship leaders needing a framework for guiding congregations through difficult conversations.

## Introduction: The Question Behind the Grief

**Word target:** 3,000–4,000

The Introduction frames the question the book answers. It opens with Rick Kirschner's grief — the loss of generosity of spirit in American discourse, friendships thinning, conversations becoming brittle, nuance disappearing — and acknowledges that the diagnosis is sound on every side. It then names what the procedural cures (be thoughtful, be fair, be curious in disagreement) leave implicit: *what do you do when you are convinced your friend is genuinely wrong about something that genuinely matters?* Christianity does not allow indefinite epistemic equipoise on the great moral questions. Some disagreements are matters of taste; some are claims about reality where one position is true and the other mistaken. The book is for those who hold convictions firmly through scriptural or natural-law warrant and who are looking for a framework that does not require either softening the conviction or hardening into contempt.

The Introduction names the four-component framework that the book develops:
1. Hold the conviction firmly.
2. Hate the evil; love the captured person.
3. Listen first.
4. Distinguish certainties, strong inferences, and prudential judgments.

It closes with a brief description of the three-part structure (Ground, Code, Witness) and what each part contributes.

## Part One: The Ground

The three foundational chapters that establish the substantive convictions, the ecclesiology, and the theological framework on which the four-component code rests.

### Chapter 1: What We Confess

**Word target:** 5,500–7,000

Lays out the substantive convictions of the Christos Civitas. Unapologetic. Refuses the contemporary etiquette that treats firm conviction as itself impolite.

**Sections:**
- The lordship of Jesus Christ and the Trinitarian God
- The full deity and full humanity of Christ; the bodily resurrection
- The authority of scripture and the closed apostolic deposit
- The reality of sin and the necessity of grace
- The personhood of the unborn child from conception
- The created order of marriage as the union of one man and one woman
- The moral seriousness of work, property, and charity to the poor
- The constitutional ordering of liberty under law
- The reality of moral truth that exists independent of consensus
- *Why the etiquette of performed-uncertainty is not Christian*

Closes with the assertion that the rest of the book presupposes these convictions; readers who do not share them will find the book of intellectual interest but not of practical guidance, and that is appropriate.

### Chapter 2: The Foundation Christ Laid

**Word target:** 5,500–7,000

The Matthew 16:18 ecclesiology. Develops the *Upon This Rock* essay material into a full chapter establishing why the foundation Christ laid has not been moved, why the church Christ promised has not been defeated, and why no claim of new restoration outside the apostolic deposit can be authorized.

**Sections:**
- *Upon this rock I will build my church* — what the rock is, what the building is
- Three readings of Matthew 16:18 considered: Petrine, confessional, Christological
- The Augustine *Retractations* shift and what it shows
- 1 Corinthians 3:11, Ephesians 2:20, 1 Peter 2:6 — the apostolic-deposit witness
- Matthew 28:20 — *I am with you alway, even unto the end of the world*
- The closing of the canon: Jude 3 *the faith which was once delivered*
- Why Mormonism, Snufferism, and every restoration claim fails this test
- Why the historic creedal tradition is the church's faithful articulation of the foundation, not its corruption

Brief application: this is why the book's confidence in the apostolic deposit is not arbitrary — the foundation has been here all along.

### Chapter 3: Why the Cross Is Morally Coherent

**Word target:** 6,000–7,500

The phenomenology-of-the-Cross framework. The deepest theological chapter and the load-bearing one for what follows. Develops the April 25 standalone essay into a full chapter articulating why penal substitution is morally coherent, why God's nature stands in the relation it does to human sin, and why the experiential structure of God's indwelling has direct consequences for the relational ethic.

**Sections:**
- The standard objection to penal substitution: how can the punishment of an innocent satisfy justice?
- The categorical insufficiency of moral-influence and ransom theories alone
- God's experiential indwelling: omniscience as inside-knowing rather than distant observation
- The recoil of God's nature against sin as experiential horror, not abstract displeasure
- Why only Christ could survive absorbing the recoil
- Susan's adjacent framing: the Father withdrawing the life-source from the Son
- The two framings as complementary descriptions of the same event from different vantage points
- Why this matters for relational ethics: the captured friend is the one through whom God bears the captivity from inside
- The Luke 15 prodigal-son parable read as the structure of God's ongoing experience
- Therefore correction of the captured is participation in God's labor of rescue, not optional kindness

This chapter is the book's most original theological contribution and would benefit from particular care in editing. The argument is tight; it should be allowed to breathe without being overstated.

## Part Two: The Code

The four-component framework, one component per chapter. The substantive heart of the book.

### Chapter 4: Component One — Holding the Conviction Firmly

**Word target:** 5,500–7,000

Why the convictions of Part One are not opinions to be performed with appropriate uncertainty but stand-line in real spiritual warfare.

**Sections:**
- *We wrestle not against flesh and blood* (Ephesians 6:12) — the spiritual-warfare frame
- Why demythologization of this language disarms the believer
- The contemporary cultural pressure to perform agnosticism about settled questions
- *Earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered* (Jude 3)
- *Be ready always to give an answer* (1 Peter 3:15)
- The difference between firmness and rigidity (forward reference to Chapter 7)
- The difference between conviction and contempt (forward reference to Chapter 5)
- Holding the line as soldier of Christ — the posture of the Reformers, Athanasius, the early martyrs
- Practical application: what holding-firm looks like in a contemporary conversation

### Chapter 5: Component Two — Hating the Evil, Loving the Captured

**Word target:** 7,500–9,000

The substantive heart of the book. The longest chapter, because the most needs to be said. Develops the proper-direction-of-hatred framework from the *Hating Evil, Loving the Captured* essay into full book treatment.

**Sections:**
- The Christian moral universe contains legitimate objects of hatred
  - Psalm 97:10 *hate evil*
  - Psalm 139:21-22 *I hate them with perfect hatred*
  - Romans 12:9 *abhor that which is evil*
  - Psalm 5:5-6, 119:104, and the broader scriptural witness
- The progressive flattening: all hatred treated as illegitimate
- The reactionary flattening: all hatred treated as legitimate when aimed at perceived enemies
- Both flattenings as theologically illiterate; the discipline is direction
- The proper objects of hatred: principalities, powers, false ways, seducing spirits, doctrines of devils
- The proper objects of love: human beings made in the image of God, including the captured
- Why image-bearer status is non-negotiable and forecloses contempt for any human being
- The phenomenology connection (back to Chapter 3): the captured friend as the one through whom God bears the captivity
- *Hate the sin, love the sinner* as a serious theological proposition rather than a sentimental cliché
- Worked examples:
  - The friend captured by ideological cult (left or right)
  - The family member captured by destructive sexual pattern
  - The colleague captured by spiritual deception about ultimate reality
  - The cultural moment as a captivity by spiritual powers operating through institutional channels
- Closing: the discipline as soldier in real war loving the captured, hating the captor

### Chapter 6: Component Three — Listening as Theological Discipline

**Word target:** 5,500–7,000

The two warrants for listening (listening pleases God to be known; listening is required for spirit-discernment) and the four diagnostic possibilities (affirm, introduce, redirect, resist). Substantial new material beyond what the essays contain — extensive worked conversational case studies.

**Sections:**
- Listening as more than pastoral courtesy
- First warrant: the structure of biblical revelation as God making Himself known, and the pattern as the pattern we owe our neighbor
- *Be swift to hear, slow to speak* (James 1:19) as reflection of God's posture
- *Inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least of these* (Matthew 25:40) — listening as something we do to Christ
- Second warrant: *try the spirits whether they are of God* (1 John 4:1) requires listening
- The four diagnostic possibilities developed:
  - *Affirm* — when the spirit is in agreement with the Holy Spirit; recognizing the Spirit's voice through unexpected mouths
  - *Introduce* — when the spirit is absent and the Holy Spirit is to be introduced; the missionary task done correctly
  - *Redirect* — when the spirit is present but inappropriate; real concern mis-channeled and to be re-channeled
  - *Resist* — when the spirit is opposed to the Holy Spirit; refusing to host the spirit without attacking the person
- The discipline of *swift to hear, slow to speak* in practice
- Worked conversational case studies: how to recognize each diagnostic situation in actual conversation

### Chapter 7: Component Four — Distinguishing Certainties, Strong Inferences, and Prudential Judgments

**Word target:** 6,500–8,000

The taxonomy that frees the believer from rigidity without dissolving into softness. Probably the chapter most useful to conservative Christian readers and most needing extensive worked examples.

**Sections:**
- The discipline of keeping epistemic categories straight
- Scriptural certainties — what they are, why they are non-negotiable
  - Examples developed at length
- Strong scriptural inferences — what they are, the appropriate posture
  - Examples developed at length
- Prudential judgments — what they are, the appropriate humility
  - Extensive examples: tax policy, immigration, schooling, foreign policy, environmental stewardship, specific cultural-moment tactics
- The cowardice of the soft Christian (treating certainties as prudential)
- The rigidity of the hard Christian (treating prudential as certainties)
- The contempt of disagreement-on-prudence as evidence of moral failure
- How to know which category a question falls into: a working procedure
- Worked examples of the procedure applied
- Why this discipline is what makes Christian unity across prudential disagreement possible
