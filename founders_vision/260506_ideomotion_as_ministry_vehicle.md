# Ideomotion as a Ministry Vehicle: Captive Audience, the Lame Walking, and the Companion-God Deliverable of Prayer

**Date:** May 6, 2026
**Primary contributor:** Thomas Lee Abshier, ND
**Other named contributors:** Charlie Gutierrez (business partner, Ideomotion); Susan (fellowship member, attributed for the prayer insight)
**Source:** Phone call between Thomas and Charlie, Otter.ai-transcribed
**Topic summary:** The case for Ideomotion as an unapologetically Christ-focused ministry-business under Renaissance Ministries, holding three convictions in tension: (1) the customer relationship is a captive-audience opportunity for the gospel that should not be soft-pedaled; (2) the deliverable of prayer most reliably claimed is companion-God, the relationship itself, per Susan's insight; (3) genuine miraculous healing remains a live possibility, attested by Jan's recovery under Charlie's rehab work and by the contemporary testimonies catalogued in Rutz's *Mega Shift*.

---

## The seed

### On Ideomotion as a ministry vehicle

> We just need to have a good, ministry-focused business.

Ideomotion's mobility devices fit a specific scriptural slot. Jesus said the lame would walk; that promise was at the heart of his earthly ministry, and it is at the heart of Renaissance Ministries' work too. An Ideomotion advertisement could fairly read: *Jesus said the lame walk. This is our effort to fulfill that scripture. His heart was for the lame; that is the heart of Renaissance Ministries.* The corporate-ministry expression of this conviction is that Ideomotion should be a wholly-owned subsidiary of Renaissance Ministries (508(c)(1)(a)), incorporated where state income tax does not erode the mission.

### On not soft-pedaling the gospel

> I want to beat 'em over the head.

(This is in deliberate contrast to Charlie's preference, also recorded in this seed below, for "modest but adequate.")

You have a captive audience. People want your products. If they are going to deal with our company, they are going to hear the gospel; they are going to hear about fellowship groups; they are going to hear that God is everything; they are going to be invited into a like-minded community of believers in mobility-device users. We can offer to connect them to the ministry's broader work and to other Walker users who are part of it.

You do not apologize for or soft-pedal the fact that you serve the King of the universe — and that the customer should too. That is the only allegiance worth having. There should be no apology whatsoever. *That is what this entire ministry is about. It is in your face.* It has to be, because nobody is paying attention. The cultural pattern is now: "Yeah, but I go to Canvas church, and that's the canvas of our life." Well, what is that with Christ? "Christ moves the brush of your hand." Powerful. Transformative. *Not.* Soft-pedaling has been tried; it is not being heard.

### Charlie's counter-position, recorded in good faith

> We don't want to beat people over the head with our spirituality, but there are some things we could do that are, I think, modest but adequate.

This is a real disagreement, not a stylistic difference. The right resolution is not for one of us to overrule the other but for the fellowship to help discern the calibration. The disagreement is named here so it is not lost.

### On the deliverable of prayer (Susan's insight, attributed)

In my naturopathic practice and in the lives of the people around me, I have observed that prayer is not generally answered as the petitioner expects. People pray for healing and die. Families pray for reconciliation and divorce. There are exceptions — and they matter — but the exception is not the rule. So the question presses: what is the actual deliverable of prayer that we can claim as Christians?

The thing I fell back on, in last Sunday's fellowship, was something Susan said. When we pray and give our hearts to God and confess our belief, what we are guaranteed is that *we have a companion*. That is the deliverable of prayer most consistently available to us. The presence of God with the praying soul, the relationship itself, is the gift. Whether more is given on top of that — physical healing, restored relationships, miraculous intervention — is in God's hand and is not the basis on which the prayer is ventured.

### On the miraculous, in tension with the above

The companion-God deliverable is the floor, not the ceiling. Charlie's video of Jan walking — after the doctor's pronouncement that she would never walk — is real. The doctor saw the video and ordered home physical therapy. That is testimony.

Charlie introduced James Rutz's *Mega Shift* into the conversation. The book catalogues contemporary miraculous testimonies from the persecuted church: a preacher led by the Spirit through the dark to a tree in a remote field, where he preached for hours until a man with a noose climbed down out of the tree to be saved; a Chinese minister whose infant fell from a fourth-floor balcony, whose head split open, who lay dead for days while the father labored at unifying four divided Chinese churches, whose skull and brain knit back together over the same days during which the churches were reunified, who was eventually fully restored.

We do not have this in America. Charlie said it plainly: "We don't have that." We are insulated against it; we are immune to it. Why? My working hypothesis from the call: *intensity, focus, ongoing dedication, full belief.* The persecuted church has all of these because the world strips away alternatives. The American church, comfortable, has none of them by default.

This is not a doctrine. It is an observation that the seed-archive should hold open for the fellowship to discern.

### On bodies and hearts

> We want people's hearts to be changed in addition to their bodies being healed.

Ideomotion's ground commitment is that the device must work — Jan's testimony is the standard. Above that, the customer relationship is an evangelistic relationship: the body is helped; the heart is invited to be changed. Not one or the other. Both, in that order.

## Elaboration hooks (open questions for the fellowship)

1. **What is the right calibration of evangelistic boldness in a ministry-business context?** Thomas's "in your face" against Charlie's "modest but adequate." The fellowship should weigh in.

2. **What is the actual mechanism of customer-to-fellowship discipleship?** The principle ("captive audience") is clear; the operational pipeline is not yet.

3. **What is the relationship between the companion-God deliverable and the miraculous?** Both are real. Both are claimed in scripture. American Christianity tends to claim only the first (companion as consolation) or only the second (miraculous as transactional). Holding both honestly is the harder and the more biblical posture. *Mega Shift*, when read, should sharpen this.

4. **Does the persecuted-church/American-church asymmetry imply that comfort is the obstacle?** If so, what does that mean for an American ministry-business that, by design, increases its customers' physical comfort? This is not a refutation of the work; it is a question the work has to be ready to answer.
