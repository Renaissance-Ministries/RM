# Chapter 2: The Foundation Christ Laid

The convictions of Chapter 1 rest on something. The Christian who confesses what we confess does not invent the content of the confession; he receives it. The question the present chapter takes up is *what is it that he receives, from where, and on what authority?* The answer, in shortest form, is that what we receive is the apostolic deposit, from the apostles whom Christ commissioned, on the authority of Christ Himself who promised that what they delivered would not be defeated. The foundation has been laid. It has not been moved. The church Christ promised has endured, and the deposit they received has been preserved through every generation since Pentecost.

This chapter is the ecclesiology that makes Chapter 1's confidence non-arbitrary. Without it, the convictions of Chapter 1 would be just our own preferred set of beliefs. With it, they are recognition of what the creator-God has actually given through the apostles He sent and the church He promised to build.

## Upon this rock

The text on which the chapter pivots is Matthew 16:13-19. Jesus is at Caesarea Philippi, near the headwaters of the Jordan and within sight of a massive limestone cliff containing pagan shrines to Pan and to the deified Augustus. He asks the disciples who men say He is, then who they say He is. Peter answers, *Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God.* Jesus blesses Peter and says: *Upon this rock I will build my church; and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it.*

What is the rock?

Three readings are available, and the choice among them has consequences for the whole structure of Christian thought.

### The Petrine reading

The first reading takes Peter himself as the rock. *Thou art Peter (Petros), and upon this rock (petra) I will build my church.* On this reading, Christ is establishing Peter as the foundational figure of the church, with Peter's office continuing through apostolic succession to the bishops of Rome. This is the Catholic reading, and it grounds the papal claim to universal jurisdiction over the church.

The reading has surface appeal — the wordplay on *Petros / petra* is real, and Peter's prominence in the early chapters of Acts is genuine — but it has serious problems. First, the Greek distinction between *Petros* (a stone, masculine) and *petra* (a rock, bedrock, feminine) suggests intentional distinction rather than identification. Second, Christ Himself in the same chapter rebukes Peter as Satan (Matthew 16:23), which sits awkwardly with the claim that Peter is the foundation of the church. Third, Paul writing to the Galatians describes confronting Peter to his face when Peter was wrong about the application of the gospel to Gentiles (Galatians 2:11-14) — not the posture of one who regards Peter as infallible foundational authority. Fourth, the New Testament elsewhere identifies the foundation differently: *other foundation can no man lay than that is laid, which is Jesus Christ* (1 Corinthians 3:11); *built upon the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Jesus Christ himself being the chief corner stone* (Ephesians 2:20).

The Petrine reading does not survive these tests. Peter is foundational in the sense that all the apostles are foundational — they are the stones laid on the cornerstone — but Peter is not the rock. The rock is something else.

### The confessional reading

The second reading takes Peter's confession as the rock. *Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God* — the confession itself, made in the Spirit (Matthew 16:17), is what the church is built on. On this reading, Christ is saying that any believer who makes Peter's confession is built into the church Christ is building.

This reading was developed strongly in the Reformation and persists in much Protestant thought. It has the merit of avoiding the difficulties of the Petrine reading and of generalizing the rock from a single apostle to every believing Christian. It also fits naturally with the Pauline teaching that the church is the body of Christ composed of all who confess Him as Lord (1 Corinthians 12:12-13, Ephesians 4:4-6).

But it has a difficulty of its own. A confession is not a foundation in the load-bearing sense the metaphor requires. The confession identifies the foundation; it does not constitute the foundation. To say *the rock is the confession* is to make the structure depend on the act of confessing rather than on what is confessed. The confession can fail, falter, be lost; but the foundation, by Christ's promise, cannot. The confessional reading therefore points in the right direction but stops one step short of the actual rock.

### The Christological reading

The third reading takes Christ Himself as the rock, identified by Peter's confession. The confession identifies the foundation; the foundation is the Christ identified by the confession. *Thou art the Christ* is what Peter says; the rock is the Christ who *is*.

This reading is what Augustine settled on in his *Retractations* near the end of his life, after defending earlier readings; what Calvin maintained throughout his commentaries; what the bulk of Protestant exegesis has held; and what makes the most sense of the broader New Testament witness. *Other foundation can no man lay than that is laid, which is Jesus Christ* (1 Corinthians 3:11) — the foundation is Christ. *Behold, I lay in Sion a chief corner stone, elect, precious: and he that believeth on him shall not be confounded* (1 Peter 2:6, where Peter himself names Christ as the cornerstone). *Built upon the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Jesus Christ himself being the chief corner stone* (Ephesians 2:20).

The Christological reading harmonizes the whole New Testament witness. The foundation is Christ. The apostles are the stones laid on the cornerstone. The believers in every generation are built into the temple as living stones (1 Peter 2:5). Peter's confession is the door through which any believer enters; the confession identifies the foundation that has been there all along.

## Why this matters

This is not antiquarian exegesis. The choice among the three readings has direct consequences for what kinds of Christian claims are possible.

If the rock is Peter, then continuing apostolic succession to a particular human office is the structure of the church, and breaking communion with that office breaks one from the foundation. This is the Catholic position. It has the virtue of making the structure visible but the burden of placing the church's continuity on a human institution that history has shown to be capable of substantial corruption.

If the rock is Peter's confession, then any community making the confession is built on the foundation, but no human institution mediates access to it. The structure becomes radically distributed; every believing Christian is built into the foundation directly. This is the Protestant position in its most generic form, and it tends toward the multiplication of independent congregations claiming independent authority.

If the rock is Christ Himself, identified by Peter's confession, then the foundation is fixed, the access to the foundation is the confession, and the church is the body of believers who have made the confession across every generation since Pentecost. This is the position that this book confesses. It avoids the institutional fragility of the Petrine reading and the structural dispersal of the bare confessional reading by locating the foundation in Christ Himself, with the confession functioning as the door through which believers in every generation enter what Christ has been building.

## The gates of hell shall not prevail

The next clause is the one that forecloses every restoration claim that has emerged in Christian history.

*The gates of hell shall not prevail against it.* The church Christ is building cannot be defeated. It cannot be lost. It cannot be apostatized into oblivion. Whatever spiritual or institutional corruptions assault it, the foundation cannot be moved. *The gates of hell* is a Hebraism for the realm of death, the fullest concentration of opposition to God; and even this fullest opposition cannot prevail against the church Christ is building.

This promise sits alongside Matthew 28:20 (*lo, I am with you alway, even unto the end of the world*) and Hebrews 13:8 (*Jesus Christ, the same yesterday, and to day, and for ever*). The pattern is consistent: Christ has not abandoned His church, has not allowed the deposit He gave to be lost, has been with His people in every generation, and will be with them until the end of the age. The church is not on its own; the Christ who founded it sustains it.

What this forecloses is the entire family of restoration claims that have emerged across the centuries. The standard form of a restoration claim is: *the original church was lost; the deposit was corrupted; we are restoring what was lost through new revelation.* This is the claim of Joseph Smith, of Mary Baker Eddy, of Ellen White, of Sun Myung Moon, of Denver Snuffer in our present moment, and of countless smaller figures across two millennia. Each of them claims that something essential to the apostolic Christianity was lost and that they have been authorized to restore it.

But Christ's promise rules this out. The church cannot have been lost, because the gates of hell would have prevailed. The deposit cannot have been substantially corrupted, because the foundation has not been moved. There is no need for restoration, because the original is still here. The faithful witness has persisted in every generation, in every nation, regardless of which institutions were faithful and which became corrupt — because the foundation is Christ Himself, and the access to the foundation is the confession, and the Spirit who indwells every believer keeps the deposit alive.

This is not abstract. It is the historical record. From Pentecost to the present, in every generation, in every nation Christianity has reached, there have been believers confessing Christ as Peter confessed Him, indwelt by the Spirit Christ promised, holding the apostolic deposit faithfully even when the institutional structures around them were corrupt. The Roman emperors could not stamp it out. The pre-Reformation Catholic corruption could not stamp it out. The Reformation's internal schisms did not stamp it out. The Enlightenment's intellectual assault did not stamp it out. The twentieth century's communist regimes did not stamp it out. The contemporary cultural revolution will not stamp it out. The faithful witness persists, because Christ promised it would persist, and Christ keeps His promises.

## The closed apostolic deposit

If the church has not been lost, the deposit has not been corrupted, and the faithful witness has persisted in every generation, then the apostolic deposit is sufficient for the church's life. We do not need new revelations to supplement it. The Spirit continues to speak to the church and to individual believers, leading them deeper into the truth already deposited, but the deposit itself is fixed.

Jude makes this explicit: *I beseech you that ye should earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints* (Jude 3). The Greek word translated *once* is *hapax*, meaning *once for all*, decisively, with no anticipation of further deliveries. The faith was delivered to the saints once. Their job, and ours, is to contend for it — to defend it, transmit it, live it — not to await its supplementation.

Paul makes the same point in Galatians 1:8-9: *though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed.* The pastoral force of *though we, or an angel* is striking. Paul is anticipating that someone may come, with apparent authority, claiming to deliver a new gospel — and he forecloses the claim before it can be made. Even if Paul himself were to come and preach a different gospel, the different gospel would be accursed. Even if an angel descended from heaven with new revelations, the revelations would be accursed if they contradicted what the apostles had already delivered.

This is the standard against which every claim of new revelation must be tested. Joseph Smith's gold-plates revelation, Mary Baker Eddy's *Science and Health*, Ellen White's prophetic writings, Sun Myung Moon's *Divine Principle*, Denver Snuffer's *Restoration Edition* scriptures — each of them claims to deliver something new. Each of them contradicts the apostolic deposit at structural points. Each of them, by Galatians 1:8, is to be rejected.

This is not contempt for the people who have made these claims. Many of them were and are sincere. Some of them may have been deceived rather than deceiving. The relational-ethics question of how to engage friends who have entered restoration frameworks is the subject of Chapter 5. The doctrinal question is settled here: the apostolic deposit is closed, and any claim of new scripture is, by the deposit's own standard, foreclosed.

## The historic creedal tradition

What about the creeds? If the apostolic deposit is closed, are the post-apostolic creeds — Apostles', Nicene, Athanasian, Chalcedonian — additions to the deposit? Or are they faithful articulations of what the deposit itself contains?

The latter. The creeds are the church's faithful articulations of what scripture itself confesses, produced when scripture's witness was tested against teachings (Arianism, Modalism, Nestorianism, Eutychianism, Pelagianism) that genuinely contradicted it. The creeds did not add new content; they preserved the deposit's actual content against attempts to dilute, modalize, or rationalize it away.

The Nicene Creed, in particular, is the church's confession that the Father and the Son are *of one substance* (homoousios, against the Arian *of similar substance*, homoiousios), one God in Trinitarian distinction. This is what scripture says when scripture is allowed to speak fully — *I and my Father are one* (John 10:30), *the Word was God* (John 1:1), *He that hath seen me hath seen the Father* (John 14:9), and the Trinitarian baptismal formula of Matthew 28:19. The Nicene articulation is not novel philosophy; it is the church confessing what scripture itself confesses against teachings that selectively read scripture to deny it.

Restorationist movements that reject the historic creeds typically do so by reading them as post-apostolic Hellenistic accretions on a simpler biblical faith. This is historically inaccurate. The pre-Nicene fathers — Ignatius, Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Tertullian, Origen — testify abundantly to the substance of what Nicaea formalized. The Trinitarian confession is older than Nicaea; Nicaea simply made the confession explicit because the Arian challenge required explicit articulation.

To reject the creedal tradition wholesale is therefore to reject the church's faithful articulation of scripture itself. The Christian who confesses what we confess in Chapter 1 finds that the historic creeds are saying the same thing he is saying, in language that has been tested for sixteen centuries against every available alternative. They are received as faithful, not as supplemental.

## What this means for the book that follows

This chapter has done foundational work. The confidence with which Chapter 1 names the convictions is not arbitrary. It rests on:

1. The foundation Christ laid is Christ Himself, identified by Peter's confession
2. The gates of hell have not prevailed and will not prevail
3. The apostolic deposit is closed and sufficient
4. The faithful witness has persisted in every generation
5. The historic creedal tradition is the church's faithful articulation of the deposit

The relational-ethics framework developed in Part Two of this book presupposes this ecclesiology. The believer holding firm convictions does so because the convictions are anchored in what the creator-God has done and said through the apostles He commissioned and the church He promised to build. He is not defending opinions. He is contending for the faith once delivered.

The next chapter takes up the third foundational question: *given what we confess and what foundation it rests on, why is the Cross morally coherent?* The Cross is the load-bearing event of the Christian gospel, and the relational ethic of Part Two depends on a particular understanding of what the Cross is and is not. That is the work of Chapter 3.
