# Ideomotion Ministry Charter (Initial Draft)

**CHANGELOG**

- v0.1 (2026-05-06): Initial draft. Source seeds: phone call between Thomas Abshier and Charlie Gutierrez, transcribed by Otter.ai (May 6, 2026), captured in `founders_vision/260506_ideomotion_as_ministry_vehicle.md`. Sections, mission language, and operating principles are first-pass and explicitly subject to fellowship discernment and Charlie's input.

---

## 1. Why this document exists

Ideomotion is a mobility-device company co-founded and co-owned by Charlie Gutierrez and Thomas Abshier. Charlie has thirty-seven years of industry experience; the company's flagship products serve people whose physical mobility has been compromised by injury, illness, age, or congenital condition. The Traveler is the lead product currently approaching market.

This charter sets out the case for Ideomotion's reconstitution as a ministry — specifically, as a wholly-owned subsidiary of Renaissance Ministries (a 508(c)(1)(a) faith organization) — and articulates the mission, purpose, methods, and operating posture that a ministry-business of this kind should have.

This is an initial draft. It is the working starting point, not the settled position. The settled position will be reached after Charlie has engaged with this draft on his own terms, after fellowship members (especially Susan, Armond, and Isak) have weighed in, and after legal and tax counsel have confirmed that the corporate structure described here is in fact the right one.

---

## 2. Theological foundation: the lame shall walk

The mission of Ideomotion sits inside a specific scriptural promise that runs from Isaiah through Acts:

*And the eyes of the blind shall be opened, and the ears of the deaf shall be unstopped. Then shall the lame man leap as an hart, and the tongue of the dumb sing.* — Isaiah 35:5–6

When John the Baptist sent disciples from prison to ask whether Jesus was indeed the one promised, the answer Jesus gave was action, not assertion:

*Go and shew John again those things which ye do hear and see: The blind receive their sight, and the lame walk, the lepers are cleansed, and the deaf hear, the dead are raised up, and the poor have the gospel preached to them.* — Matthew 11:4–5

The lame walking is a sign of the Kingdom's arrival. It was the answer John needed, and it is one of the marks the church has carried, however imperfectly, ever since. When Peter and John encountered the lame man at the gate Beautiful, Peter's response was not to apologize for having no money to give but to offer what he did have:

*Then Peter said, Silver and gold have I none; but such as I have give I thee: In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth rise up and walk. And he took him by the right hand, and lifted him up.* — Acts 3:6–7

This is the slot Ideomotion fits into. Not as a substitute for what only God does, and not as a dressed-up commercial venture, but as a faithful, ordinary, well-engineered participation in a Kingdom sign. The work is ordinary: design, manufacture, fit, train, support. The frame is not.

A second scriptural anchor matters here. In the parable of the great supper, when the invited guests refuse to come, the master commands the servant: *Go out quickly into the streets and lanes of the city, and bring in hither the poor, and the maimed, and the halt, and the blind* (Luke 14:21). The lame are not an afterthought to the Kingdom feast; they are among its first invited. A company whose customers are precisely "the halt and the lame" should know whose company it is keeping in scripture.

## 3. Mission

**Ideomotion exists to help the lame walk, and to invite every person it serves into the company of the One who promised that the lame would walk.**

The two clauses of that sentence are not optional alternatives; they are a single mission, in that order. The device must work. The customer must be helped. Above that ground commitment, the relationship that begins with a mobility device is invited — never coerced, never traded — into something larger: fellowship with the King of the Universe and with His people.

A device that does not work is not a ministry; it is a failed product. A device that works but is sold without testimony is not a ministry either; it is an ordinary business. Ideomotion is to be neither. It is to be a working device, sold by people who serve Christ, accompanied by the testimony that has been entrusted to them.

---

## 4. Purpose: what Ideomotion is, and what it is not

**Ideomotion is a ministry-business** — a for-profit corporate entity organized as a wholly-owned subsidiary of a 508(c)(1)(a) faith ministry, designed so that its mission, branding, customer relationships, and use of revenue are governed by the parent ministry's purposes rather than by ordinary commercial incentives.

**Ideomotion is not a Christian-owned business** in the usual sense. The usual sense is a business whose owners are personally Christian but whose corporate identity, marketing, and customer relationships are religiously neutral. That is a legitimate way for Christians to do business; it is not what Ideomotion is being built to be.

**Ideomotion is not a charity, and it is not a free-distribution program.** Customers pay for the products. The work of design and manufacture is real engineering work and is compensated as such. The revenue, however, is at the disposal of a ministry parent, not of private owners optimizing a personal return.

**Ideomotion is not a pulpit.** The customer is not coerced into faith as a condition of purchase. The mobility device works whether the customer accepts the gospel, declines it, or never engages it at all. The dignity of the customer's choice is non-negotiable. *We invite. We do not entrap.*

**Ideomotion is, however, unapologetic.** Where the gospel is named, it is named without apology, without hedge, and without softening for marketability. A company that exists to help the lame walk in the name of the One who promised that the lame would walk does not need to be coy about whose name is being invoked.

## 5. Methods

### 5.1. Product excellence as the floor

The first commandment of a ministry-business is that the product must work. Charlie's rehabilitative work with Jan — who was told by her doctor she would never walk and who is now walking, with her doctor sufficiently persuaded by the recent video to order her home physical therapy — is the standard. Devices that fail, programs that don't deliver, and rehab that doesn't help are not redeemed by Christian branding. They are betrayals of the mission and a reproach to the name on the door.

Engineering, manufacturing, fitting, training, and ongoing support all sit under this commandment. Charlie's three-and-a-half decades of experience are the asset that makes this commandment achievable.

### 5.2. The customer relationship as discipleship gateway

The customer who comes to Ideomotion is, in principle, a captive audience — not in the manipulative sense, but in the precise sense that they have come to us because they want what we make and because what we make matters to their lives. They are, by definition, attentive. They are, by definition, in a season where mobility, agency, and dignity are at the front of their minds. They are, by definition, often suffering.

The ministry-business response to this is not to exploit the attention but to honor it. The customer relationship is structured so that the gospel is *available* — offered, named, linked to fellowship community — but never made a condition of service.

Practical mechanisms to develop:
- A clear, brief statement of mission included with every device shipped.
- An invitation to a Renaissance Ministries fellowship group, with no obligation and no follow-up pressure.
- Optional testimony content (text, video, or both) provided alongside product material.
- A "Walker Users Fellowship" or similarly named small-group offering, in which mobility-device customers can connect with one another and with RM fellowship members.
- A direct line to Thomas, or a designee, for customers who want spiritual conversation in addition to product support.

The mechanism is to be designed jointly by Charlie and Thomas and reviewed by the fellowship before adoption.

### 5.3. Testimony as primary evangelism content

The Jan video is the model. Documented before-and-after evidence of restored mobility — produced honestly, with the customer's permission, with the doctor's evaluation where available — is the most powerful evangelistic content the company can offer. *We do not have to say more than the testimony already says; we have only to make it visible and frame it truthfully.*

A pipeline for ongoing testimony production should be built into customer support — not as a marketing afterthought but as a discipleship practice. The customer whose life is changed is invited (not required) to share that change.

### 5.4. Fellowship as the destination, not the device

The device is the entry point. Fellowship is the destination. A successful Ideomotion customer relationship looks like this: the device works, the customer's mobility is restored, and the customer has been invited — and, where they accept, integrated — into a community of people who are seeking Christ together. The corporation, by itself, cannot make a disciple. The fellowship can. Ideomotion's ministry function is to deliver the device *and* to deliver the invitation.

### 5.5. Proclamation posture: the present working position

Two postures were articulated in the founding conversation. Charlie's: *modest but adequate* — proclamation is real but restrained, content with brief signals (a scripture line on a brochure, an unobtrusive ministry attribution) and trusting the work to speak for itself. Thomas's: *unapologetic and direct* — the work does not speak for itself in a culture that has stopped listening, and proclamation should be in-your-face because soft-pedaling is no longer being heard.

**Working position for v0.1 of this charter:** the unapologetic posture is the default. We name the King of the Universe. We do not soft-pedal allegiance. The customer who chooses to do business with us knows whose company they are choosing to keep.

**Provisos:** (a) the unapologetic posture coexists with the no-coercion principle (§4) — boldness in proclamation does not authorize manipulation in sales; (b) the calibration is an open question for fellowship discernment (§9); (c) Charlie's modesty position is not dismissed but recorded, weighed, and understood as a constraint that any final language must answer rather than override.

## 6. Corporate structure

The proposed structure: **Ideomotion LLC, wholly-owned subsidiary of Renaissance Ministries (508(c)(1)(a)), incorporated in a U.S. state with no state income tax** (candidates from the founding conversation: South Dakota, Nevada, Texas, Florida; additional candidates to evaluate: Wyoming, Tennessee, Washington, Alaska).

The reason for the subsidiary structure is twofold. First, it aligns the corporate identity with the ministry mission at the legal-structural level rather than only at the marketing level — a difference that matters to the IRS, to potential ministry partners, and to the company's own self-understanding over time. Second, it routes revenue, decisions, and accountability through the ministry parent rather than through private ownership, which is the structural expression of the conviction that Ideomotion's work is for the Kingdom rather than for accumulation.

The reason for the no-state-income-tax incorporation is straightforward stewardship. Every dollar of state income tax not paid is a dollar available for the mission.

**This section is provisional and depends on confirmation from competent tax and legal counsel** that (a) a 508(c)(1)(a) parent organization can in fact wholly own a for-profit LLC subsidiary in the manner described, (b) the resulting structure is compliant with both federal tax law and the chosen state's corporate law, and (c) the donor-deductibility, unrelated-business-income-tax, and fiduciary implications are understood and acceptable. Items 1–3 of the parallel action-items document (`260506_ideomotion_call_action_items.md`) are the operational handle for this section.

## 7. The doubled commitment on prayer

Renaissance Ministries holds two convictions about prayer that are sometimes separated but that Ideomotion is required to hold together:

**The companion-God deliverable** (Susan's formulation, attributed in `founders_vision/260506_ideomotion_as_ministry_vehicle.md`): when the believer prays, what is most reliably given is the companion presence of God Himself. The relationship is the gift. This is the deliverable of prayer that Christians can claim without qualification, and it is the floor under everything else.

**Openness to the miraculous**: the companion-God deliverable is the floor, not the ceiling. Genuine miraculous healing remains a live possibility, attested by Jan's recovery, attested by James Rutz's *Mega Shift* (a contemporary catalogue of testimonies from the persecuted church that Charlie introduced into the founding conversation and that Thomas will read and respond to in essay form), and attested across the witness of the church through every century of its life.

Ideomotion holds both. We design and manufacture mobility devices because the ordinary, faithful, well-engineered work is what we are given to do — and we do it as well as forty years of Charlie's experience can do it. We pray for the companion-God who walks with the suffering customer whether the customer's body is restored or not — because that companionship is what every soul most needs. And we remain open to, and rejoice in, the cases where God does the impossible thing — because He is the same yesterday, today, and forever, and the persecuted church abroad bears witness that He has not stopped.

A ministry-business that holds only the ordinary work loses its soul. A ministry-business that promises the miraculous as a transaction is a fraud. Ideomotion is to be neither.

## 8. How Ideomotion fits into Renaissance Ministries

Ideomotion is the first ministry-business in the RM ecosystem. Its relationship to the broader ministry is multi-layered:

**Theological**: Ideomotion lives out one specific Kingdom sign — the lame walking — that the broader ministry preaches. It is the embodied complement to the proclamation work done in CFE (fellowship essays), CNL (newsletter), and CHR (historical review). The fellowship discusses, the newsletter explains, the historical-review challenges culture; Ideomotion *makes the lame walk*. Each module bears its own piece of the Kingdom witness.

**Corporate**: Ideomotion as wholly-owned subsidiary places its revenue, decisions, and corporate identity under the ministry parent. This is a structural commitment, not a marketing claim.

**Customer-to-fellowship**: Ideomotion's customers are a candidate pipeline into RM fellowship community, on entirely voluntary terms. The fellowship gains; the customer gains; the ministry's reach into a population it might not otherwise touch (mobility-device users, often older or chronically ill, often outside ordinary church life) is extended.

**Testimony pipeline**: Ideomotion-produced testimony content (videos, written accounts) feeds the broader RM communication ecosystem — newsletter, fellowship discussions, the eventual Christos AI training corpus.

**Christos AI**: in the longer arc, Ideomotion's customer-relationship and testimony-production patterns become source material for the Christos AI module's training data on real-world ministry interactions. This is not the immediate motivation, but it is the long-term harvest.

**Fellowship governance**: substantive ministry decisions about Ideomotion's posture, language, branding, and pipeline are subject to fellowship discernment in the same way that other RM modules are. The corporation is operationally led by Charlie and Thomas; the *ministry posture* of the corporation is held accountable to the fellowship.

A new RM module designation should be proposed for Ideomotion and added to `MODULES.md` once this charter has been refined past v0.1. Provisional code: **IDM** (Ideomotion). Folder structure to mirror existing modules: `IDM_ideomotion_ministry/IDM_charter.md` plus subdirectories for testimony archives, branding decisions, fellowship-pipeline materials, and operating-system documentation as those mature.

## 9. Open questions for fellowship discernment

These are the questions on which the v0.1 working positions should be tested before any of them is treated as settled.

**Q1.** What is the right calibration of evangelistic boldness in the company's branding, marketing, and customer-facing language? Charlie's "modest but adequate" against Thomas's "unapologetic and in your face." The v0.1 working position is the latter; the question is whether that should hold after fellowship review.

**Q2.** What is the operational mechanism of customer-to-fellowship discipleship? §5.2 lists candidate mechanisms; none has been designed in detail.

**Q3.** Does the wholly-owned-subsidiary structure work as described under current 508(c)(1)(a) law, federal tax law, and the chosen state's corporate law? *This is a question for legal/CPA counsel, not for fellowship discernment.* Items 1–3 of the action-items document hold this thread.

**Q4.** Does the persecuted-church / American-church asymmetry on the miraculous (§7) imply that material comfort is itself an obstacle to the kind of faith Rutz catalogues? If so, what does that mean for a ministry-business whose stock-in-trade is increasing customers' physical comfort? This is a real question, not a refutation; the work has to be ready to answer it.

**Q5.** What is the fellowship's view on the language of "captive audience" in §5.2? The phrase is honest and useful in private discussion among the founders; it may or may not be appropriate in any public-facing document.

**Q6.** Should Ideomotion's testimony pipeline (§5.3) require explicit written customer consent before any testimony is published, or is verbal consent sufficient? A draft consent process should be developed before the first publicly-published customer testimony.
