# Chapter 4: Component One — Holding the Conviction Firmly

The first component of the Christos Civitas Code is to hold the conviction firmly. With the foundational chapters complete — what we confess, why the confession is non-arbitrary, why the Cross is morally coherent — the relational-ethics framework now begins. And it begins with what the contemporary cultural etiquette would prefer to put last or to soften out of recognition: the firm holding of conviction.

This is the component the surrounding culture least wants to hear. The culture's preferred opening move on any moral question is the performance of inquiry — *let's hear all perspectives, let's be open, let's not foreclose dialogue prematurely.* These are not bad practices in their proper place. But when they become the *first* and *only* posture available to the believer, they have already conceded the contested ground. The believer who arrives at the conversation having already adopted the posture of *I'm just exploring along with everyone else* has surrendered the conviction before the conversation has begun. Whatever he says afterward, however carefully reasoned, is heard as one preference among many rather than as a confession of what is true.

The four-component framework therefore opens with the firm holding of conviction not because firmness is the highest Christian virtue but because firmness is the *prior* condition that makes the other three components possible. Loving the captured presupposes that the captured are in fact captured — that the position they hold is in fact a captivity rather than just an alternative perspective. Listening for spirit-discernment presupposes that there are spirits to discern — that what the friend brings into the conversation may be true, partly true, mis-channeled, or false, with real distinctions among these possibilities. Distinguishing certainties from prudential judgments presupposes that some things are certain and not prudential. Each of the other three components depends on the first.

So we begin with firmness. This chapter develops the spiritual-warfare frame within which firmness is the appropriate posture, distinguishes firmness from rigidity, and addresses the contemporary cultural pressures that push the believer toward false softness.

## The spiritual-warfare frame

*Put on the whole armour of God, that ye may be able to stand against the wiles of the devil. For we wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world, against spiritual wickedness in high places* (Ephesians 6:11-12).

This is not metaphor. The apostolic deposit's view of the believer's situation is unambiguous: there is a real spiritual conflict, with real spiritual enemies that are not human, operating through real human channels. The contemporary Christian temptation is to demythologize this language — to treat *principalities and powers* as quaint pre-modern shorthand for sociological forces, to treat *the devil* as a literary personification of human moral failure, to treat *seducing spirits* as a way of talking about cultural drift. The demythologization is itself one of the wiles by which the believer is disarmed. If the war is real, the believer who has talked himself out of the war by translating its language into safer categories has surrendered the field before the battle began.

The apostolic deposit's view of the situation is that the believer stands on contested ground in a real conflict, and that the conviction he holds is the stand-line of the conflict. *Stand therefore, having your loins girt about with truth, and having on the breastplate of righteousness; and your feet shod with the preparation of the gospel of peace; above all, taking the shield of faith, wherewith ye shall be able to quench all the fiery darts of the wicked. And take the helmet of salvation, and the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God* (Ephesians 6:14-17). The imagery is unembarrassed. The believer is a soldier. The convictions are armor and weapons. The standing is a defensive posture against active assault.

This is why firmness is the first component. The believer who is unsure where the line is, who will negotiate the line on social pressure, who treats the line as opinion rather than as truth, has effectively no line. The enemy moves wherever the believer's uncertainty allows him to move. The friends and family members and cultural conversations the believer engages with have nothing solid to push against and nothing solid to be drawn toward. The truth that could rescue them from captivity is not on offer because the believer has not made it available.

Firmness is the precondition for the rest of the work. It is not the whole of the work. But it is what the rest of the work rests on.

## The contemporary etiquette of performed-uncertainty

The contemporary cultural etiquette pressures the believer in the opposite direction. The polite move on any moral question, in the ambient atmosphere of late-modern American discourse, is to perform uncertainty about one's own commitments. *I'm not sure, I'm just thinking out loud, I see good points on both sides, who am I to say.* These performances are sometimes appropriate — there are questions on which one really should be uncertain — but they have become the *default* posture, the entry-condition for being heard at all. The believer who arrives at the conversation having already done his work and arrived at conclusions is treated as having committed a category error. Conclusions are bad form. Conclusions are not where conversations are supposed to end up; conversations are supposed to remain perpetually open.

This etiquette has cultural roots. The collapse of confidence in any moral truth that began with the late-modern philosophical movements (existentialism, critical theory, postmodernism) has settled into a folk epistemology in which all moral claims are merely expressions of preference. On this folk view, the only intellectually honest posture is to acknowledge one's claims as preferences, to entertain alternative preferences as equally valid, and to refuse the move that would rank some preferences as truer than others. To insist that one's position is *true* — not just preferred, not just culturally inherited, not just emotionally resonant, but *true* — is to violate the folk epistemology and to be heard as either naïve or aggressive.

The believer rejects this etiquette for two reasons. First, the etiquette is false. There are moral truths. They are not constructed by consensus. The believer's convictions, where they line up with what scripture teaches and what reality is, are not preferences but recognitions. Performing uncertainty about a recognition is dishonest. Second, the etiquette serves the ambient cultural lordships, not the gospel. A culture that has decided no one is allowed to confess what is true has built a barrier against the gospel, and the believer who adopts the barrier as his own posture has joined the barrier-maintenance project. The work of evangelism, of catechesis, of the formation of the believer's own children, of cultural witness, depends on confessing what is true. The etiquette of performed-uncertainty closes every one of these doors.

So the believer rejects the etiquette. Not by being rude — Component Two will address how to hold the firmness with love, and Component Three will address how to hold it with listening — but by refusing the entry-condition. The believer enters the conversation as someone who has done his work and arrived at conclusions, and is willing to defend the conclusions if the conversation requires it. *Be ready always to give an answer to every man that asketh you a reason of the hope that is in you with meekness and fear* (1 Peter 3:15). *Be ready* — not *be vague*. *Give an answer* — not *defer to your interlocutor's preference*. *With meekness and fear* — yes, the tone is gentle; but the answer is given.

## Firmness is not rigidity

A clarification is needed before proceeding, because the contemporary culture conflates firmness with rigidity in a way that obscures the distinction. The believer who holds the conviction firmly is not the believer who shouts at every disagreement, who refuses to listen, who treats every interlocutor as an enemy. That is rigidity, not firmness, and rigidity is its own corruption — Component Four will develop this in detail. The contemporary culture's conflation works to the culture's advantage, because it allows the culture to dismiss firmness as rigidity and to treat the only respectable Christian as the one who has dissolved his convictions into agreement with the culture's preferences.

The distinction is real. Firmness is the holding of conviction with confidence appropriate to the conviction's epistemic status. Rigidity is the holding of every conviction with the same maximum confidence regardless of epistemic status, and the treatment of every disagreement as an attack on the whole. The firm believer holds the gospel as gospel and the prudential judgment about specific tax policy as a prudential judgment, and knows the difference. The rigid believer holds his preferred tax policy as if it were the gospel. The firm believer engages the disagreeing friend with curiosity about what the friend has actually concluded and why. The rigid believer cannot hear the friend because the friend's disagreement has already been categorized as an attack on the whole.

Component Four will develop the taxonomy that lets the believer keep these distinctions straight. For now, the point is that firmness in Component One is not a license for rigidity. The conviction held firmly is the conviction; not every preference is the conviction.

## Earnestly contend

The active form of firmness is contending. *Beloved, when I gave all diligence to write unto you of the common salvation, it was needful for me to write unto you, and exhort you that ye should earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints* (Jude 3). The Greek word translated *earnestly contend* is *epagonizesthai*, from which we get *agonize* — the language of athletic contest, sustained effort, struggle on behalf of something worth struggling for. Jude is not commanding mild affirmation. He is commanding active, sustained, costly contending.

What is contended for? *The faith which was once delivered unto the saints.* The faith — the apostolic deposit, what we confess. The contending is not for the believer's own opinions, his preferred political program, his cultural preferences. The contending is for the deposit. This narrows the scope of the contending appropriately and keeps the believer from imagining that every cultural fight is the gospel fight.

But within that scope, the contending is real. Where the gospel is being denied, mis-stated, or replaced with a counterfeit, the believer contends. Where the apostolic deposit's content is being eroded inside the church or attacked from outside it, the believer contends. Where the lordship of Christ is being challenged by some rival lordship — political, ideological, philosophical, religious — the believer contends. The contending is not optional. It is the active form of the firmness the first component requires.

This is why the apostolic deposit is full of warnings against false teachers, exhortations to defend the faith, and direct confrontations with those who corrupt it. *I marvel that ye are so soon removed from him that called you into the grace of Christ unto another gospel: which is not another; but there be some that trouble you, and would pervert the gospel of Christ. But though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed* (Galatians 1:6-8). This is contending. Paul is not negotiating with the Judaizers; he is naming the corruption and pronouncing it accursed. *That ye be no more children, tossed to and fro, and carried about with every wind of doctrine* (Ephesians 4:14). *For there are many unruly and vain talkers and deceivers... whose mouths must be stopped* (Titus 1:10-11). The apostolic posture is not soft.

## Holding the line as the soldiers before us did

Christian history is full of believers who held the line at substantial cost. Athanasius standing alone against the Arian world (*Athanasius contra mundum*) is the paradigmatic case: a single bishop, exiled five times, refusing to soften the Trinitarian confession even when the imperial church was prepared to soften it for him. The English Reformation martyrs — Latimer, Ridley, Cranmer — burning rather than recant. The Confessing Church under National Socialism — Bonhoeffer, Niemöller, the Barmen Declaration — holding to *Jesus Christ as he is attested for us in Holy Scripture is the one Word of God whom we have to hear, and whom we have to trust and obey in life and in death*, against an imperial church that wanted to make Hitler the Word of God.

These believers were not temperamentally rigid. Many of them were gentle in their personal lives, careful with their interlocutors, sustained in their hope. But on the things that mattered, they did not move. The line was where the line was, and they would not be moved off it for any social or political or personal cost.

The contemporary American Christian rarely faces costs at the level Athanasius or Latimer or Bonhoeffer faced. He is not threatened with exile, burning, or execution. He is threatened with social inconvenience: lost friendships, awkward family gatherings, professional difficulty, perhaps the loss of certain career options. These are real costs, and they should not be minimized, but they are far smaller than the costs the great cloud of witnesses paid before us. If they could hold the line at the price of their lives, we can hold the line at the price of our social comfort.

The encouragement, then: the firmness Component One requires is the firmness the great cloud of witnesses already practiced. We are not innovating. We are participating in a tradition that has held since Pentecost, against every available pressure, and is holding still. Our faithfulness now is one more thread in a tapestry the apostles started weaving. The threads matter. The faithfulness matters. *Wherefore seeing we also are compassed about with so great a cloud of witnesses, let us lay aside every weight, and the sin which doth so easily beset us, and let us run with patience the race that is set before us* (Hebrews 12:1).

## What firmness looks like in a contemporary conversation

Practically: what does Component One look like when the believer is sitting across the table from a friend, family member, or colleague who holds a position the believer believes is wrong on a question of substance?

It looks like this. The believer, when the question arises, says what he actually thinks. Not aggressively. Not as a sermon. Not with a tone that signals contempt for the other position. Just plainly. *I think the personhood of the unborn child is real from conception, and that the legal regime that has permitted abortion at scale has been a regime of mass injustice.* Or *I think marriage is the union of one man and one woman, and that the cultural redefinition of marriage in the last twenty years has been a mistake.* Or *I think the constitutional ordering the founders bequeathed us is substantively good and should be defended where it has been eroded.* The conviction is stated. The believer does not retreat from the statement when the friend pushes back. He may receive the friend's pushback carefully, may listen to what the friend is actually saying, may acknowledge what is true in what the friend brings — Components Two and Three will develop these — but he does not dissolve his own statement under the pushback.

What the believer does *not* do, in Component One: he does not preface his statement with apologies for holding it. He does not soften it into agnosticism. He does not pretend not to believe what he believes. He does not retreat into procedural neutrality (*well, who's to say*) when the friend names the disagreement. He does not perform uncertainty he does not feel.

What the believer also does not do — and this is critical — is treat the conversation as won when he has stated his position firmly. Component One alone is not the whole framework. Stating the position firmly without the love of Component Two is contempt-culture conservatism, which has done substantial damage. Stating the position firmly without the listening of Component Three is monologue, which does no rescue work. Stating the position firmly without the taxonomy of Component Four is rigidity, which converts every disagreement into a wholesale fight.

But the position is stated. The line is held. The friend who has pushed back knows where the believer stands and knows that the believer will not move there for the friend's social comfort. From this stable line, Components Two through Four can do their work.

We turn now to the second component, which is the substantive heart of the framework: the hatred properly directed at the spiritual evil that captures, alongside the love properly directed at the human being captured. That is the work of Chapter 5, the longest and most demanding chapter of the book.
