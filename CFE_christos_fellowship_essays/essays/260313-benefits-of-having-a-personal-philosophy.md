By Steven Yates
March 12, 2026
A *core philosophy*is a set of values and priorities that center your life and provide you with a basis for meaningful action in a world seemingly gone mad, and in which we sometimes have to deal with terrible losses and sorrows.
For many of us, our core philosophy has always been grounded theologically: “God exists. He gave us His Word, the Bible, the Holy Scriptures, His commands.” These we try to live by; always imperfectly, which is why He came to Earth in the Person of Jesus Christ.
I have friends who kindly remind me when I start waxing on and on about the latest world crisis, “God is in control.”
I never know how to reply to this, or if I should. I’m neither a pastor nor a theologian. I’m not sure I have the best answer to what philosophers call the problem of evil when Christianity’s critics ask, Why has God allowed genocides if He is all-powerful and thus has the power to stop or prevent them? Why does He allow innocent children to die from deadly cancers? And why do some Christians fight and kill other Christians over doctrine?
More modestly: should I pray to God to get paid on time? I know of Christians who thank God when their misplaced car keys turn up. Are we so presumptuous as to think He cares about such things? Given the above, doesn’t He have more important matters to attend to? Especially when others pray for loved ones to be healed of debilitating diseases and nothing happens.
I think the fundamental problem, though, is that “God is in control” is not *actionable*. That’s the reason we have no idea what to say or do when someone says this. If you’re a Christian you know it’s true, but beyond offering comfort it’s unhelpful.
So for this project I’ve felt compelled to begin in a different place, to arrive somewhere with a greater sense of: “This is how to take the next step.”
**Why Have a Core Philosophy?**
First, though, what benefit is there to having a core philosophy — or set of core values, if you prefer? I think the greatest benefits come from how it helps a person “escape the Matrix,” as it were. Without such a perspective, one has only the “programming” one received from parents, in school, from peers, from media, perhaps from one’s dispensationalist pastor, etc. One risks being buffeted about by life, all but helpless in the face of events, helpless before what others say, what the crowd does (what is “popular”), what marketers concoct, what one’s employer does, and more broadly what those with wealth and power say and do.
These days, it’s worse than ever! I don’t think you can spend any time online anymore without being marketed to or hustled by “influencers” peddling some point of view, or just distracting you with *look at me! look at me! look at me!*
A core philosophy, created and cultivated, gives a person the capacity for at least some independent judgment, a place to stand amidst the noise, and hence genuine personal freedom — to follow or not to follow, to choose between narratives or write one’s own story; or to combine the narratives of others in ways that answer one’s own questions or solve one’s own problems. And filter out all the rest (probably 99% of what’s out there).
This can make a person dangerous to the crowd or to those with authority, or place you at a disadvantage in the much-vaunted marketplace.
After all, your core philosophy might tell you that your so-called political leaders have feet of clay; that most corporations are indifferent to anything except profit; that a totally unregulated marketplace might allow dangerous projects to flood into our homes unchecked if they make money. And that the person you must learn to trust the most is yourself.
I’ll just discuss two principles of my own core philosophy here. I hope these principles will seem reasonable to readers, although ultimately we’re all — each of us — responsible for what we choose to center our lives around.
**First Principle: Truth Matters!**
My first and most basic principle is that *truth matters!*
We are nearly always better off in the long run — and usually in the short run — if we believe what is true instead of falling for falsehoods and lies. Without getting into longwinded theories of what *truth* is, it should be clear: much of the time, determining what is true is straightforward. For example, it’s *true* that if I stepped off the balcony of my old apartment, ten floors up, I’d fall and be killed. Gravity is real. It’s physics. It’s not a “social construct.” That’s all one need say, and it’s more than one *should* need to say … but times being what they are….
Someone needs to tell this to those far-left gender-fluidity types, before more young people (including seriously misled *children*!) are maimed for life by the “hormone therapy” of “gender affirming care” and surgical alteration.
We should *rid* ourselves of this sickening and dangerous idea that one is “assigned a gender” at birth. No, *you are born a boy or a girl, based on your chromosomes*. This is sound biology, also solidly grounded theology:
**“God created man in His own image; in the image of God He created him; *male and female He created them*” (Gen. 1:27).**
There are a few exceptions, a few individuals born with damaged or incomplete chromosomal information. But these aren’t what the gender-benders have in mind; they are few in number; and they can’t be “fixed” with “gender affirming care.”
Truth matters! To deny truths of basic experience and what science has legitimately discovered about how things work is to invite unpleasant consequences.
Unfortunately, results of truth denial often don’t manifest themselves at once. Irrational monetary and other economic policies take years to exact their consequences, especially if they can be papered over, quick-fixed as it were, by *more* irrational policies, surrounded by a blanket of academic and mass media rationalization and denial.
Since falsehoods aren’t always evident and can be protected, those with vested interests in them may seem to have won great advantages: with government and in the marketplace.
The answer is that reality always gets the last laugh — sometimes it just takes a while. Bad monetary policies *always* end in crashes. *Always*. In the meantime, if *you* have stood for what you *know* is true and found yourself at a temporary disadvantage, at the very least, you can look at yourself in the mirror in the morning.
One caveat here: there are truths most people simply aren’t ready to hear.
Recall that line from Morpheus in *The Matrix*:
**“The Matrix is a system, Neo. That system is our enemy. But when you’re inside, you look around, what do you see? Businessmen, teachers, lawyers, carpenters. The very minds of the people we are trying to save. But until we do, these people are still a part of that system and that makes them our enemy. You have to understand, most of these people are not ready to be unplugged. And many of them are so inured, so hopelessly dependent on the system, that they will fight to protect it.”**
One may have reasonable certainty that one knows an important truth and still need to exercise reasonable discernment when thinking about telling it.
Will telling it accomplish anything?
Or will the resulting backlash harm only the truthteller?
You may know the truth, but it’s important to realize: you’re under no moral obligation to tell it at your own expense.
So don’t lie. Just be silent on the subject. Until the time is right.
The decision to speak or be silent is within your control, and that brings me to the *second* major element of my own core philosophy that seems to me worth sharing.
**Second Principle: Freedom (in *This* World) Comes Through Self-Mastery.**
Not mastery over events, or over other people.
That means having cultivated virtues like discernment and emotional self-control. According to the ancient Stoic philosophers (echoing Aristotle), there were four crucial virtues: temperance, courage, justice, wisdom. Cultivating these opens doors to discovering what matters, versus what ought to be ignored.
The 1st century Stoic philosopher Epictetus put his fingers on the key to self-mastery: *distinguishing what you can control from what you can’t control, and focusing the bulk of your attention on the former*. Epictetus spent the first 30 years of his life as a slave in Rome and knew hardships most of us can’t begin to fathom. He stated:
**“Some things are within our power, and some things are not within our power. Within our power are opinion, aim, desire, aversion, and, in one word, whatever affairs are our own. Beyond our power are body, property, reputation, office, and, in one word, whatever are not properly our own affairs.”**
Note the pattern here. *You* control *your* thoughts, emotional responses, immediate choices of action.
If you don’t control these, then, pray tell, who does?
Answer: anyone who wants to, who thinks they can gain advantage from it, or profit from it.
*You* have to control where you place your attention, especially given today’s environment which Epictetus could never have imagined, an environment in which you face constant bombardment with ads, information, whatever comes your way when you go online — even just to check email (which are overproduced and with which we are overloaded)! Or maybe just outside your front door!
It’s a commonplace that we can’t control the weather (leaving aside, for now, those claims that “they”*can*control it!). You can’t control what the economy does, which is just the aggregate choices made by multitudes of others.
You can’t control traffic: again, an aggregate product of others’ choices and priorities. Road rage is grade-A stupid! Not simply because you don’t know if the stranger you just yelled at might have a gun and be trigger-happy and not merely loose-tongued as you just were, but because you’ve assumed *his* priorities, which led him to cut you off in traffic, ought to reflect *your* priorities (to get to work, get to the store, whatever).
This last is the truly ridiculous assumption, if you just think it through.
Once, long ago, I had a girlfriend who, when we were out, would comment on someone across the room, whether it would be what they were wearing, or something equally trivial, and then stop abruptly and ask me, genuine fear in her voice, “You don’t think she heard me, do you?”
I had to explain patiently — repeatedly — some variation on, “No, Debbie. I don’t think she heard you. I don’t think she was listening to our private conversation. I don’t think we’re on her personal radar.”
We sometimes think and act as if we were the focus of others’ attention. Or should be. It shouldn’t take much reason to correct this, but often it does.
Adolescent minds always worry, *what will others think?*
Mature minds figure it out: *they aren’t thinking about me at all*.
Wiser adult minds realize: *they aren’t thinking about me at all and it’s a good thing, too!!* Because if they were, in most cases you’d quickly tire of the attention and want them to mind their own business!
Summing up: not in our power are the emotions, thoughts, opinions, and choices of others.
Not in our power are events, ranging from the trivial (e.g., rain this morning) to political-economic annoyances (tax season is here!) to the deep and truly disturbing (life and death).
This is easily grasped intellectually, I think. Living it is much, much harder. I’ll readily admit, I’m still a work in progress after all these years.
How *do* we accept major losses, e.g., parents, or — worse still — the passing of a beloved spouse — or perhaps worst of all, the loss of an innocent child.
Only through the painful recognition of mortality as a key feature of the human condition, our capacity to honor with our grief those who have passed, and then a determination to live on, because that is what the person lost would have wanted.
Stoic philosopher and Roman emperor — history’s *only* true philosopher-king — Marcus Aurelius put it like this:
**“You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength.”**
Such realizations are the keys to taking those crucial next steps.
Also recall Solomon, in Ecclesiastes (3:1-2; 12:13):
**“To everything there is a season, a time for every purpose under heaven. A time to be born, and a time to die; a time to plant, and a time to harvest….  Fear God and keep His commandments, for this is man’s all.”**
With that, I think we’ve come full circle.
