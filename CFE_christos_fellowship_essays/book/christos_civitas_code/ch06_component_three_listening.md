# Chapter 6: Component Three — Listening as Theological Discipline

The first two components of the Christos Civitas Code establish the believer's posture: hold the conviction firmly, in the spiritual warfare frame, with hatred properly directed at the spiritual evil and love properly directed at the human person captured. The third component is what makes this posture operationally possible. The believer who arrives at the conversation having already held his conviction firmly and having sorted his hatred and love properly still has work to do *in the conversation itself*. He must listen — not as performance, not as pastoral courtesy, but as theological discipline.

This chapter argues that listening is more than a virtue alongside the others. Listening is a theologically required practice that has two distinct warrants in the apostolic deposit, and that produces, when practiced well, four diagnostic possibilities for what the believer's response should be. Without the listening, the believer is responding to a phantom of the conversation rather than to the conversation itself, and his response will at best miss the mark and at worst do harm. With the listening, he can engage the conversation God is actually presenting to him and respond as the moment requires.

The chapter has four movements. First, listening as more than pastoral courtesy. Second, the first warrant: listening pleases God to be known, doing for the neighbor what God does for us. Third, the second warrant: listening is required for spirit-discernment per 1 John 4:1. Fourth, the four diagnostic possibilities — affirm, introduce, redirect, resist — and how to recognize each.

## Listening as more than pastoral courtesy

The contemporary Christian conversation about listening tends to treat it as a soft virtue — the kind of thing we praise in pastoral counseling settings but treat as optional in serious doctrinal or political conversation. The believer has the truth; the friend may or may not have any of the truth; the believer's job is to deliver the truth and the friend's job is to receive it. Listening, on this view, is a courtesy extended to make the delivery feel less harsh. It is not part of the substantive work.

This view is wrong, and it is wrong in a way that produces the contempt-culture posture Chapter 5 named as one of the two flattenings. The believer who has decided in advance what he is going to say to every interlocutor on every question, regardless of what the interlocutor actually says, is not engaging the interlocutor; he is delivering monologues at a person-shaped object. The interlocutor knows this. The interlocutor responds to the monologue-delivery posture by closing whatever openness he might have brought to the conversation. The believer experiences the closure as further evidence that the interlocutor is hardened against the gospel; in fact the closure is the believer's own posture echoing back at him. Nothing is being communicated because nothing is being heard.

Listening is not a courtesy that softens the delivery. It is the substantive discipline by which the believer comes to know who he is talking with, what they actually believe, what spirit is operative in the conversation, and what response the moment actually calls for. Without it, the believer is shooting in the dark, and his hits — when they happen — happen by accident.

This chapter develops listening as theological discipline by grounding it in two warrants the apostolic deposit makes explicit. Each warrant is independently sufficient to require the practice. Together, they make listening as foundational to the relational ethic as the conviction-holding of Component One and the love-and-hatred-direction of Component Two.

## The first warrant: listening pleases God to be known

Consider the structure of biblical revelation. From Genesis through Revelation, the dominant pattern is that God makes Himself known. He speaks through creation (Romans 1:20). He speaks to the patriarchs and through Moses. He speaks through the prophets. He speaks, finally and decisively, through the Son (Hebrews 1:1-2). The whole biblical story is the story of a God who wishes to be known by His creatures, who takes the initiative to make Himself known, and who desires that the knowing be received as gift.

What does it look like for a creature to receive God's self-disclosure well? It looks like attention. It looks like *let him that hath ears to hear, hear* (Matthew 11:15). It looks like Mary at the feet of Jesus, listening to Him speak, while Martha cumbered herself about much serving (Luke 10:38-42). It looks like the disciples being told, on the Mount of Transfiguration, *this is my beloved Son: hear him* (Mark 9:7). The reception of revelation is the reception of a person making Himself known, and the form of the reception is hearing, attention, the willingness to receive what is being given.

If this is the structure of God's relationship to His creatures — He makes Himself known, and the creatures' job is to hear — then the structure of the believer's relationship to other image-bearers should reflect it. When another person speaks, when another person tells us what they think and what they have lived through and what they believe, they are doing for us a small-scale version of what God does on the cosmic scale: they are making themselves known. The proper response to being made-known-to is hearing, attention, the willingness to receive what is being given.

*Be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath* (James 1:19). This is not just prudential advice for managing arguments. It is a reflection of God's own posture toward His creatures, who hears their cries before they finish speaking and is patient through their full account. The believer who is swift-to-speak and slow-to-hear is failing to image the God whose patience-with-creature precedes the creature's full self-disclosure.

The first warrant for listening, then, is that listening honors the image-bearer status of the person being listened to. Refusing to listen carefully is refusing to do for the neighbor what God does for us. The neighbor who feels unheard by the believer is feeling, in some real sense, unheard by the believer-as-bearer-of-Christ-to-them. The believer's listening is part of the Christ he is bringing to the conversation.

There is also the converse, which Christ stated directly. *Inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least of these my brethren, ye have done it unto me* (Matthew 25:40). Listening carefully is one of the things we do *to* people. The listening is, in the structure of the kingdom, something we do to Christ Himself. The friend who feels heard at the kitchen table has, in some real way, been heard by Christ through the believer's listening; the friend who feels unheard at the kitchen table has, in some real way, been unheard by Christ through the believer's failure to listen.

This warrant is sufficient by itself. Even if listening produced no diagnostic information about what to say next, it would still be required, because it is what the image-bearer is owed and what God Himself does for us. But there is a second warrant, which makes the listening operationally as well as relationally necessary.

## The second warrant: listening is required for spirit-discernment

*Beloved, believe not every spirit, but try the spirits whether they are of God: because many false prophets are gone out into the world* (1 John 4:1). This verse commands a discipline that requires listening. The discipline is the *trying* of spirits — the testing of what is in the conversation to determine what its origin is. The friend speaking is bringing into the conversation some configuration of spirit, and the believer's job is to recognize what configuration that is before responding.

The configurations are not always obvious. The friend may be speaking from the Holy Spirit, even when the friend is not himself a believer — God is not constrained to speak only through professed Christians, and a thoughtful natural-law conservative or a sincere seeker may bring real truth into the conversation that the believer should recognize and affirm. The friend may be speaking from a spirit of deception, even when the friend is professedly Christian — false teaching is a real category, and the believer is commanded to recognize it (1 John 4:1, 2 John 1:7-11, Galatians 1:8). The friend may be speaking from a mixture — from real moral seriousness mis-channeled, from real grief that has hardened into ideology, from real love that has been distorted by a captivity. The configurations are diverse and the recognition requires careful attention.

The second warrant for listening, then, is that the believer cannot discharge the discipline of trying spirits without listening first. He cannot recognize what is in the conversation if he has not heard what is in the conversation. He cannot respond appropriately to the configuration if he has not identified the configuration. The discipline of spirit-discernment is not optional for the believer — it is commanded — and listening is the condition of the discipline.

This second warrant has practical force. A believer who responds to every conversation with the same response, regardless of what the friend has actually said, is not discerning spirits; he is delivering a fixed monologue. The fixed monologue may sometimes hit; more often it will miss, and the missing is not innocent — it is the failure to do the work the apostolic deposit explicitly commands. The disciplined believer listens long enough to know what is in the conversation, then responds appropriately to what is actually there.

## The four diagnostic possibilities

What configurations might the listening reveal, and how does the response vary based on what is found?

The apostolic deposit and the broader Christian tradition suggest four diagnostic possibilities. Each requires a different response. The disciplined believer learns to recognize each and to respond with the appropriate kind of speaking-after-listening.

### 1. Affirm: when the spirit is in agreement with the Holy Spirit

The first possibility is that the friend is speaking truth — that the spirit operative in their speech is in agreement with the Holy Spirit, and that the appropriate response is recognition and affirmation.

This possibility is more common than the contempt-culture posture allows. Truth is not the exclusive property of professed Christians. *Despise not prophesyings* (1 Thessalonians 5:20) — Paul commands the church not to despise speech that comes with the marks of the Spirit, even when its source is unexpected. *Prove all things; hold fast that which is good* (1 Thessalonians 5:21) — the testing produces a recognition of what is good, and the good is to be held fast even where its source surprises.

The natural-law conservative who reaches conclusions through general revelation is often speaking truth that the believer should affirm. The honest atheist who has noticed something true about human nature or about the moral structure of human life is often speaking truth. The cultural critic, even one who is hostile to Christianity, who has identified a real pathology in the surrounding culture is often speaking truth. The friend whose first-person account of his own life reveals real struggle, real growth, real wisdom hard-won, is often speaking truth.

When the listening reveals that the spirit operative in the friend's speech is in agreement with the Holy Spirit, the believer's response is to recognize and affirm. *Yes — that is right.* *Yes — I see what you are seeing, and I think you have named it correctly.* *Yes — this is true, and I am glad you have named it.* The affirmation does not require the friend to agree with the believer about everything else; it does not require the conversation to become a soliloquy of agreement. It is simply the recognition that on this point, in this moment, the friend has said something true, and the believer affirms it.

The believer who cannot affirm anything the non-Christian says has misjudged the situation. He has assumed in advance that the non-Christian's speech is uniformly wrong, and the assumption has prevented him from hearing what is actually there. The disciplined listener can hear the truth in the friend's speech and can name it as truth, even when many other things in the friend's framework remain in disagreement.

### 2. Introduce: when the spirit is absent and the Holy Spirit is to be introduced

The second possibility is that the conversation is operating in a frame in which the Holy Spirit's witness is absent, and the believer's task is to introduce what is missing.

This is the missionary task, properly conceived. The friend is operating with a worldview that does not include the gospel — perhaps a thoughtful secular worldview, perhaps a non-Christian religious worldview, perhaps an unreflective consumer-culture worldview that has never explicitly considered the gospel. The conversation is not about contesting positions held against the gospel; it is about whether the gospel is in the conversation at all.

The believer's task in this case is to introduce. Not aggressively, not as a sermon, but to add what is missing in a way the friend can actually hear. *Have you considered what the Christian view says about this?* *I think there is something in the way Christianity frames this that you might find worth engaging.* *The reason I see this differently is that the gospel teaches…* The introduction is calibrated to the conversation: it adds what is missing without overwhelming the friend with everything the believer believes about the topic.

The introduction requires the listening. The believer must have heard the friend's frame carefully enough to know what is missing and to add it in a form the friend can receive. The introduction made before the listening is shotgun-evangelism — it sprays the gospel at someone whose actual frame the believer has not bothered to understand, and it tends not to land. The introduction made after careful listening is targeted — it adds what the friend's frame is actually missing, in the form the friend's frame can actually receive.

### 3. Redirect: when the spirit is present but inappropriate

The third possibility is the most subtle and may be the most common. The friend is bringing into the conversation real spiritual energy — real moral seriousness, real concern for justice, real love for some particular human good — but the energy has been mis-channeled. The friend's concern is real and right; the channel through which it is currently flowing is wrong, and is producing distorted conclusions.

Examples are everywhere in the contemporary moment. Real concern for justice has been mis-channeled into ideological rage that produces injustice. Real concern for compassion has been mis-channeled into the affirmation of self-destructive patterns that harm the people compassion was meant to help. Real concern for truth has been mis-channeled into conspiratorial cynicism that loses the capacity to recognize truth when it appears. Real concern for the suffering of marginalized groups has been mis-channeled into a totalizing ideology that prevents the actual help the suffering need.

In each case, the underlying spiritual energy is good. The believer can recognize and honor it. *I see what you are responding to. I share that concern. The suffering you are describing is real, and the impulse to address it is right.* But the channeling has been distorted, and the believer's task is to redirect — to name the distortion, offer a different channel for the same underlying energy, and invite the friend to consider whether the redirection might produce the actual good the friend has been reaching for.

The redirect is harder than the introduce. It requires the believer to recognize what is good in the friend's frame even where the friend's conclusions are wrong, and to offer a redirection that does not invalidate the underlying energy. The temptation, for the believer who has not done the work of careful listening, is to attack the conclusions and miss the underlying energy entirely — which leaves the friend feeling unheard and reinforces the friend's commitment to the mis-channeled form of his own real concern.

The redirect, done well, can be one of the most fruitful conversational moves the believer makes. It honors the friend's seriousness, names the real good the friend is reaching for, and offers a path that may actually produce the good the friend wanted in the first place. Many friends who would never accept a simple introduce-the-gospel move will accept a redirect, because the redirect begins with what they already care about and shows them how the same caring leads somewhere different from where they have ended up.

### 4. Resist: when the spirit is opposed to the Holy Spirit

The fourth possibility is the hardest, and the one the contemporary culture most wants to forbid. Sometimes the spirit operative in the conversation is not material to be corrected, not partial truth to be supplemented, not real concern to be redirected — it is active opposition to God to be resisted.

The seducing spirits and doctrines of devils Paul names in 1 Timothy 4:1 are real. The wolf in sheep's clothing of Matthew 7:15 is real. The angel of light who masquerades as an angel of light in 2 Corinthians 11:14 is real. Some of what gets brought into conversations is not just wrong but is actively opposed to the gospel and is operating with spiritual force to obscure or replace it. The disciplined believer can recognize when this is what is happening and can refuse to host the spirit.

Refusing to host the spirit is not the same as attacking the person. The person is still loved (Component Two). The image-bearer status persists. The relational ethic of love is not suspended. But the *spirit* operative in the conversation is named, refused, resisted. The believer does not pretend not to recognize what he recognizes; he does not give the spirit hospitality. He may end the conversation, or he may name what he is seeing and decline to engage further on its terms, or he may insist on different terms before continuing. The form varies. The substance is that he does not let the spirit pass.

This possibility makes the contemporary culture nervous, because the culture has decided that calling something a *spirit of deception* or a *doctrine of devils* is itself an act of cultural aggression. The culture's preferred posture is that everything is to be heard out, everything is to be entertained, everything is to be treated as one perspective among many. The believer rejects this. *Try the spirits* requires that some be rejected; the apostolic deposit explicitly commands the rejection. The believer who cannot resist anything is not faithfully neutral; he is failing to do the discernment work the apostolic deposit commands.

The four diagnostic possibilities are not always cleanly separable. A given conversation may move among them, requiring different responses at different moments. A friend may bring in something to be affirmed, then something to be redirected, then something to be resisted, all within an hour at the kitchen table. The disciplined listener can move with the conversation — affirming what is true, introducing what is missing, redirecting what is mis-channeled, resisting what is opposed to the gospel — without the conversation collapsing into a single fixed posture.

This is what listening as theological discipline looks like in practice. It is harder than monologue. It is harder than fixed-position-defense. It requires the believer to be present to the conversation rather than reciting a script. But it is what the apostolic deposit commands, and it is what conscious participation in the Christos Civitas requires.

## The closing discipline

Listening is not optional. It is theological discipline, with two warrants in the apostolic deposit — listening pleases God to be known and honors the image-bearer being listened to; listening is required for the spirit-discernment 1 John 4:1 commands. Without it, the believer is delivering monologues at person-shaped objects and confusing the experience for evangelism. With it, the believer is engaging actual conversations with actual people in whom the Holy Spirit may already be at work, in whom partial truth may need affirmation, in whom the gospel may need introduction, in whom mis-channeled concern may welcome redirection, and through whom seducing spirits may need active resistance.

The four diagnostic possibilities — affirm, introduce, redirect, resist — give the believer a vocabulary for thinking about how to respond after he has listened. They do not exhaust the possibilities; the actual conversations are richer than any taxonomy. But they orient the believer toward the kinds of move he might make and away from the contempt-culture default of treating every disagreement as opposition-to-be-defeated.

We turn now to the fourth and final component, which is the discipline that prevents the framework from collapsing into rigidity: the careful distinguishing of certainties, strong inferences, and prudential judgments. That is the work of Chapter 7.
