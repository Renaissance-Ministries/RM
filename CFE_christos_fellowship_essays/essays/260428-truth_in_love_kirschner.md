# Truth in Love, Across the Lines That Divide

## A Christos Civitas Code of Relational Ethics — In Conversation with Dr. Rick Kirschner

**Fellowship Discussion Essay | April 28, 2026**

**Occasion:** This essay was prompted by a public reflection from Dr. Rick Kirschner, ND — author, speaker, and longtime colleague — who recently posted on social media about the grief he is feeling as American discourse fractures, friendships thin out, and moral certainty squeezes out kindness. He is contemplating writing a book tentatively titled *False Friends and Fractured Professionals: How Good People Drift Into Bad Behavior — and How to Be a Good Person Anyway.* His proposed chapters include *The Seduction of Moral Superiority*, *Certainty as a Social Drug*, *How Groups Reward Conformity (and Punish Thought)*, *Curiosity vs. Judgment: The Fork in Every Conversation*, and *Boundaries Without Bitterness*. The diagnosis is sound and the proposed cures are humane. The question I want to engage in this essay is the one his framework leaves implicit: *what do you do as a Christian, or as a thoughtful natural-law conservative, when you are genuinely convinced that on certain fundamental questions you actually do have substantially correct positions, and the other side is substantially mistaken?* Kirschner himself, as a thoughtful cultural conservative who reaches his conclusions through natural-law reasoning rather than scriptural warrant, is not a relativist; he holds convictions firmly. The question is therefore not *Kirschner versus the convinced Christian* but *how do those who hold firm convictions — for whatever reason — live the relational ethic Kirschner describes without dissolving the convictions themselves?*

**Context:** This is the first essay in what may become an extended Christos Civitas treatment of relational ethics. The Christos Civitas framework was named in the [April 19 fellowship-meeting analysis](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/CFE_christos_fellowship_essays/essays/260419-where_is_god_in_this.md) and is positioned as conscious participation in the church Christ has been building since Pentecost rather than the founding of any new movement. The relational-ethics treatment is part of working out what conscious participation in Christ's ongoing work looks like in 2026 American life — including in conversations with friends across deep disagreement, of which the [systematic Snuffer-engagement corpus](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/CFE_christos_fellowship_essays/essays/260428-snuffer_addresses_to_all_christians.md) committed earlier today is one specific instance.

---

To the Fellowship —

Rick has put his finger on something real. Let me start by saying it as plainly as he did.

People we thought we knew are looking at the same world, the same events, the same people, and reaching opposite conclusions — and *despising us for ours*. The despising is the part that hurts most, because the disagreement was always part of life. People disagree. Marriages survive disagreement. Friendships survive disagreement. Republics survive disagreement. What they do not always survive is the moment when disagreement becomes *contempt* — when the person who disagrees with me is no longer simply a friend who sees the world differently but is recategorized as a moral defective whose disagreement is itself evidence of his corruption. When that recategorization happens, the friendship is essentially over even before either side declares it. Curiosity dies. The exchange of views becomes the exchange of accusations. As Rick observes, the relationship is already finished once the contempt becomes mutual.

This is happening across America, and it is happening on both sides of every fault line. It is happening between Christians and secularists, between conservatives and progressives, between Catholics and evangelicals, between vaccinated and unvaccinated, between those who think 2020 was stolen and those who think 2024 was stolen, between those who watch one set of news sources and those who watch another. The pattern is the same in every direction. The friendships that survive are increasingly the friendships among people who already agree about everything, and the friendships that fracture are the friendships across difference, which were the friendships that made up the fabric of an actually-functional civil society. What we are watching is not just the fraying of individual relationships. It is the unraveling of the social fabric on which the American experiment depended.

Rick's diagnosis of the symptoms is correct. His proposed cures are also correct as far as they go: *be thoughtful, be fair, be curious when we disagree.* I agree with every word of that. The question this essay engages is not whether Rick is right about the diagnosis — he is — but rather what the cure looks like for those of us who hold firm convictions on questions that we believe are not in epistemic equipoise. The question is harder than Rick's framing makes it, because Rick himself is not in equipoise either. He is a cultural conservative who reaches conclusions through natural-law reasoning, the logic of fairness, outcome comparisons, premise-to-conclusion fidelity, self-consistency, and empirical evidence. When he calls for curiosity in disagreement, he is not saying that all positions are equally valid. He is saying that the people holding the positions are equally human and deserve to be engaged as such, even when their positions are mistaken. That is closer to the Christian view than the surface of his framing suggests, and it is the convergence I want to begin from.

I will engage the question in five movements. First, what is true in Rick's diagnosis. Second, what is incomplete in the procedural framing as it tends to be heard, and what the convinced Christian or convinced natural-law conservative still owes the conversation. Third, the three-component Christian alternative — holding the conviction firmly, loving the person more than the position, and distinguishing the certainties from the prudential judgments. Fourth, the proposed structure of a Christos Civitas code of relational ethics as a book that could serve both Christians and natural-law conservatives in their shared difficulty. Fifth, application to the fellowship's actual situation in 2026 — the conversations with Leonard, with Mike Sherman, with extended family across political lines, and with the cultural moment we are living through.

## I. What Is True in Rick's Diagnosis

The first move is to acknowledge that Rick is describing something real, not exaggerating, and not picking on any one side.

**The pattern is real.** People who used to be able to disagree without contempt have, in the last decade or so, become unable to do so at scale. The pattern is not symmetrical — different communities have fallen into it at different rates and through different mechanisms — but it is genuinely cross-partisan. The cancel-culture instinct on the secular left and the contempt-culture instinct on the populist right are mirror images of the same underlying pathology: the collapse of the distinction between the person and the position, and the consequent licensing of contempt for the person whose position differs.

**The mechanism is real.** Rick names it precisely. The information ecosystem we inhabit — algorithmically curated, engagement-optimized, outrage-amplified — does not reward the careful articulation of disagreement; it rewards the production of contempt. Rick's diagnosis is that the steady stream of information we swim in has tied moral meaning to political positions, so that mere disagreement begins to feel like a character flaw rather than a difference in perspective. Once that re-coding happens, curiosity fades and certainty takes over. Every social media platform monetizes contempt because contempt drives engagement and engagement drives ad revenue. We are not in this place by accident. We are in this place because powerful institutions have spent the last fifteen years building infrastructure that systematically rewards contempt and punishes nuance. The personal failure of generosity is real; it is also the predictable output of a system designed to produce exactly this failure at scale.

**The cost is real.** Rick is grieving something specific: friendships thinning out, conversations becoming brittle, nuance disappearing. This is not abstract. This is the texture of life in 2026. People who used to talk about everything now censor themselves around their families. People who used to have genuine intellectual exchange with colleagues now stay in safe lanes. Friendships that survived the pre-2016 disagreements have dissolved over post-2016 disagreements. The grief is appropriate. Something has been lost.

**The corruption of moral certainty is real.** Rick names *the seduction of moral superiority* and *certainty as a social drug.* These are not strawmen. They are real spiritual conditions that real people fall into, and they are no respecter of party or tradition. The progressive who is certain that anyone disagreeing with the latest cultural orthodoxy is a moral defective is suffering from this corruption. The conservative who is certain that anyone disagreeing with his political reading of current events is a moral defective is suffering from the same corruption in mirror image. The corruption is not the certainty itself; certainty about true things is appropriate. The corruption is the slide from *I am certain about this* to *anyone who disagrees with me is morally deficient* to *I am therefore licensed to despise them.* That slide is happening on every side, and it is poisoning the country.

So far, Rick is right. The diagnosis is accurate, the mechanism is named, the cost is real, the moral pathology is identified. Where the conversation gets harder is at the next step, where Rick's procedural cures meet the actual situation of someone who holds firm convictions.

## II. What the Procedural Framing Leaves Implicit

The cures Rick proposes — thoughtfulness, fairness, curiosity in disagreement, the refusal of contempt, the setting of boundaries without bitterness — are not wrong. They are necessary. But by themselves they leave a question implicit that the convinced Christian and the convinced natural-law conservative both have to answer, and that the contemporary culture has largely been refusing to answer: *what do you do when your friend is genuinely wrong about something that genuinely matters?*

The procedural framing as it tends to be heard in 2026 American culture has an unstated premise: *all serious disagreements are reducible to differences in perspective, and the work of virtue is to hold one's own perspective lightly enough to maintain relationship across the difference.* On this reading, the answer to *what do you do when your friend is wrong* becomes *recognize that he is not actually wrong, he just sees it differently, and the work is to honor the difference.* This is the move that postmodern liberalism has been making for fifty years, and it is, on Christian and natural-law grounds alike, false.

Some disagreements are reducible to differences in perspective. Whether Beethoven is greater than Bach. Whether the mountains are more beautiful than the sea. Whether baseball is more interesting than basketball. These are matters of taste, and the work of friendship across them is exactly what the procedural framing describes: hold your view lightly, honor the other person's view, enjoy the conversation.

Some disagreements are not reducible to differences in perspective. Whether the unborn child has the same moral standing as the born. Whether marriage is the union of one man and one woman. Whether the human person is a body-soul unity made in the image of God or a chemistry experiment with delusions of meaning. Whether human beings have a nature that constrains their flourishing or whether they are infinitely self-fashioning. These are not matters of taste. They are claims about reality. One of two opposing positions on each of them is correct and the other is mistaken, and the gap between them has consequences that no amount of procedural goodwill can dissolve.

A Christian who believes that the unborn child has the moral standing of personhood, and that the legal regime permitting the routine killing of unborn children is a regime of mass injustice, cannot honestly say to a friend who supports that regime, *we just see things differently.* He believes his friend is mistaken about something on which a great deal turns. To pretend otherwise is to lie to the friend, and the friendship purchased through that lie is not actually a friendship. The same is true on every other question of moral substance. To engage the friend honestly is to acknowledge the disagreement at the level it actually exists — *I think you are wrong about this, and I think the wrongness has serious consequences, and I am unable in honesty to relate to you as if we were merely differing in taste* — and then to ask, *given that, can we still walk together?*

Rick himself, I suspect, would not actually disagree with the foregoing. He is a cultural conservative who has watched the progressive cultural revolution of the last fifteen years with growing alarm. His proposed book is partly about how once-rational people slide into certainty-tribalism-moral-superiority *and stop questioning the very narratives they once would have examined.* He is not in equipoise. He believes those who have made that slide are mistaken, and he is willing to say so. What his framing does is hold the disagreement at the level of *how to engage* rather than *what is true* — and that is a useful framing for a book about discourse, but it leaves the deeper question unresolved.

The deeper question is this: *if I am genuinely convinced that I have substantially correct positions on the great moral and civic questions of our time, and that those who hold opposite positions are substantially mistaken, what does the relational ethic actually look like?* It is not procedural neutrality, because I am not neutral. It is not contempt, because contempt forecloses the possibility that the friend may yet be persuaded, and because contempt is itself wrong. What is it?

The Christian answer — and, I think, the answer a thoughtful natural-law conservative would also reach through his own warrant — has three components.

## III. The Three-Component Alternative

### Component One: Hold the Conviction Firmly

The first move is to refuse the contemporary pressure to soften the conviction in order to keep the friendship.

This pressure is enormous in 2026 American culture. The contemporary etiquette is that holding a firm conviction on a contested moral question is itself rude, that the price of admission to polite society is the willingness to perform uncertainty about one's own commitments, and that anyone who refuses to perform that uncertainty is acting in bad faith. This etiquette is not a Christian virtue. It is, for that matter, not a natural-law virtue either. It is a particular cultural posture of the late-modern West, generated by the postmodern collapse of confidence in any moral truth, and it should be rejected on the merits.

Christians believe that scripture teaches things, and that those things are true, and that the Christian's job is to confess them as true even when the confession costs him something. *Speaking the truth in love* (Ephesians 4:15) requires the truth to be there to speak. *Earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints* (Jude 3) — the contending is part of the Christian vocation. *Be ready always to give an answer to every man that asketh you a reason of the hope that is in you* (1 Peter 3:15) — *be ready*, not *be vague*. The apostolic posture is not equipoise. It is conviction held with the appropriate tone — *with meekness and fear*, the verse continues — but it is conviction.

The natural-law conservative reaches a similar conclusion through different warrant. If there is a moral order built into the structure of the human person, and if that order is accessible through careful reasoning from observation and experience, then to refuse to confess what reasoning shows is itself a kind of intellectual dishonesty. The thoughtful natural-law conservative does not need to perform agnosticism about marriage, about the moral status of the unborn, about the relationship between work and human flourishing, about the conditions under which property and liberty produce human good. He has reasoned his way to conclusions, and he holds them. Performing equipoise about them would be a betrayal of his own intellectual integrity.

So the first component of the relational ethic, on Christian and natural-law grounds alike, is to refuse the pressure to soften. The conviction is held firmly. The Christian does not pretend to be unsure that the unborn child is a person. The natural-law conservative does not pretend to be unsure that marriage as a social institution has a definite shape that the surrounding culture has been wrong to redefine. The convictions are held, confessed, and not negotiated away to keep the friendship comfortable.

### Component Two: Love the Person More Than You Hold the Position

The second move is the harder one, and it is the move that the contemporary culture, on every side of every fault line, has largely abandoned: *love the person more than you hold the position*. Not less than. *More than*.

This sounds like a contradiction with Component One, but it is not. Component One says *hold the conviction firmly*. Component Two says *love the person fiercely*. Both are operative simultaneously, and the discipline of the Christian relational ethic is keeping both alive at once. The cancel-culture instinct on the left collapses Component Two into Component One: the position is held so firmly that the person disagreeing with it becomes a target. The contempt-culture instinct on the right does the same in mirror image: the conviction about the cultural revolution becomes so fierce that the human beings caught up in it become objects of contempt rather than image-bearers to be loved. Both sides are doing the same wrong move, and both sides need to be called out of it.

The Christian warrant for Component Two is that every human being is made in the image of God (Genesis 1:27) and is among those for whom Christ died (John 3:16, 1 Timothy 2:6). To despise a person — *any* person — is to despise an image-bearer of God and to despise one for whom Christ shed His blood. This is theologically foreclosed. It is not allowed. The Christian who claims to hold orthodox positions firmly while treating those who disagree with him with contempt is in violation of the second great commandment (Mark 12:31) and is, on that score, no better than those whose positions he opposes.

The natural-law warrant reaches a similar conclusion. The dignity of the human person is not contingent on the correctness of his current opinions. A friend who has reached a wrong conclusion is still a friend, still a person, still possesses the inherent worth that makes him deserving of fair treatment, careful listening, and honest engagement. To recategorize him as a moral defective on the basis of his having reached a wrong conclusion is to treat him as something less than a person, and the natural-law conservative who would not tolerate such recategorization in the abstract should not tolerate it in himself in the concrete.

The discipline of Component Two is that it is *more demanding than either contempt or endorsement*. Contempt is easy: you write the person off, and you no longer owe him anything. Endorsement is easy: you pretend to agree, and the friendship is preserved at the cost of honesty. Loving the person while disagreeing with the position is hard, because it requires you to keep showing up — to keep listening, to keep speaking honestly, to keep being available — even when the disagreement is real and the cost is real. It is the harder path because it does not let you off the hook.

The contemporary cultural pressure is to collapse the love into the agreement. The phrase *if you really loved me you would affirm my truth* is the catchphrase of this collapse. It is theologically and humanly false. *If you really loved me you would tell me the truth even when it costs you* is closer to the actual structure of love. A father who watches his son drift into addiction does not love the son more by affirming the addiction; he loves the son more by telling the truth about the addiction even when the truth is unwelcome. The truth-telling is the love. The pretended affirmation would be the abandonment. This applies, with appropriate calibration, to friendships across moral disagreement. Love does not require agreement. Love sometimes *requires* disagreement, voiced honestly, and held in place over time.

### Component Three: Distinguish the Certainties from the Prudential Judgments

The third component is the discipline that Christian conservatives — and, I think, conservative Christians as a movement — have most often gotten wrong, and where the most useful internal correction is needed.

Not all of our convictions occupy the same epistemic space. Some are scriptural certainties: confessed in the apostolic deposit, held firmly by the historic creedal traditions, and beyond reasonable Christian debate. Some are strong scriptural inferences: applications of clear scriptural principles to specific questions where reasonable Christians have reached largely converging conclusions. Some are prudential judgments: applications of broader scriptural principles to complex contemporary situations where reasonable Christians of good faith may reach different conclusions. The discipline is *knowing which kind of question you are dealing with*, and the temptation on every side is to elevate prudential judgments to scriptural certainties so that disagreement on prudential matters becomes evidence of moral failure or spiritual compromise.

Examples may help.

*Scriptural certainties.* The lordship of Jesus Christ. The Trinitarian God. The full deity and full humanity of Christ. The bodily resurrection. The authority of scripture. The reality of sin and the necessity of grace. The personhood of the unborn child from conception. The created order of marriage as the union of one man and one woman. The moral seriousness of work and the obligation of charity to the poor. These are not negotiable for orthodox Christians. They are confessed, and the confession is non-prudential.

*Strong scriptural inferences.* The general pattern of constitutional ordering — government of limited powers, the rule of law, the protection of liberty under law — is a strong inference from biblical principles about the nature of human authority, the reality of sin, and the requirement of justice. Christians have reached largely converging conclusions on the wisdom of this pattern, even though scripture does not specify it as such. The general moral seriousness of family life, of the obligations of parents to children, of fidelity in marriage, of honesty in commerce — these are strong inferences with broad Christian consensus, even though specific applications may vary by situation.

*Prudential judgments.* The optimal tax rate for funding the limited functions of government. The specific structure of immigration policy that best balances justice and order. The specific approach to public schooling that best serves families and the common good. The specific economic policies that best foster human flourishing while protecting the vulnerable. The specific foreign policy posture that best fulfills the requirements of justice and prudence in a fallen world. The specific approach to environmental stewardship that best balances multiple legitimate goods. Christians of good faith reach different conclusions on these questions, and the differences should be held with appropriate humility.

The discipline is keeping the categories straight. The cowardice of the soft Christian is to treat the certainties as prudential — to act as if the personhood of the unborn or the created order of marriage were matters of opinion on which reasonable Christians may differ. They are not. The rigidity of the hard Christian is the opposite — to treat the prudential as certainties, so that any Christian who disagrees with him on tax policy or immigration policy or specific approaches to particular cultural moments is recategorized as theologically suspect or spiritually compromised. This rigidity is a real and current problem in conservative Christian circles, and it is the cause of much of the Christian-on-Christian friction that should not exist and that hinders the witness of the church.

The relational ethic across the third component is therefore this: *hold the certainties firmly, the strong inferences confidently, the prudential judgments charitably, and know which is which.* On the certainties, do not negotiate, even at the cost of friendship. On the strong inferences, hold confidently while recognizing that thoughtful Christians may emphasize different aspects of the same principles. On the prudential judgments, hold opinions but grant fellow Christians (and fellow citizens of good faith) wide liberty to reach different conclusions, and refuse to use disagreement on prudential matters as a litmus test of fidelity.

Rick's framework, on this account, mostly maps onto the third component rather than the first two. He is, as you note, not in equipoise on the deeper questions; he holds his cultural-conservative convictions firmly. What his framework adds is the relational discipline that conservative Christians often lack — the willingness to hold prudential judgments with appropriate humility, to extend curiosity to those who disagree, to refuse contempt as a tool of cultural combat. This is a contribution worth receiving, not a position to argue against.

## IV. The Christos Civitas Code as a Book

What I am sketching here could become the structure of an actual book — a Christos Civitas code of relational ethics that would serve both Christians within the fellowship and thoughtful natural-law conservatives like Rick who are reaching for similar conclusions through different warrant. The book would be in three parts, mirroring the three components articulated above.

**Part One: What We Confess and Why It Cannot Be Negotiated.** This part lays out the substantive convictions of the Christos Civitas position — the Christian and Constitutional commitments that orient the rest of the framework. It is unapologetic. The lordship of Christ. The Trinitarian God. The created order of marriage. The personhood of the unborn. The moral seriousness of work and property and charity. The constitutional ordering of liberty under law. The reality of moral truth that exists independent of consensus. The chapter for the Christian reader would ground each of these in scripture; the chapter for the natural-law reader would ground each of these in the converging witness of natural law and historical experience. The point of Part One is to refuse the contemporary pressure to perform agnosticism about settled questions. We confess. We do not negotiate.

**Part Two: How We Hold What We Confess.** This part is the harder discipline, and it is where Rick's diagnosis becomes a Christian and natural-law call. Curiosity becomes a virtue not because we are uncertain about the truth but because we are responsible to understand the people who disagree with us before we engage them. Fairness becomes a virtue not because all positions are equal but because every image-bearer deserves to have their actual position represented accurately rather than caricatured. Generosity of spirit becomes a virtue not because we are soft on the disagreement but because the disagreement is *with a person Christ died for* (or, in natural-law terms, with a fellow human being whose dignity is not contingent on his current opinions). Refusal of contempt becomes a discipline not because contempt is socially awkward but because contempt is theologically and humanly foreclosed. This is where Rick's chapter ideas — *The Seduction of Moral Superiority*, *Certainty as a Social Drug*, *Curiosity vs. Judgment*, *Boundaries Without Bitterness* — find their proper home, integrated into a larger framework that holds the convictions firm while disciplining the way the convictions are held.

**Part Three: How We Distinguish the Confessions from the Prudential Judgments.** This part is the internal correction most needed in current Christian conservative discourse. It would lay out the taxonomy described above — certainties / strong inferences / prudential judgments — with extensive worked examples drawn from current cultural and political controversies. The point of Part Three is to free conservative Christians from the rigidity of treating every political opinion as a doctrinal certainty, while preserving the firmness of actual doctrinal certainties that are sometimes elevated and sometimes flattened depending on which side of an internal debate is convenient. The discipline is consistency: we hold the certainties firmly *whether or not* it is culturally convenient, and we hold the prudential judgments charitably *whether or not* the friend reaches our conclusion on them.

A book in these three parts, written carefully and grounded in both the apostolic deposit and the natural-law tradition, would be useful for several constituencies that the contemporary publishing landscape is mostly failing to serve. It would be useful for Christians who are tired of being told that their convictions are themselves the problem, but who are also tired of the contempt-culture posture that has come to characterize too much conservative Christian discourse. It would be useful for natural-law conservatives like Rick who feel the same grief and are looking for a relational framework that does not require the dissolution of conviction. It would be useful for younger believers who are trying to figure out how to live faithfully in a culture that has come to treat orthodox Christianity as morally beyond the pale, without falling into the mirror-image contempt that has characterized too many of their elders.

Whether the book gets written is a separate question. The fellowship essay you are now reading is at least the first articulation of what such a book would do.

## V. Application to the Fellowship's Actual Situation

This essay is not abstract for us. The questions it engages are alive in conversations the fellowship is having right now, and three specific applications deserve to be named.

**The conversation with Leonard.** The systematic Snuffer-engagement corpus committed to the repository earlier today represents the substantive conviction-side of the relational ethic articulated above. Six essays totaling roughly thirty-five thousand words name plainly that the Snuffer framework rests on a foundation the apostolic deposit does not authorize, and that the closed door of the 2025 Philippines address is the destination toward which the front porch of 2017 was always leading. Component One — *hold the conviction firmly* — has been honored. The convictions are on the record. Component Two — *love the person more than you hold the position* — is the work that begins when the essays are done and the conversation with Leonard begins. Leonard is not the Snuffer movement. Leonard is a brother who has reached a conclusion I cannot follow him into, and who is reading the essays to see whether his conclusion can hold up under careful examination. The work is to keep showing up, to keep listening, to keep being present, and to trust that the Spirit will do what argument alone cannot do. Component Three — *distinguish the certainties from the prudential* — applies internally: the certainty is the apostolic-deposit gospel; the prudential question is how this specific friendship is held over time, and that question requires improvisation, prayer, and grace rather than another essay.

**The conversation with Mike Sherman.** Mike's *Meaning in Life* mandala system represents a different kind of conversation across deep difference. Mike is not in the Snuffer framework; he has been working out his own philosophical-religious synthesis for decades. The essay on the [three conversations at one table](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/CFE_christos_fellowship_essays/essays/260426-three_conversations_at_one_table.md) treated Mike's position with appropriate care. The relational ethic with Mike is, again, the three components: hold the Christian conviction firmly (Component One — Christ is Lord, the Trinitarian God is the only God, the apostolic deposit is the standard); love Mike fiercely as a brother who has been thinking carefully and seriously about the deepest questions for fifty years (Component Two — Mike is a person, not just a position to be argued against); and distinguish what is certain from what is prudential (Component Three — Mike's mandala may contain valuable insights even where its overall framework is mistaken; the integration question can be held with curiosity rather than anxiety).

**The conversations across political lines, including extended family.** Every member of this fellowship has people in their life on the other side of one or more political fault lines. Spouses, siblings, children, parents, in-laws, longtime friends. The pressure of the moment is enormous: to dissolve the conviction in order to keep the relationship, or to break the relationship in order to honor the conviction. The three-component framework refuses both options. We hold the conviction firmly — we do not pretend the personhood of the unborn is a matter of opinion, or that the collapse of marriage as a category is a neutral cultural development, or that the various other moral confusions of the moment are simply alternative perspectives. We love the person fiercely — the daughter who has reached a position we cannot endorse is still our daughter; the in-law who supports policies we believe are unjust is still our in-law; the longtime friend who has drifted into a different worldview is still the friend we loved before the drift began. We distinguish the certainties from the prudential — the moral truth about the unborn is not in the same category as the optimal mechanism of legal protection for the unborn, and the moral truth about marriage is not in the same category as the specific policy approach to navigating the cultural moment we now live in. We keep showing up. We tell the truth when we speak. We refuse contempt. We refuse pretended endorsement. We trust that over time, the witness of a life lived with both conviction and love will do what neither shouting nor silent submission can do.

This is what the Christos Civitas relational ethic looks like in practice. It is not easy. It costs something every day. It does not always work — some relationships will end no matter how carefully we hold them, because the other side decides to end them. But it is the right ethic, on Christian grounds and on natural-law grounds alike, and it is the ethic the fellowship is being called to live out in 2026.

## VI. Closing — and an Invitation to Rick

Rick, if you read this essay — and I hope you will — I want you to know that the response you are receiving here is not pushback against your diagnosis. The diagnosis is sound. The grief is appropriate. The proposed cures are humane. The book you are contemplating writing would be a useful book, and I hope you write it.

What this essay is doing is naming what I think your framework leaves implicit, which is the question of what to do when the disagreement is not merely a matter of perspective but a matter of conviction held firmly on grounds that are not negotiable. You yourself, as I read your work over the years, are not in equipoise on the deepest questions. You hold convictions firmly through the warrant of natural-law reasoning, careful logic, empirical evidence, and the cross-cultural moral intuitions that converge on certain truths because those truths are real. The framework I am sketching here is, I think, compatible with yours at the level of relational ethic — and where it differs is mostly at the level of warrant rather than at the level of conclusion.

For me as a Christian, the warrant is the apostolic deposit; for you as a thoughtful cultural conservative grounded in natural-law reasoning, the warrant is the structure of human flourishing as it actually plays out in observable consequence. The apostle Paul, in the second chapter of his letter to the Romans, names the natural-law witness explicitly: the Gentiles who do not have the written law of Moses *show the work of the law written in their hearts.* The natural-law warrant is, on Christian terms, the witness of *general revelation* — the moral structure that God has built into creation and that careful reasoning from observation can apprehend. When you reach the conclusion that the contempt-culture and the cancel-culture are both wrong, that fairness and curiosity are virtues, that human dignity is real and not a social construction, that disagreement should not collapse into contempt — you are reading the law written on the heart. I, as a Christian, read the same law, plus the further light that scripture and the apostolic tradition supply. We are not on opposite sides. We are reading the same moral grammar from different vantage points.

So write the book. Name the patterns. Speak the truth about the contempt-culturalists, the virtue-signalers, the rainy-day friends, the bully-betrayers. Refuse to mirror their contempt. And know that, on Christian grounds and on natural-law grounds alike, what you are calling people back to — fairness, thoughtfulness, curiosity, love that holds across disagreement — is real and is reachable, even in 2026, even in this country, even in conversations that have already become difficult.

To the fellowship: the Christos Civitas relational ethic is the framework within which we are now living and witnessing. It is harder than either the soft option (pretend to agree) or the hard option (reach for contempt). It requires us to hold the convictions firmly *and* to love the person fiercely *and* to know which of our positions are scriptural certainties and which are prudential judgments where reasonable people may differ. It is the apostolic posture. *Speaking the truth in love.* (Ephesians 4:15). *In meekness instructing those that oppose themselves; if God peradventure will give them repentance to the acknowledging of the truth* (2 Timothy 2:25). *Be ready always to give an answer to every man that asketh you a reason of the hope that is in you with meekness and fear* (1 Peter 3:15). All three apostolic verses hold the conviction *and* the relational ethic in the same breath. That is the model. That is what we are being called to.

Christ Himself, the only person ever to hold perfect conviction with perfect love, gave us the pattern. *Full of grace and truth* (John 1:14). Both, simultaneously, all the way down. We will fall short of the pattern every day, and the gospel covers our shortfall. But the pattern is the pattern, and we walk toward it.

Thomas
