The Honest Questions: A Fellowship Conversation on Why the Death and Resurrection Were Necessary

By: Thomas Lee Abshier, ND

Date: July 7, 2026

Fellowship Discussion Essay | 7 July 2026

And straightway the father of the child cried out, and said with tears, Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief. — Mark 9:24

Occasion. This morning Isak and I talked for two hours — nominally about the video project, actually about the thing under the video project. Isak has been working to turn our creation-and-atonement material into short films, and he ran into the wall every honest maker of such a film must hit: you cannot film what you cannot yet say in your own words. His confession was the most valuable sentence of the call: "I can say it, but I'm struggling with it... I believe, but I don't have enough answers for myself to make it a real gung-ho belief." That is not doubt to be scolded; that is Mark 9:24 in a young man's mouth, and it is precisely the condition of the audience the video exists for. So instead of hiding the objections, we spent two hours walking through them — and I told him at the end that his questions were themselves the raw material for a second round of videos, because every honest seeker carries the same list. This essay keeps that promise. Below is the register of his objections, stated at full strength; the answers as they came in the conversation, tightened; and then the coherent story the whole discussion assembles into — including one link the conversation itself left implicit, the necessity of the resurrection, which I supply at the end so the story closes.

I. The Register: Objections and Answers

O1 — "It doesn't feel fair. It doesn't feel earned." How does accepting somebody else's sacrifice wipe away my sin? I didn't do anything.

A1. The objection is correct in everything except its assumption about what the transaction is for. It does not feel earned because it is not earned — that, as Isak himself said mid-objection, seems to be the point. But the deeper correction is about the frame. We instinctively hear salvation as a performance review: my deeds weighed, someone else's credit oddly substituted, the books cooked in my favor. That picture deserves the discomfort it produces. The real situation is not about performance at all; it is about compatibility of natures. God's nature is purity that consumes impurity the way fire consumes paper — not as a grudge, not as a policy, but as what He is. "It's not personal," as I put it on the call; "it's all I can be." And our nature, unrenovated, is the paper. So the question the atonement answers is not "how do I get a passing grade?" but "how can a being like me exist at all in the presence of a Being like that?" Fairness objections belong to grading systems. This is a survival architecture. And once the frame shifts, the "unfairness" inverts: the only unearned thing in the story is that a way exists at all.

O2 — "How does a death two thousand years ago make any difference to my life, my sins, my relationship with God, right now?" It feels abstract — almost like a quantum thing, something happening once and applying backward and forward in time.

A2. Isak's physics instinct is better than he knew. The event is singular and its application is timeless because the Accountant is timeless. Walk the mechanism: the Son is the layer in which we live — in him we live, and move, and have our being (Acts 17:28) — and His covering is what stands between our impurity and the Father's consuming purity every day of our lives. At death the body is gone, the covering withdrawn, and we stand exposed with our character imprinted on us. What the cross put into history is the one event that generates infinite credit: the Builder of the layer stepped into His own world as a man, lived with no debt of His own, and was executed without cause — an unjust death in a universe whose Accountant is perfect, which therefore stands as a credit that can never be exhausted and never repeated. The death happened in 2,000-years-ago; the credit exists in the ledger of the timeless One, which is why it reaches backward to Abraham and forward to Isak without strain. Your sins were not forgiven two millennia early or two millennia late; they are cancelled at the throne, by the Advocate, out of an account that time cannot deplete. The distance that troubles us does not exist where the transaction clears.

O3 — "Everybody has it within themselves to do bad — evil people run countries and crime rings, and every evil thing that exists, exists within me too. So what distinguishes anyone?"

A3. Nothing distinguishes anyone — that is the doctrine, not the objection. All have sinned, and come short of the glory of God (Romans 3:23). We are animals: the animal nature wants what it wants, and there is no shame in the wiring — but we are animals given free will, and the gift changes our category. A creature that can choose between the animal and God is held to the accountability of the level it can choose — God's level. And here is the stake that most modern people never hear: the destiny in question is not harps and clouds but authority — what Jesus calls true riches, the word-command reality of the world of spirit, where you speak and substance moves. Give that authority to a character that is self-first and the result is havoc by declaration: "road be built" — and everyone in its path is ruined because you had a wild hair. The wolf among sheep that Isak described cannot be handed the keys to a universe that runs on speech. So the question was never "are you better than the tyrant?" It is: "can you be trusted with the powers of a son of God?" — and the honest answer, for every one of us as we stand, is no. That is the problem the whole plan exists to solve: not to sort the good animals from the bad ones, but to transform animals into beings fit for true riches.

O4 — "So is the benefit psychological? Accepting forgiveness so you stop carrying guilt and can live well?"

A4. Living well is real, and it is a side effect. The stakes are not living well; they are living at all. There is a condition that God is, and a condition that we are, and the two are incompatible in the way fire and paper are incompatible — no negotiation, no "on a better day He'd let it slide." The therapeutic gospel — Jesus as a technique for unburdening the conscience — is downstream of the real one and cannot survive without it, because a psychological comfort founded on nothing would be one more self-deception, and we have plenty. The comfort is real because the ontology is real: the account actually clears, the nature actually begins to change, and the peace that follows is the felt awareness of an accomplished fact — not the fact itself.

O5 — "What happens to those who don't accept it? Do they cease to exist? Is hell real? We know so little — it's referenced, but all we have is folklore imagery."

A5. Isak is right that the imagery in circulation is mostly folklore, and right that Scripture is sparer than the paintings. What we can say: the incompatibility is real, and its outcome is separation — Gehenna, the valley of burning outside the city, is Jesus's own image, and "depart from me" is His own sentence. Whether the fire's work is eternal conscious torment, destruction, or something our categories cannot hold, the fellowship should say what Scripture says and hold the rest with open hands. Two anchors keep the question from despair. First — and this came from a testimony Margo has cited, of a man shown his own judgment who could say only "it is completely fair" — whatever the outcome is, it will be fair, exactly and personally fair, because shall not the Judge of all the earth do right? (Genesis 18:25). Second, there are hints in Scripture — held quietly, I believe deliberately — of a reconciliation wider than our systems: hidden, I suspect, for the same reason the whole design is hidden, because a published escape clause would abort the very transformation the test exists to produce. We are not authorized to preach the hints as promises. We are authorized to trust the Judge.

O6 — "Then what about the hard cases? The pygmy who never heard. The honest intellectual who wanted to believe and couldn't. And the deathbed convert — Vito Corleone prays the sinner's prayer with his last breath while the lifelong servant dies the same hour. Both rise?"

A6. Both rise — and the modern instinct that something is wrong with that picture comes from flattening two things Scripture keeps distinct: entrance and inheritance. Entrance is by the credit alone; there is no second door. But authority in the Kingdom — the true riches of O3 — is granted commensurate with the character developed here: because thou hast been faithful in a very little, have thou authority over ten cities (Luke 19:17). Vito enters — and enters as an infant: cleansed, loved, and trusted with nothing, first in the old world and last in the new, with everything to learn. The lifelong servant enters into rule. The parable of the eleventh-hour laborers (Matthew 20) gives everyone the same denarius — life — and the parable of the pounds distributes the cities; both parables are true at once, and the apparent unfairness dissolves when you stop assuming heaven is a flat destination rather than a world with vocations. As for those who never heard, or could not honestly cross the line: their cases are judged by the Judge whose fairness the redeemed themselves will certify — Romans 2 says the Gentiles without the law are judged by the law written on their hearts — and our assignment is not to adjudicate the edge cases but to shrink them: go ye into all the world.

O7 — "Why the mystery, then? Why doesn't God just make it plain, instead of leaving everyone to argue for two millennia?"

A7. Because the mystery is load-bearing. Full certainty abolishes the very thing the design exists to produce. A God beheld in unanswerable clarity is obeyed the way gravity is obeyed — universally, instantly, and without a single act of love in it. So He gives enough light for the seeker and enough obscurity to keep the choice alive, and He hides the machinery for the same reason a good teacher does not print the answers in the margin: the working is the education. Even the hard doctrines are calibrated to this: as I told Isak, if the escape clauses were published, "I don't really have to try that hard" would abort the transformation in half the race. Isak saw it himself in real time: "there has to be a little bit of mystery, so that we try." And there is a mercy in this for the honest doubter — the standard is not total certainty but adequate certainty, certainty enough to act on, which is all faith has ever been: the evidence of things not seen (Hebrews 11:1). Nobody crosses this bridge with the archive in hand.

O8 — "Was the Father shielded from the beginning, or only after the fall? Who walked in the garden? Who appeared in the Old Testament?"

A8. The shielding is architectural, not remedial — older than the fall, built into creation's floor plan. The Father declared one Son, perfect and wholly Himself (and only one, because a perfect self-expression admits no different second — another "Son" identical in perfection would simply be the Son); the Son declared the world; and so from the first morning of creation, everything impure that would ever arise was already inside the Son's world, not the Father's. The fall did not create the veil; it made the veil necessary to us. And this answers the garden and the Old Testament in one stroke: no man hath seen God at any time; the only begotten Son... he hath declared him (John 1:18). The Presence that walked in Eden's cool of the day, that Abraham fed, that Jacob wrestled, that Moses saw pass by — every theophany is the Son, the visible of the invisible God (Colossians 1:15), meeting His creatures inside the world He made, because meeting the Father directly was never survivable and never the design.

O9 — "But Jesus operates in the world among evil — He was here, He's present now. How is the incompatibility real if God is walking around inside it?"

A9. Because the incompatibility is specifically the Father's, and the architecture is specifically degrees of separation. The Father's world contains one thing: the Son, who is pure. The Son's world contains everything else — pure and impure alike — and the Son can be present to impurity without contradiction because His world was built to hold the contest: light and darkness, wheat and tares, until the harvest. That is not a loophole; it is the entire design. The Son is the mediating layer in person — which is why no man cometh unto the Father, but by me (John 14:6) is a statement of architecture before it is a statement of religion.

O10 — "Once somebody accepts Jesus, is the shielding gone? Are we in direct communion with the Father now?"

A10. As long as we are in this body, we are shielded — the veil is our life support, and it stays on until death removes it. What changes at acceptance is not the architecture but the account and the occupancy: the credit is applied, and the Spirit — the sent presence of the Father and the Son — takes up residence within the innermost layer, which is as direct as communion gets on this side. And a sober word Isak should hear because the prosperity preachers will not say it: the first fruits vary. One person throws the stash in the fireplace and the addiction dies in the flame — I have heard that testimony first-hand; her husband fought the same battle for years afterward. Grace is not a vending machine, and promising specific miracles is playing God with someone else's hope. What is promised is the Advocate at the throne, the Spirit in the heart, and the slow certain rewriting of the character — which is the miracle the others were always pointing at.

O11 — "The animal nature, then — is it evil? If our appetites are the problem, why did God build them in?"

A11. The animal is good — this must be said loudly, because half of Christian history mumbled it. If we did not desire food, beauty, rest, and one another, life would be a cardboard cutout; it is the animal that makes the world tastable. The appetites are not the enemy; they are the instrument — both of the test and of the joy. Lust, anger, greed, pride, attachment: each has a proper execution — desire ordered to covenant, anger ordered to injustice, acquisition ordered to provision, pride ordered to workmanship, attachment ordered to faithfulness — and each, unseated from its order, becomes a god, and gods of that kind devour their worshippers. The diagnostic is insatiability: the rebel yell of more, more, more — and the discovery, made by every honest hedonist from Solomon down, that there is no satisfaction in more: he that loveth silver shall not be satisfied with silver (Ecclesiastes 5:10). Even the disciplines get reframed here: fasting is not holiness — it is will-training, a reset, the deliberate practice of feeling the appetite at full voice and saying "no, and I remain in command," so that the no is trained and ready in the hour it matters. And the East's solution — extinguish the appetites entirely and dissolve like a drop into the ocean — answers the wrong exam: you start with all God and end with all God, and the whole adventure of otherness was for nothing. The Bible's ending is the opposite of dissolution: glorified bodies, a new heaven and a new earth, appetite redeemed rather than amputated — the animal finally safe to enjoy, forever, because the character holding it can be trusted. World without end.

O12 — "I believe, but I can't repeat it back yet. I can say the story; I can't say it as my own. Is that enough?"

A12. It is exactly enough, because it is exactly what faith looks like mid-construction. Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief was answered with a miracle, not a rebuke. The demand for gung-ho certainty before commitment gets the order backward — understanding follows obedience more often than it precedes it (if any man will do his will, he shall know of the doctrine, John 7:17). Isak's own prescription at the end of the call was the correct one and I co-sign it as the fellowship's standing counsel to every honest doubter: write it out. Put the story in your own words; put the questions under the story; find where the links hold and where they don't. That is not a failure of faith — that is faith doing its homework, and it is precisely how this morning's conversation became tomorrow's videos.

O13 — "And the free-voucher problem: if acceptance is everything, why not pray the prayer and party hardy?"

A13. Because the prayer, honestly prayed, is not a voucher — it is an oath of Lordship, and the man who prays it as a voucher should reread the most frightening verses in the Gospels: Lord, Lord, did we not... and then will I profess unto them, I never knew you (Matthew 7:22–23). Savior-without-Lord is a category the New Testament does not contain. What acceptance opens is not retirement but apprenticeship: now the hard part starts — the moment-by-moment "what would my Lord do here, and I am His representative, so that is what I will do." And on the call, Isak and I stumbled into the wager that answers the cynic: live it fully, and if you are somehow wrong, the worst that happens is that you spent your life making earth a little more like heaven. On your deathbed you will not be crying, "I'm sorry I wasn't more selfish." The bet is asymmetric. Take it.

II. The Coherent Story

Now assemble the pieces into the single thread, because Isak's real complaint was not with any one answer — it was that the answers felt like an array of sentences from a story that haven't been put in order. Here is the order.

There is one God, pure beyond our categories — a purity that is not a preference but His existence itself, so that impurity in His presence is consumed as paper in fire, impersonally, necessarily. This God desired the one thing His perfection could not simply declare into being: love that is chosen — which requires creatures free to refuse Him, which requires creatures with appetites worth choosing over Him, which requires a world where His face is veiled enough that the choice stays real. But such creatures, freely choosing, will sin — and sinners cannot exist in His presence for one instant. So before the first morning, the architecture was laid: the Father declared one Son, perfect and wholly Himself; the Son declared the world and everything in it; and thus every impurity that would ever arise was born already inside the Son — shielded from the Father's fire not by distance but by design, a house within a house. Inside that shelter we live our animal lives, choose our loves, form our characters — a womb, a cocoon, a school for beings destined, if they will have it, for true riches: the authority of sons in a world that moves at a word, an authority that can be entrusted only to the utterly transformed. At death the shelter is withdrawn and the soul stands in the fire with nothing but what it has become — and what it has become is not enough; it never is; there is none righteous, no, not one. So the Builder of the shelter did the one thing that could balance a perfect ledger: He stepped into His own world as a true man, lived the only debtless life ever lived, and was executed without cause — and an unjust death of an infinite Person is a credit that cannot be exhausted, held by an Advocate with the authority to apply it to any account He deems willing. And here the story requires the resurrection, on pain of collapsing — the link our conversation assumed and never spoke: a dead substitute saves no one. The credit is worthless without a living Advocate to stand at the throne and apply it; the verdict on the debtless life had to be rendered in public, and the empty tomb is the verdict — death examined His account, found nothing, and was compelled by justice itself to release Him (Acts 2:24). If Christ be not raised, your faith is vain; ye are yet in your sins (1 Corinthians 15:17); He was raised again for our justification (Romans 4:25). The resurrection is not the happy ending appended to the atonement; it is the atonement's proof of solvency and its mechanism of delivery — the living Christ at the throne, saying of one ransomed animal after another: this one is mine. And the end of the story is not dissolution into light but a door opening onto a world — glorified bodies, a renewed earth, appetite redeemed, authority distributed to the faithful according to the characters they built in the school — the Father at last unveiled to children who can survive the sight, because they have been made like the Son. World without end.

III. To Isak — and to Every Honest Doubter in the Fellowship

One more thing from this morning belongs in the record, because it is the whole essay in a single image, and it is Isak's, not mine. He told me about his walk on the bluffs: a woman, sixty maybe, serious about her fitness, stooping on the trail to gather someone else's Modelo bottles, carrying them to the trash, and starting her hike — no audience, no signaling, no idea she was seen. And Isak, watching, said: "it felt like I was looking at God picking up bottles." Then, a little later on the same trail, he found himself stooping for a can — not thinking of her, just finding the impulse now native. That is the doctrine of this whole essay walking around in shoes. What is done to the least of these is done to Him; the love given to the world is love God Himself experiences; and the surest evidence that the credit has been applied to an account is that the account-holder starts, without ceremony, to clean up the Father's world — see a need, fill a need, faithful in the very little, being fitted quietly for the ten cities. Isak, you asked how a death two thousand years ago touches your life this morning. You answered it on the bluffs before we ever got on the phone: the same Spirit that raised the Substitute is teaching your hands to pick up bottles. The mechanism is working. Write your questions down — all of them, at full strength, as raw material for the films — and be at peace about the ones that stay open awhile. The father in Mark 9 did not have his theology in order either. He had a son he loved and a Lord he half-believed, and he brought the one to the other, and it was enough.

Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief.

— Thomas
