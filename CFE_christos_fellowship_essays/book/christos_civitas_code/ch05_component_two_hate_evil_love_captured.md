# Chapter 5: Component Two — Hating the Evil, Loving the Captured

This is the substantive heart of the book.

The previous chapter established that the believer holds his convictions firmly, in real spiritual warfare, as soldier of Christ holding the line of the apostolic deposit. This chapter takes up what that firmness is *toward*. Toward whom does the believer direct his hatred, and toward whom does he direct his love? The contemporary culture answers these questions in two flattened ways, both wrong, and the believer must reject both flattenings to do his work properly.

The proposition this chapter develops is that the Christian moral universe contains legitimate objects of hatred — principalities, powers, spiritual wickedness in high places, the false way, the doctrines of devils — and legitimate objects of love — every human being made in the image of God, including those captured by the things rightly hated. The discipline is keeping the hatred directed at the right objects (the spiritual evil) and the love directed at the right objects (the human persons), simultaneously, at full intensity, all the time. The collapse of either one into the other corrupts everything.

This is what *hate the sin, love the sinner* means as a serious theological proposition rather than a sentimental cliché. The cliché has been worn smooth by overuse, but the substance underneath it is exactly the discipline this chapter develops. The chapter has six movements. First, the scriptural witness that legitimate objects of hatred exist. Second, the proper objects: principalities, powers, false ways, the spiritual evil that captures. Third, the proper objects of love: human beings as image-bearers, including the captured. Fourth, the two contemporary flattenings (progressive and reactionary) and why both are theologically illiterate. Fifth, the connection back to Chapter 3's phenomenology — captured friend as the one through whom God Himself bears the captivity. Sixth, worked examples of the discipline applied to actual cases.

## Some things are to be hated

The contemporary cultural reflex, on hearing the word *hatred*, is to recoil. Hatred is what the bigots do. Hatred is what the cancel-culture left and the contempt-culture right do, in mirror image, and those should both be rejected. Hatred is corrosive, destructive, the opposite of love. To say *some things are to be hated* sounds, in the contemporary ear, like a slip back into the very pathology that the relational ethic is supposed to oppose.

But the apostolic deposit, and the broader scriptural witness, is unambiguous: some things are to be hated. The believer who hates nothing has misread the moral universe.

*Ye that love the LORD, hate evil: he preserveth the souls of his saints; he delivereth them out of the hand of the wicked* (Psalm 97:10). David is not making a literary flourish. He is commanding the lovers of God to hate evil, on the grounds that this is what loving God involves. The two attitudes — love of God and hatred of evil — are linked. To love God is to hate what God hates; to refuse to hate what God hates is to fail at loving God.

*Do not I hate them, O LORD, that hate thee? and am not I grieved with those that rise up against thee? I hate them with perfect hatred: I count them mine enemies* (Psalm 139:21-22). David's hatred of those who hate God is not contradicted in the New Testament; it is *redirected*. The hatred is properly aimed at the spiritual reality that opposes God, not at the human beings caught up in opposing Him — but the hatred itself remains. The fully-developed New Testament view, articulated by Paul in Ephesians 6:12, redirects David's hatred from human enemies to spiritual enemies, but it does not dissolve the hatred. The hatred is real. It is properly aimed.

*Abhor that which is evil; cleave to that which is good* (Romans 12:9). Paul is commanding the abhorrence of evil. The Greek word *apostygountes* is the strongest available term for revulsion. The Christian who is indifferent to evil rather than abhorring it has failed at the most basic level of moral seriousness. Indifference to evil is not love; it is its own corruption.

*Thou hatest all workers of iniquity. Thou shalt destroy them that speak leasing: the LORD will abhor the bloody and deceitful man* (Psalm 5:5-6). God Himself is named as hating, abhorring, destroying. The believer who imagines that love is the only attribute of God has read scripture selectively. God loves what is to be loved and hates what is to be hated, with both attitudes operating simultaneously and at full intensity. Sentimental Christianity, which knows only love, does not know the God of the Bible.

*Through thy precepts I get understanding: therefore I hate every false way* (Psalm 119:104). The hatred of every false way is the consequence of understanding the precepts. To understand truth is to hate falsehood. The two are not separable. The believer who claims to know the truth but is indifferent to falsehood has not actually understood the truth; understanding the truth produces, as its natural consequence, hatred of what contradicts the truth.

So the scriptural witness is consistent. Hatred is not always wrong. There are objects that are properly hated. The discipline is direction, not absence. The believer who hates nothing has not transcended hatred; he has failed to direct it at what deserves it.

## The proper objects of hatred

What is to be hated?

The apostolic deposit names the proper objects with precision. They are not human beings. They are the spiritual realities that operate behind and through human beings, capturing them and making them instruments of the spiritual realities' purposes.

*We wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this world, against spiritual wickedness in high places* (Ephesians 6:12). Paul is naming with precision who the enemy is. The enemy is not the flesh-and-blood person across the table, even when that person is currently saying or doing things that grieve God. The enemy is the spiritual reality that is using the flesh-and-blood person as its channel.

*The weapons of our warfare are not carnal, but mighty through God to the pulling down of strong holds; casting down imaginations, and every high thing that exalteth itself against the knowledge of God, and bringing into captivity every thought to the obedience of Christ* (2 Corinthians 10:4-5). The targets are *strongholds* (entrenched ideologies and false patterns of thought), *imaginations* (mental constructions that mislead), and *every high thing that exalteth itself against the knowledge of God* (any structure of thought or culture or institution that has set itself up against the truth). These are the proper targets. They are real. They are dangerous. They deserve the believer's hatred.

*Now the Spirit speaketh expressly, that in the latter times some shall depart from the faith, giving heed to seducing spirits, and doctrines of devils* (1 Timothy 4:1). *Seducing spirits* and *doctrines of devils* are real categories of spiritual operation. They are at work. They are seducing actual people away from the faith through actual doctrines that have spiritual origins. The believer who is indifferent to seducing spirits is not faithfully neutral; he is failing to recognize what is at work.

The list of proper objects of hatred therefore includes:

- **Principalities and powers** — the spiritual entities that operate as rulers of darkness in the present age. The biblical witness names them; the believer takes them seriously rather than demythologizing them away.
- **The false way** — every system of thought or pattern of life that has set itself up against the knowledge of God. Including the systems of thought that have captured contemporary academia, much of contemporary politics, much of contemporary culture.
- **Doctrines of devils** — teachings whose origin is not human invention but spiritual deception. The believer can recognize them by their fruit and by their structural opposition to the apostolic deposit.
- **Strongholds and imaginations** — the entrenched mental constructions that hold individuals and cultures in captivity. Ideological captures, conspiracy-theoretic captures, sexual-pattern captures, the captures of pride and bitterness and despair.
- **Seducing spirits** — the active spiritual agents that seduce believers away from the faith into something that masquerades as faith but is not.

These are the believer's enemies. Hating them is appropriate. Refusing to hate them is the believer's failure.

## The proper objects of love

What is to be loved?

The apostolic deposit names these targets with equal precision. The proper objects of love are human beings — every human being, including those currently captured by the things on the previous list.

*God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life* (John 3:16). The world here is the *kosmos*, the world of human beings in their fallenness. God's love is for the world He came to save. Not just the world of the already-righteous; the world as it actually is, including the captured.

*The Lord is not slack concerning his promise, as some men count slackness; but is longsuffering to us-ward, not willing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance* (2 Peter 3:9). The waiting of the present age is the patience of God toward those who have not yet repented. He is *not willing that any should perish*. The captured are not written off. The patience is real. The reaching is real.

*Let love be without dissimulation. Abhor that which is evil; cleave to that which is good* (Romans 12:9). The same verse that commands abhorrence of evil commands love without dissimulation. The two are not in tension; they are joint commands. Love human beings; hate evil. Both, simultaneously, all the time.

*Mercy rejoiceth against judgment* (James 2:13). The mercy is real. The judgment is real. The mercy does not dissolve the judgment, but mercy *rejoiceth against* it — the structural posture of God's economy is that mercy is the trajectory toward which justice has been bent through Christ. The captured human being is the object of mercy even when his captivity is the object of judgment.

The image-bearer status of every human being is non-negotiable. *So God created man in his own image, in the image of God created he him; male and female created he them* (Genesis 1:27). This is not a status that the human being can lose. He cannot lose it by being a sinner; all human beings are sinners and all retain the image. He cannot lose it by being captured by some destructive ideology; the image persists through the captivity. He cannot lose it by being our enemy; even our enemies bear the image. The image-bearer status forecloses contempt for any human being. Contempt for a person who is currently saying or doing things we believe are wrong is contempt for an image-bearer of God. The structural prohibition is absolute.

This is why love of the human being is the second component of the framework, alongside hatred of the spiritual evil. The two are joint requirements. The believer who hates the spiritual evil but does not love the human person captured by it has done half the work and corrupted the half he has done. The believer who loves the human person but does not hate the spiritual evil has done the other half and corrupted that half by being indifferent to what is destroying his beloved. The discipline is *both*, simultaneously, properly directed.

## The two contemporary flattenings

Most contemporary American Christians — and most contemporary American non-Christians who are arguing about Christianity — flatten the discipline in one of two directions, and the two flattenings are mirror images of the same theological illiteracy.

### The progressive flattening

The progressive flattening treats all hatred as illegitimate. *You should not hate anything; hatred is the problem; the failure to affirm what someone has chosen is itself a kind of hatred.* On this view, the only legitimate Christian posture is unconditional affirmation of every human being and everything they do. To name a behavior as sin, a pattern as captivity, an ideology as a lie, is to engage in *hate*, and Christians are not allowed to do that.

This flattening is theologically illiterate at every layer. It contradicts the explicit biblical commands to hate evil (Psalm 97:10, Romans 12:9). It misreads what love is, treating love as approval rather than as the willing of the beloved's actual good. It treats the captivity as freedom and the indifference to the captivity as compassion, which is a category error in two directions at once. It leaves the captured in their captivity in the name of respecting their autonomy, which abandons them.

The progressive flattening has substantial cultural reach. It has captured significant portions of mainline Protestantism and some Catholic and Evangelical sectors. It produces a Christianity that cannot resist anything because resistance has been redefined as hatred. It produces a Christianity that cannot rescue anyone because rescue has been redefined as paternalism. It produces a Christianity that has nothing distinctive to say to the surrounding culture because everything distinctive has been edited out as offensive.

The believer rejects this flattening. The hatred of evil is appropriate, even commanded. The naming of captivity is appropriate, even necessary. The willing of the beloved's actual good — including their good as God defines it, not as their current captivity defines it — is what love actually is.

### The reactionary flattening

The reactionary flattening, in mirror image, treats all hatred as legitimate when properly aimed at perceived enemies. *They are the enemy, and so they are the proper object of hatred; the cultural war is real and the people on the other side of it are the war.* On this view, the people captured by the cultural revolution — progressive friends, family members who have drifted, public figures who advocate for the captivity — become objects of contempt. The contempt is rationalized as appropriate hatred-of-evil; the people are conflated with the evil that has captured them.

This flattening is theologically illiterate in mirror image to the progressive one. It contradicts the explicit biblical witness that the enemy is not flesh and blood (Ephesians 6:12). It misreads who the captured are, treating them as the enemy rather than as the field on which the enemy operates. It dishonors the image-bearer status of the captured by treating them as objects of contempt rather than as objects of rescue. It produces a Christianity that fights *people* rather than *spiritual evil*, which is precisely the inversion the apostolic deposit forecloses.

The reactionary flattening has substantial cultural reach in contemporary American conservative Christianity. It produces a posture that confuses the ideological combat against bad ideas with personal combat against the people captured by those ideas. It produces a public witness that signals to the surrounding culture that Christians are angry at people rather than grieving with God over the captivities those people are in. It alienates exactly the people the gospel is trying to reach.

The believer rejects this flattening as well. The contempt for any human being — including the friend who has reached opposite conclusions on the great moral questions, the family member who has drifted, the public figure whose ideology is destructive — is forbidden by the image-bearer status of the human being. The hatred is properly directed at the ideology, the captivity, the spiritual evil that captures. The love is properly directed at the human being captured. Confusing the two is the reactionary's particular sin, and it is the sin contemporary conservative Christianity most needs to repent of.

### Both flattenings as failures of the same discipline

The progressive flattening and the reactionary flattening look like opposites. They are in fact the same failure — the failure to keep the hatred directed at evil and the love directed at the human person, simultaneously and at full intensity. The progressive flattening collapses the hatred into love, leaving no resistance to evil. The reactionary flattening collapses the love into hatred, treating the person as the evil. In both cases, the discipline of *both, properly directed* has been abandoned in favor of the easier monomovement of *one only*.

The Christos Civitas Code is the discipline of *both*. It is harder than either flattening. It costs more. It does not yield the satisfactions that either flattening yields — the progressive's feeling of being on the right side of history, the reactionary's feeling of being a warrior against the right enemy. It demands the harder, more sustained work of grieving over the captivity while loving the captured, of resisting the spiritual evil while pursuing the rescue of those it has captured. This is what the apostolic posture actually is.

## The phenomenology connection

Chapter 3 developed the framework in which God experiences His creatures from inside — omniscience as experiential indwelling rather than distant observation. The connection to the present chapter is direct and load-bearing.

If God indwells His creatures from inside, then the captured friend is the one through whom God Himself is bearing the captivity. The friend who has been seduced by an ideological cult, by a destructive sexual pattern, by a spiritual deception, by a contempt-culture political tribe — God is feeling the captivity from inside that friend, in real time, every day the captivity continues. The grief is not external to the friend; it is internal, located in the field of God's own experiential indwelling.

This sharpens what love of the captured friend actually is. Love is not optional kindness toward someone whose situation we happen to find regrettable. Love is participation in God's own grief over the captivity, the same grief God is bearing from inside the friend right now. The believer who turns away from the captured friend is turning away from a grief that God is currently bearing. The believer who refuses to name the captivity in honest love is refusing to participate in the rescue God is currently working.

This also sharpens what hatred of the spiritual evil is. The spiritual evil is the captor of the friend whom God is grieving over from inside. To hate the spiritual evil is to hate what is currently grieving God, what is currently holding the friend captive, what is currently the obstacle to the rescue God is working. The hatred is not abstract. It is hatred of the specific captor that has captured the specific person God loves and is reaching toward.

The two attitudes are not in tension because they are unified by the situation: God is loving the captured from inside and grieving over the captivity that holds them. The believer participates in both motions of God's own engagement. He loves what God is loving (the captured person, from inside) and hates what God is grieving over (the captivity that holds them). The unification is in God's own experiential field; the believer's participation is the alignment of his attitudes with God's.

## Worked examples

Abstract principle yields little; let us work through cases.

### The friend captured by ideological cult

Consider a friend, longtime, intelligent, formerly a thoughtful interlocutor on every question, who in the last several years has been captured by an ideological framework — let us say, for concreteness, a captivity to the contemporary progressive consensus on race, sexuality, and the meaning of America. The friend has come to believe that disagreeing with the consensus on any of these points is itself evidence of moral defect; that orthodox Christianity is, on these questions, a hate group whose teachings are dangerous; that anyone who voted for the wrong political candidates is participating in fascism. The conversations with the friend have become difficult. The mutual ground that used to exist has eroded. The friend now treats the believer's continued conviction as evidence of the believer's moral failure.

What does Component Two require?

Component Two requires the believer to hate the ideological framework that has captured the friend. This is not optional. The framework is a captivity. It is operating with spiritual force in the friend's life, walling him off from previous capacities for honest engagement, making him an instrument of the framework's own purposes. The framework is doing damage to the friend's relationships, the friend's intellectual life, the friend's openness to the gospel. Hatred of the framework is appropriate; the believer who feels nothing about what the framework is doing to the friend has not understood the situation.

Component Two also requires the believer to love the friend himself with full intensity. The friend has not become subhuman because he is captured. The image-bearer status persists through the captivity. The friend is still the friend, still loved, still pursued, still the subject of the believer's prayer and continued availability. Contempt for the friend, even when the friend is currently treating the believer with contempt, is forbidden. The believer holds out for the friend, prays for the friend, keeps showing up where he can, refuses to write the friend off, refuses to let the captivity be the last word.

The discipline is the simultaneous operation of both. The framework is hated; the friend is loved. The believer does not soften the hatred to keep the friendship comfortable (that would be the progressive flattening); he does not extend the hatred onto the friend (that would be the reactionary flattening). He keeps both directed at the right objects, and over time, in God's grace, the love-of-the-friend may be the channel through which the rescue-from-the-framework occurs.

### The family member captured by destructive sexual pattern

Consider a family member — a son, a daughter, a sibling — captured by a sexual pattern the believer believes is destructive. This is a particularly painful case because the family member often demands not merely tolerance but enthusiastic affirmation, and treats anything less than affirmation as personal rejection.

What does Component Two require?

The pattern is hated. Not because it is unfamiliar or inconvenient, but because it is a captivity that grieves God and that, lived out across years, will produce real damage in the life of the family member. The hatred is for what the pattern is doing to the beloved family member, not for the family member him- or herself.

The family member is loved. The image-bearer status persists. The family relationship persists. The believer continues to show up — at the holidays, at the major life events, at the moments of crisis when the family member needs someone. The believer does not pretend to affirm what the believer cannot affirm; pretended affirmation is its own form of contempt, treating the family member as someone whose actual good the believer cannot will. But the believer continues to love, in the form love has to take in this case: presence, prayer, honest non-affirmation that does not require the family member to perform conversion as the price of relationship.

This is hard. The family member often will not accept this posture. The cultural pressure is for either total affirmation (the progressive flattening) or total break (the reactionary flattening). The Christos Civitas posture is neither. It is the harder, sustained work of remaining present and loving across the disagreement, in hope that the love-without-affirmation will eventually create the space in which the captured one can hear the truth.

### The colleague captured by spiritual deception about ultimate reality

Consider a colleague who has built, over decades, a serious philosophical-religious framework that the believer cannot share — a New Age synthesis, a sophisticated atheism, a syncretic spirituality, a careful construction that does the work of a religion without confessing the Christian God. The colleague is not hostile; he is genuinely thoughtful, has earned the believer's respect on many other questions, and engages the believer's Christianity with curiosity rather than contempt.

What does Component Two require?

The framework, where it diverges from the apostolic deposit at structural points, is hated as falsehood. Not the colleague's intelligence, sincerity, or care — these are honored. But the framework itself, insofar as it tells the colleague that ultimate reality is something other than what the gospel says it is, is a captivity. It walls the colleague off from the actual gospel. It makes the colleague think the question is settled when the question is open. It is doing harm at the deepest level.

The colleague is loved as image-bearer, fellow-traveler in many other domains, brother in the search for truth even where the conclusions diverge. The believer remains in conversation. The believer listens carefully (Component Three will develop this). The believer continues to commend the gospel where openings appear, without performing aggression or contempt. The believer trusts that over time, the truth and the spirit may do work the believer cannot do.

### The cultural moment as a captivity

Consider, finally, the cultural moment we are all living through — the contemporary American moment in which spiritual warfare is operating openly through institutional channels. The redefinition of fundamental categories of human existence, the ideological capture of educational and corporate and governmental structures, the systematic production of contempt as the default mode of public discourse, the active resistance to the gospel in domains where the gospel was once welcomed. The believer hates all of this. Not the people captured by it — the millions of Americans who have absorbed the captivity through the channels by which captivity is currently distributed. But the captivity itself, the spiritual structures behind it, the principalities and powers that are operating through it.

The believer's posture toward the cultural moment is therefore: real hatred of the spiritual evil at work, real love for every human being captured by it, sustained witness in word and life that points toward the rescue, refusal to mirror the contempt-culture posture that the cultural moment is producing on every side. The believer is a soldier in real warfare, loving his neighbors who have become temporary inhabitants of the enemy's territory, refusing to confuse the neighbors with the enemy.

## The closing discipline

This is what Component Two of the Christos Civitas Code actually says. It is the substantive heart of the framework. The two attitudes — hatred of the spiritual evil, love of the captured human being — operate simultaneously, at full intensity, all the time. Neither softens the other; neither displaces the other. The discipline is keeping both directed at the right objects.

The contemporary culture wants to flatten this discipline into something easier. Either *love everyone, hate nothing* (the progressive flattening) or *hate the enemies, love only the allies* (the reactionary flattening). The Christos Civitas Code refuses both. It costs more. It demands more. It does not yield the satisfactions of either flattening. But it is what the apostolic deposit teaches, and it is what conscious participation in the Christos Civitas requires.

We turn next to Component Three, which is what makes Component Two operationally possible: listening as theological discipline, the diagnostic work of recognizing what is in the conversation before responding, and the four ways the response must vary based on what the listening reveals. That is the work of Chapter 6.
