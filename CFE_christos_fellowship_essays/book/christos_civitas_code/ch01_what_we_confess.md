# Chapter 1: What We Confess

A book about how to hold convictions has to begin by stating the convictions. The contemporary etiquette of moral discourse pressures the writer in the opposite direction — to perform a tone of inquiry, to gesture toward the convictions only obliquely, to apologize in advance for any reader who might be offended that convictions are being held at all. That etiquette is not Christian. The apostles did not perform inquiry; they confessed. *Whom say ye that I am? — Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God* (Matthew 16:15-16). Peter did not say *well, I see this from one perspective, others may differ.* He said the thing that was true and let it stand.

This book is built on the same posture. Before any of the relational-ethics framework can do its work, the framework needs ground to stand on, and the ground is what we confess. What follows is not exhaustive — no chapter could exhaust it — but it is the essential frame. Readers who do not share these convictions will find the book of intellectual interest but not of practical guidance. Readers who hold them will find the book is written for them.

## The lordship of Jesus Christ

Jesus of Nazareth — born of the virgin Mary, crucified under Pontius Pilate, raised on the third day, ascended to the right hand of the Father — is Lord. *Jesus is Lord* (Romans 10:9, 1 Corinthians 12:3, Philippians 2:11) is the original Christian confession, predating every creed and every theological system, and it remains the ground of the Christian life. To confess Jesus as Lord is to confess that He has authority over every domain of created life — over the individual, the family, the church, the nation, the cultural moment — and that no rival lordship may displace Him without lying.

This confession sets the foundational terms of every conversation a Christian has with the surrounding culture. If Jesus is Lord, then the cultural lordships that compete with His — the state as ultimate authority, the autonomous individual as ultimate authority, popular consensus as ultimate authority, scientific expertise as ultimate authority — are all secondary at best and rebellious at worst. The Christian does not hold convictions firmly because he is temperamentally rigid. He holds them firmly because the Lord he confesses has spoken, and the speaking has content, and the content makes claims on him that the surrounding culture's competing claims cannot displace.

## The Trinitarian God

The God whom Jesus called Father is one God, eternally subsisting in three persons: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, of one substance, equal in power and glory. The Trinity is not a philosophical accretion on a simpler biblical teaching. It is the church's faithful articulation of what scripture actually says when scripture is allowed to speak fully. *In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God* (John 1:1). *I and my Father are one* (John 10:30). *Go ye therefore, and teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost* (Matthew 28:19). The Trinity is what the apostolic deposit teaches when held together rather than flattened.

The unitarian alternatives — Arianism, Modalism, the various contemporary Mormon and Jehovah's Witness and unitarian-Pentecostal variations — all fail at the same point: they preserve one or another aspect of the biblical witness by sacrificing the rest. The Trinitarian articulation is the only confession that holds everything scripture says about Father, Son, and Holy Spirit without contradiction. It is therefore non-negotiable, and the historic creeds — Apostles', Nicene, Athanasian, Chalcedonian — are received as faithful witnesses to what scripture itself confesses.

## The full deity and full humanity of Christ

Jesus Christ is fully God and fully man, two natures in one person, without confusion, without change, without division, without separation. The Chalcedonian Definition (AD 451) articulates the boundary conditions within which any orthodox Christology must operate. The Christ who walked the dusty roads of Galilee was God the Son in human flesh, not a man adopted by God, not God appearing as a man, not a divine emanation, not a mode of the one God's self-presentation. He was the eternal Word incarnate, weeping at Lazarus's tomb and commanding the wind and the waves with the same authority.

The full humanity matters because what is not assumed is not redeemed; if Christ did not take on a fully human nature, He cannot redeem humans. The full deity matters because no merely human being could pay the price the Cross required. The two natures together, in one person, are what the gospel needs to be true.

## The bodily resurrection

On the third day after His crucifixion, Jesus Christ rose bodily from the dead. The resurrection is not a metaphor for the disciples' renewed spiritual energy. It is not a literary device. It is not a category mistake by first-century enthusiasts. The tomb was empty. The body that had been dead was alive again, transformed but continuous with the body that had been crucified. The disciples saw Him, touched Him, ate with Him, and were transformed by the encounter from cowering deniers into apostles willing to die for what they had witnessed.

If Christ is not raised, our faith is in vain (1 Corinthians 15:14). The whole structure of Christian life rests on the historicity of the resurrection. The apostolic deposit is not the record of a moral teacher whose followers gradually inflated him into divinity; it is the record of an actual encounter with an actual risen Lord by witnesses who staked their lives on what they had seen.

## The authority of scripture and the closed apostolic deposit

Scripture — the sixty-six books of the Old and New Testaments — is the inspired, inerrant, and authoritative Word of God. It is the sufficient rule of faith and life for the church and for the believer. The church does not stand over scripture; scripture stands over the church.

The apostolic deposit is closed. Jude commands that we *earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered unto the saints* (Jude 3) — *once*, in the Greek *hapax*, meaning *once for all*. Paul warns that if anyone, including an angel from heaven, preaches another gospel than the one preached by the apostles, that one is to be accursed (Galatians 1:8). The pattern of new revelation through new scripture closed with the close of the apostolic age. The Spirit continues to speak personally to believers in every generation, leading them deeper into the truth already deposited; but the *scripture* is fixed.

This confession forecloses every restoration claim that has emerged in the centuries since the apostles. Mormonism's Book of Mormon and Doctrine and Covenants, the Christian Science writings of Mary Baker Eddy, the Ellen White expansions of the Adventist tradition, the various Pentecostal-prophetic claims of contemporary movements, the Snuffer-restoration writings of the present moment — none of these can be received as scripture because the canon is closed. Whatever value they may contain as devotional reflection or historical record, they are not authoritative for Christian doctrine. The believer is not at liberty to add to the deposit.

## The reality of sin and the necessity of grace

Human beings are made in the image of God (Genesis 1:27) and are therefore precious beyond measure. They have also fallen — corrupted by sin from Adam down through every generation, including ours. The fall is not a metaphor for psychological maladjustment; it is the actual moral condition of the human race apart from grace. *All have sinned, and come short of the glory of God* (Romans 3:23). The image-bearer status and the fallenness are not in tension. They are the two facts that together explain why human life is what it is — capable of glory and of horror, of love and of brutality, of soaring beauty and of unspeakable evil, often in the same person within the same week.

Salvation is by grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone. No human being earns salvation by works. *For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: not of works, lest any man should boast* (Ephesians 2:8-9). The believer is justified by Christ's finished work, received through faith, applied by the Spirit, and lives the rest of his life out of gratitude rather than out of an attempt to earn what has already been freely given.

## The personhood of the unborn child from conception

From the moment of conception, the unborn child is a human person, made in the image of God, possessing the same intrinsic moral worth as any human being already born. This is not a matter on which reasonable Christians disagree. The biblical witness consistently treats the unborn as persons (Psalm 139:13-16, Jeremiah 1:5, Luke 1:41-44), and the embryological reality — that the new genome is established at fertilization, that development is continuous from conception to birth, that no morally significant transition can be located between conception and birth — confirms what scripture teaches.

The legal regime that has permitted, since *Roe v. Wade* and continuing in modified form since *Dobbs*, the routine killing of unborn children is a regime of mass injustice. The Christian cannot, in honesty, treat this as a matter of opinion on which fellow citizens of good faith may differ. It is a question of whether human persons are being killed by the millions for reasons of convenience. The conviction is firm because the truth is firm; the relational-ethics question of how to engage friends and family who disagree is the subject of later chapters, but the conviction itself is non-negotiable.

## The created order of marriage

Marriage is the lifelong, exclusive, covenantal union of one man and one woman, ordered toward mutual love, mutual sanctification, and the procreation and rearing of children. *Have ye not read, that he which made them at the beginning made them male and female, and said, For this cause shall a man leave father and mother, and shall cleave to his wife: and they twain shall be one flesh?* (Matthew 19:4-5). Christ's reading of Genesis 1-2 is decisive: the marriage union is what the creator-God made it to be at the beginning, and the cultural redefinitions of the last sixty years — no-fault divorce, same-sex civil unions, same-sex marriage, the increasing detachment of marriage from procreation and from sexual complementarity — do not change what marriage actually is.

The cultural pressure to perform agnosticism on this question is intense. Few orthodox Christian convictions cost more in contemporary American life. But the conviction is firm because the creator-God has spoken, and the speaking has content. The pastoral question of how to love individuals captured by patterns that the created order does not endorse is the subject of later chapters; the foundational confession is that marriage as the union of one man and one woman is what God made and what we receive as a gift.

## The moral seriousness of work, property, and charity

Human beings are made for productive work. *The Lord God took the man, and put him into the garden of Eden to dress it and to keep it* (Genesis 2:15) — work is given before the fall, not as punishment but as vocation. The dignity of work, the legitimacy of property as the fruit of work, and the obligation of charity to those who cannot work or whose work has not yet sustained them are the three pillars of a Christian economic ethic.

This ethic is in tension with both contemporary collectivist and contemporary libertarian extremes. Against the collectivist: property is legitimate, the seventh commandment forbids theft (Exodus 20:15), and the redistributive expropriation of property by state action is a form of theft. Against the libertarian: charity is required, the believer is his brother's keeper (Genesis 4:9 read inversely — Cain's *am I my brother's keeper?* is a lie), and the systematic neglect of the poor is a sin against the gospel (Matthew 25:31-46, James 2:14-17). The Christian economic ethic is the discipline of holding all three — work, property, charity — together, against every contemporary pressure to drop one in favor of the others.

## The constitutional ordering of liberty under law

Government exists by God's appointment to restrain evil, defend the innocent, and provide the conditions under which human flourishing is possible (Romans 13:1-7, 1 Peter 2:13-17). The American constitutional ordering — government of limited powers, separation of powers, the protection of natural and unalienable rights, the rule of law, federalism, the distinction between church and state — is not the only legitimate political order, but it is a strong inference from biblical principles and has been, in practice, the most successful structure for protecting human liberty under law that any nation has produced.

This conviction means that the Christian Constitutionalist takes the founding documents — the Declaration of Independence, the Constitution, the Bill of Rights — seriously, as a substantively good ordering that should be defended and restored where it has been eroded. This is not idolatry of the country (the nation is not the kingdom of God), but recognition that what the American founders achieved was, by the standards of human history, a remarkable approximation of biblical principles applied to political life. The contemporary erosion of constitutional ordering — through administrative-state expansion, through judicial supremacism, through the collapse of federalism, through the growing willingness to use government power to enforce ideological orthodoxy — is therefore something the Christian opposes.

## The reality of moral truth that exists independent of consensus

Moral truth exists. It is not constructed by human consensus, by majority vote, by cultural drift, or by the latest expert pronouncements. It is given by the creator-God who built moral order into the structure of reality, and is accessible through scripture (special revelation) and through the careful reading of the moral structure of human flourishing as it actually plays out in observable consequence (general revelation, the natural law).

The contemporary cultural assumption that moral claims are merely expressions of preference, that disagreement on moral questions is therefore disagreement on taste, that no one is in a position to say that any moral position is *true* — this assumption is not just wrong but corrosive. It dissolves the ground on which any serious moral conviction can stand. The Christian rejects it. There is moral truth. We have substantial access to it. The convictions named in this chapter are not opinions; they are recognitions of what is.

## Why the etiquette of performed uncertainty is not Christian

The convictions named above are firm. They are not held tentatively. The contemporary etiquette pressures the believer to soften them — to add hedges, to perform the appearance of inquiry, to apologize in advance for any reader who might be offended. This etiquette is not virtue. It is the eclipse of virtue. To pretend uncertainty about settled questions is to lie. To soften firm convictions for social comfort is to betray the convictions and the people they were given to serve.

Paul's posture is the posture: *I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ* (Romans 1:16). The believer is not ashamed because he is persuaded without apology that the gospel is true and the revelation speaks words of life to the world. Shame would be appropriate if the convictions were false, or if the creator-God were evil. But he sincerely believes in the goodness of God, the divinity of His works, and the perfection of His plan. If God's Law or works planted hidden seeds of evil, the shame would be justified. But the believer's conscience is clear, and the shame does not land.

The book that follows is built on the convictions of this chapter. The framework it develops — the four-component code of relational ethics — applies to those who hold these convictions and seek a way to live with them in a culture of morally reversed ethics. The next chapter takes up the second foundational question: *why is it legitimate for the believer to be confident that what we confess is what Christ Himself authorized?* That is the foundation Christ laid, and the apostolic deposit that rests upon it.
