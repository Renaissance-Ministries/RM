# Chapter 7: Component Four — Distinguishing Certainties, Strong Inferences, and Prudential Judgments

The fourth component of the Christos Civitas Code is the discipline that prevents the framework from collapsing into rigidity. It is the discipline of keeping straight which of the believer's positions are scriptural certainties (held with confidence proportional to the apostolic deposit's clarity), which are strong scriptural inferences (held confidently as applications of clear principles), and which are prudential judgments (held seriously but with the recognition that fellow Christians and citizens of good faith may reach different conclusions on the same evidence).

This component is the internal correction most needed in current conservative Christian discourse. The contempt-culture posture that has captured large portions of conservative Christianity in the last decade does not arise from the firmness of conviction (Component One); it arises from the failure to keep the categories straight. The believer who treats every position he holds with the same maximum confidence — whether the position is *Christ is risen* or *the optimal corporate tax rate is 21%* — has dissolved the distinctions that allow conviction to be firm without being rigid. He cannot tell the difference between a fellow Christian who confesses the gospel and disagrees with him on tax policy and a heretic who denies the gospel itself. He treats both as enemies because his epistemic taxonomy has flattened into a single category: *positions I hold, all of which are equally certain and any of which is grounds for treating those who disagree as compromised.*

This is rigidity, and it is the failure of Component Four. The chapter develops the taxonomy that prevents this failure, names what kinds of questions fall into each category, and develops the working procedure by which the believer can sort questions correctly when they arise.

The chapter has four movements. First, the discipline of keeping epistemic categories straight, and why it is harder than it sounds. Second, the three categories developed with extensive examples. Third, the dual failures of the soft Christian (treating certainties as prudential) and the hard Christian (treating prudential as certainties). Fourth, the working procedure for sorting questions in real time, with worked examples from contemporary cultural and political life.

## The discipline of keeping categories straight

Not all of the believer's positions occupy the same epistemic space. Some are direct confessions of the apostolic deposit, held with the confidence of scripture itself. Some are applications of clear scriptural principles where reasonable Christians have largely converged. Some are prudential judgments about how to apply broader principles to complex contemporary situations, where reasonable Christians of good faith reach genuinely different conclusions on the same evidence.

The discipline is keeping these distinct. The cowardice of the soft Christian is to flatten downward — to treat the certainties as prudential, so that the personhood of the unborn or the created order of marriage become matters of opinion on which thoughtful Christians may differ. The rigidity of the hard Christian is to flatten upward — to treat the prudential as certainties, so that any Christian who disagrees on tax policy or specific cultural-moment tactics becomes theologically suspect or spiritually compromised.

Both flattenings are failures of the same discipline. Both produce the same kind of corruption in the believer's relationships, even though the corruptions point in opposite directions. The soft flattening produces a Christianity that has nothing distinctive to say because everything has been negotiated down to the level of opinion. The hard flattening produces a Christianity that cannot maintain unity even within the household of faith because every prudential disagreement has been elevated to a doctrinal anathema.

The discipline is harder than it sounds. The temptation, on any question the believer feels strongly about, is to elevate the question to certainty status — *this is so important to me that disagreement on it cannot be in good faith.* The temptation is mostly unconscious. The believer does not notice he has elevated the question; he simply finds himself unable to extend charity to the person who has reached a different conclusion. The conversation becomes impossible because the believer has, without examining the move, treated his own prudential judgment as if it were the gospel.

The discipline therefore requires deliberate self-examination. *Is the question I am holding firmly a certainty, a strong inference, or a prudential judgment?* *On the basis of what evidence am I treating it as I am?* *Is the friend who has reached a different conclusion necessarily theologically compromised, or have I conflated my prudential judgment with the gospel?* These are uncomfortable questions, because honest answers often reveal that the believer has been treating prudential judgments as certainties without noticing. But the questions are necessary, and the discipline of asking them is what keeps the believer from collapsing into rigidity.

## The three categories

### Scriptural certainties

Scriptural certainties are the positions the apostolic deposit teaches clearly, the historic creedal traditions confess with one voice, and orthodox Christianity across centuries has held without significant variance. They are non-negotiable. They are not opinions. They are not perspectives. They are confessions of what the creator-God has done and said, and the believer holds them with the confidence of scripture itself.

The list is shorter than the contemporary culture-warring believer often supposes, but it is also more substantive than the soft Christian often allows. It includes:

- **The lordship of Jesus Christ.** Jesus is Lord. Every rival lordship — the state, the autonomous individual, popular consensus, scientific expertise — is secondary at best.
- **The Trinitarian God.** One God in three persons, of one substance, equal in power and glory. Every unitarian alternative fails the test of holding the whole biblical witness.
- **The full deity and full humanity of Christ.** The Chalcedonian boundaries hold. Every Christology that fails the boundaries fails.
- **The bodily resurrection.** The tomb was empty; the body that died was raised; the disciples were transformed by the encounter.
- **The authority of scripture and the closed apostolic deposit.** Sola Scriptura in the appropriate Protestant sense, or the equivalent recognition that scripture is the supreme authority in the Catholic and Orthodox traditions. The apostolic deposit is closed; new revelations claiming canonical authority are foreclosed.
- **The reality of sin and the necessity of grace.** All have sinned; salvation is by grace through faith in Christ.
- **The personhood of the unborn child from conception.** The biblical witness and the embryological reality both confirm; no morally significant transition can be located between conception and birth.
- **The created order of marriage.** One man and one woman, lifelong covenantal union, ordered toward procreation and mutual sanctification.
- **The image-bearer status of every human being.** Every human being, without exception, is made in the image of God and possesses intrinsic moral worth that cannot be lost.

Most of what the contemporary culture-warring conservative Christian is fighting about is not on this list. That is the point. The list is what the apostolic deposit teaches with sufficient clarity to merit the confidence of scripture. The list is what the believer holds as non-negotiable certainty.

### Strong scriptural inferences

Strong scriptural inferences are applications of clear scriptural principles where reasonable orthodox Christians have largely converged across substantial time periods, even though the inference itself is not directly stated in scripture in the form it is held. The believer holds these confidently, with appropriate space for fellow Christians who emphasize different aspects of the same principles.

Examples:

- **The general pattern of constitutional ordering.** Government of limited powers, separation of powers, the protection of natural rights, the rule of law, federalism, the distinction between church and state. This is a strong inference from biblical principles about the nature of human authority, the reality of sin, and the requirement of justice. Christians have reached largely converging conclusions on its wisdom, even though scripture does not specify it as such.
- **The general moral seriousness of family life.** The obligations of parents to children, the importance of intact marriages for child welfare, the distinct contributions of mothers and fathers to child rearing, the danger of family disintegration to the broader social order. These are strong inferences from biblical teaching on family.
- **The general pattern of fidelity in marriage and honesty in commerce.** The expectation that promises will be kept, contracts will be honored, deception in commercial dealings is sin, sexual fidelity within marriage is required. These are strong inferences with broad Christian consensus.
- **The general moral seriousness of work.** Work is good, idleness is corrupting, the worker deserves his wages, the able-bodied who refuse to work should not eat at others' expense (2 Thessalonians 3:10). These are strong inferences from biblical teaching on work.
- **The general pattern of charity toward the poor.** Christians are obligated to care for the poor; specific applications vary; but the general obligation is a strong inference.

Strong scriptural inferences are held confidently. The believer does not soften them into prudential judgments. But he recognizes that on the *applications* of these inferences to specific contemporary situations, fellow Christians may reach different conclusions, and the differences are usually about the application rather than about the inference itself.

### Prudential judgments

Prudential judgments are applications of broader principles to complex contemporary situations where reasonable Christians of good faith reach genuinely different conclusions on the same evidence. The believer holds opinions but grants fellow Christians wide liberty to reach different conclusions, and refuses to use disagreement on prudential matters as a litmus test of fidelity.

This category is large. It includes, among many other things:

- **The optimal tax rate** for funding the limited functions of government. The general inference that government should be limited and that confiscatory taxation is unjust does not yield a specific tax rate; the specific rate is a prudential judgment on which Christians of good faith differ.
- **The specific structure of immigration policy** that best balances justice, mercy, the rule of law, the protection of the citizen body, and the obligations to the stranger. Christians may reach different conclusions on the specific structure while agreeing on the underlying principles.
- **The specific approach to public schooling** that best serves families and the common good. Public schools, charter schools, private schools, homeschooling, hybrid approaches — Christians of good faith reach different conclusions, and the differences are often about which approach best fits a particular family in a particular community.
- **The specific economic policies** that best foster human flourishing while protecting the vulnerable. Free markets work; markets need regulation; specific tradeoffs between freedom and protection are prudential.
- **The specific foreign policy posture** that best fulfills the requirements of justice and prudence in a fallen world. Just war theory yields principles; their application to specific conflicts is prudential.
- **The specific approach to environmental stewardship** that best balances multiple legitimate goods. Christians reach different conclusions on climate policy, energy policy, conservation policy.
- **The specific cultural-moment tactics** in resistance to ongoing cultural decay. Christians may agree that the cultural decay is real while disagreeing on whether engagement, withdrawal, building of parallel institutions, or political organization is the best response in this moment.
- **The specific candidates** to support in any given election. Christians may share substantive policy commitments while reaching different judgments about which actual candidate, with which actual flaws and strengths, best serves those commitments in the specific election cycle.
- **The specific responses to specific cultural controversies** as they arise. Most of what generates contemporary Christian-on-Christian friction is at this level — disagreement about how to respond to a particular incident, a particular cultural moment, a particular institutional captures. The disagreements are usually prudential.

The believer who has done the work of Component Four can hold his prudential judgments seriously while extending wide charity to fellow Christians who reach different conclusions. His prudential judgments may be right; they may be wrong; he may revise them as more evidence comes in; he does not treat disagreement on them as evidence of theological compromise. The fellow Christian who has reached a different prudential conclusion is still a fellow Christian, still a brother, still part of the body, and the prudential disagreement is not a doctrinal break.

## The dual failures

The two failures Component Four prevents are mirror images of each other.

### The cowardice of the soft Christian

The soft Christian flattens downward. He treats the certainties as prudential — *who's to say what the apostolic deposit really teaches; reasonable Christians may differ; we shouldn't be too dogmatic.* On marriage, on the unborn, on the lordship of Christ, on the closed apostolic deposit — the soft Christian softens his position to keep the social comfort of the surrounding culture, and he tells himself the softening is humility.

It is not humility. It is cowardice. The apostolic deposit is what it is. Reasonable Christians do not differ on whether Christ is Lord, on whether marriage is the union of one man and one woman, on whether the unborn child is a person. These questions have been answered, in scripture and in the historic confession, with sufficient clarity that the answers are not in epistemic equipoise with their alternatives. To treat them as if they were is to lie about what the deposit teaches, and to abandon the captured friends and family members who need the truth more than they need our social comfort.

The soft Christian's flattening produces a Christianity that has nothing distinctive to say. It produces a Christianity that cannot rescue anyone because rescue requires truth, and the truth has been negotiated away. It produces a Christianity that is, finally, indistinguishable from the surrounding cultural progressivism with a thin Christian veneer. The veneer eventually comes off, because there is no substance underneath it.

### The rigidity of the hard Christian

The hard Christian flattens upward. He treats the prudential as certainties — *if you don't agree with me on this tax policy, this immigration approach, this cultural-moment tactic, you are theologically compromised; the gospel itself is at stake.* Every disagreement becomes a referendum on fidelity. Every prudential difference becomes evidence that the differing party has departed from the faith.

This is the contempt-culture posture from a different angle. It is what produces the phenomenon Rick Kirschner named — friendships thinning out, conversations becoming brittle, the loss of generosity of spirit even within the household of faith. The hard Christian cannot hold relationship with a fellow Christian who has reached a different prudential judgment because the hard Christian has converted the prudential judgment into a doctrinal certainty. The fellow Christian is not just wrong about a contestable application; he is on the wrong side of the gospel itself.

This is theologically illiterate. The apostolic deposit explicitly teaches that there are *disputable matters* on which Christians of different judgments are to extend charity to one another (Romans 14, 1 Corinthians 8). The disputable matters in the New Testament were specific — eating meat sacrificed to idols, observance of certain days — but the principle generalizes. Christians who agree on the gospel may disagree on its prudential application, and the disagreement is not grounds for breaking fellowship. *Him that is weak in the faith receive ye, but not to doubtful disputations* (Romans 14:1). *Let every man be fully persuaded in his own mind* (Romans 14:5). *Why dost thou judge thy brother?* (Romans 14:10). The apostolic posture toward Christian disagreement on disputable matters is wide charity. The hard Christian's posture is the opposite.

The hard Christian's flattening produces a Christianity that cannot maintain its own unity. It splits congregations over prudential disagreements. It alienates fellow Christians who would otherwise be allies in the substantive defense of the apostolic deposit. It signals to the watching world that Christians fight each other more readily than they fight the actual spiritual evil at work in the surrounding culture. The watching world draws appropriate conclusions about the credibility of the witness.

## The working procedure

How does the believer sort questions in real time? Several diagnostic questions can help.

**First: where in the apostolic deposit is the position taught?** If the position is taught directly in scripture (the lordship of Christ, the resurrection, the personhood of the unborn child as it can be argued from Psalm 139 and Luke 1), it is a certainty. If the position is a clear inference from teaching that is direct (the constitutional ordering as inference from teachings on authority and human sin), it is a strong inference. If the position is a contemporary application of broader principles to a specific situation that scripture does not address (a specific tax rate, a specific candidate), it is a prudential judgment.

**Second: what does the historic creedal tradition say?** If the position is in the Apostles' Creed, the Nicene Creed, the Chalcedonian Definition, or the major Reformation confessions, that is strong evidence it is a certainty. If the position has emerged in particular Christian traditions but not been universally confessed, it may be a strong inference but not a certainty. If the position has no creedal tradition behind it at all, it is unlikely to be a certainty.

**Third: do orthodox Christians of good faith disagree?** If across the spectrum of orthodox Christianity — Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant in its various forms — there is real disagreement among believers who hold the apostolic deposit, the question is probably not at the level of certainty. If the disagreement is wholly between orthodox and heterodox, the question may be a certainty after all. If the disagreement is within orthodoxy, the question is at most a strong inference and may be prudential.

**Fourth: what kind of evidence would change my mind?** If the answer is *no possible evidence — this is what scripture teaches and I confess it*, the position is functioning as a certainty. If the answer is *more empirical data, better arguments, observation of how policies work in practice*, the position is functioning as a strong inference or a prudential judgment. The honest answer to this question often reveals what category the believer is actually treating the question as.

**Fifth: am I treating fellow Christians who disagree as compromised?** If yes, and the question is genuinely a prudential matter, this is the rigidity of the hard Christian, and it is the failure Component Four is meant to prevent. The disagreeing fellow Christian is owed wide charity, not theological suspicion.

These diagnostic questions are not algorithmic. They require honest self-examination, and they sometimes produce uncomfortable answers. But they orient the believer toward keeping the categories straight and away from the dual failures that Component Four prevents.

## Worked examples

Let us work through some specific contemporary questions and sort them.

**Is abortion morally wrong?** *Certainty.* The personhood of the unborn child from conception is taught in scripture and confessed throughout the historic Christian tradition. The believer does not soften this.

**Should abortion be illegal?** *Strong inference, possibly approaching certainty.* If unborn children are persons, then their killing for reasons of convenience is a grave injustice, and the state's role in restraining grave injustice is a strong scriptural inference. Most orthodox Christians have reached this conclusion.

**Should the legal protection of unborn life be pursued through federal legislation, state legislation, constitutional amendment, or some combination?** *Prudential judgment.* Christians of good faith reach different conclusions about the best mechanism. The believer holds opinions, may argue for them vigorously, but does not treat fellow Christians who reach different conclusions as compromised.

**Is marriage the union of one man and one woman?** *Certainty.* Christ's reading of Genesis 1-2 in Matthew 19 is decisive; the historic Christian confession is unanimous; orthodox Christians do not differ on this.

**Should the state recognize only this form of marriage?** *Strong inference.* Most orthodox Christians have concluded that the state's marriage-recognition function should track the actual structure of marriage; some have concluded that disestablishment of state marriage is the better path; the disagreement is real but the underlying conviction about what marriage *is* is shared.

**Should Christian business owners be required to participate in same-sex weddings?** *Currently a contested question with both moral and legal dimensions; strong inference that the answer is no, on grounds of religious liberty and conscience protection; specific legal mechanisms by which to defend this are prudential.*

**Is the constitutional ordering of the United States substantively good and worth defending?** *Strong inference.* It is a strong inference from biblical principles applied to political life. Most orthodox Christians have reached this conclusion. Some have argued for different orderings; the disagreement is real but the inference from biblical principles to limited government and protected liberty is broad.

**What should the federal income tax rate be?** *Prudential.* No specific rate is a Christian doctrine. The believer may have strong opinions; he extends wide charity to Christians who differ.

**What should immigration policy look like?** *Prudential, with strong-inference elements.* The general principles — the rule of law, justice toward the stranger, the protection of the citizen body, the limits of state capacity — are strong inferences. The specific policies that best balance them are prudential, and Christians of good faith reach different conclusions.

**Should I support [specific political candidate]?** *Prudential.* No candidate is a Christian doctrine. Christians may share substantive policy commitments while reaching different judgments on which actual candidate, with which actual flaws and strengths, best serves them.

**Should we engage with the culture, withdraw from it into parallel institutions, or pursue some combination?** *Prudential.* Christians of good faith reach genuinely different conclusions, and the conclusion that fits any particular family or community depends on circumstances scripture does not directly address.

The pattern: the certainties are short, substantive, and non-negotiable. The strong inferences are broader and held confidently with charitable space for fellow Christians who emphasize different aspects. The prudential judgments are large in number and held with appropriate humility, even when held strongly.

The believer who can sort questions in this way avoids the dual failures. He does not soften the certainties to keep social comfort. He does not elevate the prudential to certainties to perform tribal loyalty. He holds the firmness where the firmness belongs, the confidence where the confidence belongs, the humility where the humility belongs. And his relationships with fellow Christians, fellow citizens, and friends across the lines of disagreement are stable across the categories — firm where firmness is appropriate, gentle where gentleness is appropriate, charitable where charity is appropriate.

## Closing

Component Four is the discipline that prevents the framework from collapsing into rigidity. Combined with the firmness of Component One, the directed hatred and love of Component Two, and the listening of Component Three, it produces a relational ethic that holds firm convictions without contempt, listens carefully without dissolving conviction, and distinguishes the certainties from the prudential without losing the certainties or weaponizing the prudential.

This is the Christos Civitas Code. The four components operate together. They do not compete; they constrain each other. The collapse of any one corrupts the others. All four together constitute the relational ethic of the believer engaged in real spiritual warfare, who hates what he should hate and loves what he should love and listens carefully to what is in the conversation and knows the difference between the gospel and his own preferred prudential applications.

We turn now to Part Three, the witness, which takes the framework into the lived situation of 2026 American Christian life. Chapter 8 develops the application — what the framework looks like in the specific conversations the fellowship is actually having, what it requires of believers in the broader cultural moment, and what it leaves to the Spirit and to time.
