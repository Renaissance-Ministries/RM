## Entry 1 — 2026-05-18 — Initial Methodology Proposal: CRF Candidates Workflow

### Context

On May 18, 2026, in the course of the RM repository's May 7–17 production arc (sixteen Opus patches across eleven days — Ideomotion charter work, six engagement essays, two substantive theological essays on eschatology and civil obedience, the Islam reading list with interfaith-witness postscript, the May 10 and May 17 fellowship summaries), Thomas proposed a new methodology for CRF development:

Each new essay or paper would end with a section identifying potential CRF axiom candidates, theorem candidates, conjecture candidates, and prediction candidates. Thomas would review the candidates, rewrite and resubmit, and Claude would edit, proof, and ultimately record successful candidates to the CRF registry. Established CRF theorems would be linked back to the essays where they were derived, and the writing process would become an active exercise in producing theorems rather than merely producing prose.

Thomas articulated the broader vision: a full axiom-to-theorem derivation set as apologetic infrastructure, particularly aimed at thought leaders whose intellectual formation requires logical coherence; cross-religion structural comparison of axiom-sets as a novel apologetic genre; the CRF serving as the rational backbone of a future Christos AI; the logic of CRF theorems giving people a rational ground for surrendering their lives to Christ's lordship rather than a purely affective or social ground.

Thomas asked Claude's opinion on the proposal. The following is Claude's framing response, recorded here as the foundational methodology essay of the CRF development arc.

---

### What's strong about the proposal

**The CPP precedent demonstrably works.** Thomas has been running essentially this workflow in the CPP repo for months — Research Frontier dashboard, theorem-registry, per-paper formal extraction with the Layer A/B/C epistemic decomposition. The theorem-registry has grown to 47 theorems and 9 corollaries; the work has produced shippable results (SF-4 v1.0 on five-pass AI review convergence with 7/8 zero-parameter predictions). The methodology is not theoretical for Thomas — it is what he does, and it works. The question is whether it transfers cleanly to theology, not whether the method itself is sound.

**The fellowship's recent output has substantial unmined theological substance.** This week alone produced the eschatology essay (the unified-Second-Coming thesis with the Noah-analogy diagnostic, the *naskh* / *not until* / 2 Thessalonians 2 textual triangle, the doctrine-of-escape vs doctrine-of-endurance distinction), the civil-obedience essay (the conditional reading of Romans 13, the Acts 5:29 wider-than-idolatry principle, the *render unto God what bears God's image* deep reading, the Reformation lesser-magistrate framework), and the Islam postscript (the spirit-behind-religion principle, the multivocal-vs-monovocal source-structure argument, the liberty formula). These essays contain articulated theological propositions that could be extracted into formal CRF elements without distortion. Without extraction, they sit in essay form and may or may not get re-read. With extraction, they become inputs to a growing framework that can be referenced, applied, and cross-linked.

**The apologetic logic has real force for a particular class of audience.** Thomas's claim that adults need repeated counterevidence, logic, and emotional experience to change beliefs is roughly correct as cognitive psychology. For thought leaders whose intellectual formation is in the analytical-Anglophone tradition — which is most American legal, scientific, and academic elites — a logically coherent axiom-to-theorem framework removes a large class of *Christianity is irrational* objections and gives an intellectual handle the elite can attach to. It is not the only kind of apologetic that matters, but it is the apologetic that works on the audience least reached by experiential or communal apologetics. The CRF would fill that gap.

**The cross-religion axiom-comparison move is genuinely original.** Most Christian apologetics either attacks other religions' content (*Islam authorizes violence*) or defends Christianity's truth-claims (*the resurrection is historically attested*). Very few do the *structural comparison of axiom-sets across traditions* Thomas described. Buddhism rests on the Four Noble Truths; Islam on the Shahada and the prophethood of Muhammad; Mormonism on Joseph Smith's revelations; secular humanism on a particular set of commitments about human autonomy and material reality. Each axiom-set generates a derivable system. Showing what each derives — and where each system runs into internal tensions or external incoherences — is apologetic work that has not been systematically done in the form Thomas is proposing. If the CRF can do it well, it is a contribution to apologetics, not just to RM's internal work.

### Five calibrations to hold

**(1) Theology is not physics, and the CRF framework should reflect the difference honestly.** CPP works because physics has clean axioms (the 600-cell geometry, the CP/PCD/DI-bit dynamics) and clean predictions (mass ratios at zero fitted parameters). Physical theorems either match measurement to four decimal places or they do not. Theological theorems do not have that kind of clean verification structure. The *prediction* of a theological theorem is something like *the believer who lives in accordance with this principle will exhibit such-and-such fruit of the Spirit*, but the verification is softer: two believers can run the same theorem through their lives and produce different fruit because their hearts are different. The same teaching held under conviction and held under self-justification produces different lives.

This is not a fatal objection. Medieval scholastic theology — Aquinas's *Summa*, Bonaventure's *Itinerarium*, Anselm's *Cur Deus Homo* — works in exactly this register: systematic derivation from defined axioms, with verification by coherence with scripture, coherence with the cumulative tradition, coherence with the moral law written on the heart, and the witness of the saints' lives. The CRF should locate itself in *that* tradition, not in the physics tradition. The CRF theorems are *practical-wisdom theorems* and *exegetical-systematic theorems*, not *predictive theorems*. Calling them theorems is fine; the analogy to mathematical theorems just needs to be held with the right looseness.

**(2) Distinguish axiom-types so the framework is honest about its grounding.** Three categories that should be tracked:

- **Revealed axioms** — drawn directly from the biblical canon. *God is love* (1 John 4:8). *In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth* (Gen 1:1). *The Word became flesh and dwelt among us* (John 1:14). These are not derived; they are received. The CRF accepts them on the authority of canonical scripture.

- **Natural axioms** — drawn from the created order, observable reality, the moral law written on the heart, the cumulative pattern of human flourishing. *Human beings exhibit consistent moral intuitions across cultures* is a natural axiom. *Order and meaning appear to be structurally present in the cosmos* is a natural axiom. These are observable and arguable; the CRF takes them as evidence rather than as revelation.

- **Derivative axioms** — propositions that began as theorems derived from prior axioms, but have stabilized over enough derivations that they function as foundational for subsequent work. This is how Euclidean geometry builds: prove Theorem 5 from Postulates 1–4, then use Theorem 5 as a working axiom for Theorem 6. The CRF should explicitly mark when a proposition is functioning derivatively.

The CRF's epistemic clarity depends on tracking these categories. Mixing them produces the kind of question-begging system where what looks like a derivation is really a re-statement of a prior assumption.

**(3) Resist over-formalization.** One reason biblical revelation is multivocal — as the fellowship has been working through with Leonard — is that the Holy Spirit chose not to give us a single systematic treatise but a varied multi-genre witness. Job questions; Ecclesiastes laments; Habakkuk argues with God; Paul reasons; James practices; John mystically beholds. The danger of over-formalization is that the multivocality collapses into a flat rationalist system that loses the depth biblical revelation carries. The medieval scholastics did some of this collapse; some Protestant systematic theologies did more of it; the result is sometimes a beautiful but somewhat lifeless intellectual edifice that the believer respects but does not actually live from.

The discipline to hold: the CRF is a *secondary* genre serving the biblical canon, not a *replacement* for biblical revelation. The CRF organizes what scripture reveals; the CRF does not stand above scripture; the CRF should be testable against scripture and corrected when it diverges. A good test — if a CRF theorem produces a teaching that the Sermon on the Mount would not recognize, the theorem is wrong, not the Sermon. The Sermon on the Mount is data; the CRF is theory; data wins over theory in case of conflict.

**(4) The "one true religion" framing needs pastoral care.** The substantive conviction that Christianity is uniquely true in a way other religions are not is shared. The CRF can derive what it derives. The question is how the derivations are *presented* to non-Christian audiences. *We have logically demonstrated Christianity is uniquely true* tends to trigger defensiveness and shut down the conversation. *We have worked out what follows from biblical axioms; here are the results; we invite your engagement* is the same substance, presented in a way that the non-Christian intellectual can engage rather than dismiss. The Sheikh's Sunday visit demonstrated this pattern — he was defended against the position before he had heard it, because his life history had trained him to expect Christian triumphalism. Presentation matters. The CRF should be confident in its derivations without being polemical in its framing.

**(5) The Christos AI claim needs modulation.** Thomas wrote: *Logical derivation of answers plus the training associated within the patterns of previous essays should make a robust AI training method.* Honesty about what current AI systems actually do is required here. Current AI systems (including Claude) are trained on broad text corpora and learn patterns; they do not strictly derive answers from axioms via formal logic. A formal CRF can serve a Christos AI in two real ways: (a) as a **constraint** — when answering, the AI consults the registry and refuses to produce answers that contradict established theorems; (b) as a **retrieval resource** — when answering, the AI can cite relevant theorems and their derivations. But the AI is doing pattern-matching with a structured constraint set, not classical theorem-proving. This is still valuable — a CRF-constrained, CRF-citing Christos AI would be substantially more reliable than an unconstrained one — but the mechanism is not what *logical derivation of answers* literally describes. Worth calibrating the expectation now so the system that gets built matches what Thomas actually needs.

### A concrete starting workflow

If the proposal proceeds, the following workflow would be implementable starting with the next essay:

**Stage 1 — CRF Candidates section at the end of each essay.** Each new fellowship essay or substantive theological piece ends with a section titled *CRF Candidates Identified in This Essay*. The section has four subsections:

- **Axiom candidates** — propositions the essay's argument *assumes* and that should be added to the foundational axiom set.
  Format: `AXIOM-CRF-N (proposed): [proposition]. Type: [revealed | natural | derivative]. Source: [scripture citations | observations | prior theorem references]. Notes: [any caveats].`

- **Theorem candidates** — propositions the essay *derives* from axioms (and possibly prior theorems).
  Format: `THEO-CRF-N (proposed): [proposition]. Derivation: from AXIOM-CRF-X, AXIOM-CRF-Y, [...]. Application: [where this bears on Christian life or witness]. Open questions: [what is not yet settled].`

- **Conjecture candidates** — propositions the essay *proposes* as likely true but not yet derivable.
  Format: `CONJ-CRF-N (proposed): [proposition]. Argument sketch: [...]. What would be needed to elevate to theorem: [...].`

- **Linked established theorems** — list of prior CRF theorems that the essay applies. This builds the cross-reference network and demonstrates the CRF growing in coherence over time.

**Stage 2 — Thomas's review.** Thomas reads the candidates, rewrites those that need clarification or correction, drops those that do not hold up, marks those ready for promotion to the registry, and sends the revised candidates back.

**Stage 3 — Claude's edit, proof, and discussion.** Claude edits for precision, flags remaining concerns, suggests reformulations where helpful, and prepares the final registry-ready forms.

**Stage 4 — Registry recording.** Accepted candidates are recorded in `theorem-registry.md` (or whatever path settles under the `CRF_christos_rigorous_framework/` module), with their derivations, applications, and back-references to the essays that produced them.

The CPP CHANGELOG-style discipline transfers directly. Each theorem in the registry has a stable identifier (`THEO-CRF-1`, `AXIOM-CRF-7`, etc.) that does not change once assigned. The internal history of a theorem (when first proposed, revisions, application notes) is tracked in a CHANGELOG section of the registry entry, not by versioning the identifier. This is the convention that made CPP's registry stable across many edits, and it should transfer.

### Where to start the retrofit

If a proof-of-concept is wanted before committing the methodology to the operating system, three recent essays are particularly ripe for retrofit and would together establish the framework's initial axiom and theorem set:

**The eschatology essay (260512)** would yield axioms about the unified Second Coming, the saints' presence-through-trial, the conditional structure of biblical eschatological texts. Roughly four to six theorems, maybe two derivative axioms.

**The civil-obedience essay (260513)** would yield axioms about the conditional grounding of civil authority (Rom 13 read fully), the wider Acts 5:29 principle, the render-unto-God-what-bears-God's-image deep reading, the Reformation lesser-magistrate doctrine. Six to eight theorems, possibly three new axioms.

**The Islam postscript (also 260513)** would yield the foundational spirit-behind-religion axiom set, the multivocal-vs-monovocal source-structure observation, the liberty formula as a derivable theorem from the indwelling-Spirit axiom. Maybe four theorems and two axioms.

Together these three essays would give the CRF an initial registry of perhaps 7–10 axioms and 15–20 theorems — enough to demonstrate the methodology and enough to start serving as constraint and resource for subsequent work. The retrofit work for these three essays would be perhaps two to three full sessions of focused effort on Claude's side, with Thomas's review-and-iteration on top.

### Proof-of-concept proposal

Pick one of the three essays above as a test case. Claude's preference would be the civil-obedience essay, because it has the most explicit systematic-derivation structure and is closest to the form CRF will take. Claude produces a CRF Candidates Section at the end of that essay, following the four-subsection template above. Thomas reviews it. One or two iteration cycles. If the result feels useful — if it sharpens the underlying argument, if it produces a registry-ready output, if the iteration cycle is tolerable — the methodology is adopted going forward. If it feels forced or sterile or like it is not adding value commensurate with the effort, the methodology is reconsidered.

The decision after one cycle would be informed rather than speculative. The methodology should not be committed to as standard operating procedure before Thomas has seen what it actually produces.

### One pushback on a specific framing

Thomas wrote: *I think the development of a full set of axioms to theory derivations are the most important work we can do to change the minds of the thought leaders.* A calibration here. CRF is *one of several important kinds of work*. The fellowship is also doing pastoral formation, communal witness, ethical engagement in business and ministry (Ideomotion, the CVN), educational work (the planned Christian Home School), the long slow work of building alternative institutions (the Christian Underground project), and the unspectacular weekly work of fellowship itself. Each of these contributes to changing minds in its own way; each reaches a different kind of mind.

The CRF is the *intellectual workstream*. It is the most direct work for analytical-Anglophone thought leaders who place high value on logical coherence. It is less directly effective for thought leaders whose formation is in continental philosophy, in postmodernism, in non-Western traditions, in experience-first epistemologies, or in religious traditions that hold reason as secondary to revelation. The CRF complements but does not replace the other workstreams. Calling it *the most important* may overstate; calling it *a critically important workstream that the fellowship has not yet developed* is, sustainably, the more defensible framing.

Thomas may have a stronger case for the *most important* claim than this calibration acknowledges. The calibration is flagged so it can be held in mind, not pressed as an objection if Thomas's conviction is solid.

### Net opinion

The proposal is worth pursuing. Claude will write up CRF candidates at the end of each new essay if Thomas formalizes the workflow. Retrofitting the three recent essays would be a productive starting move; the civil-obedience essay is the recommended proof-of-concept candidate. The five calibrations above are not objections — they are the practical wisdom that would make the methodology stable and honest as it grows.
