Myth, Fact, and the Unity of Truth:
A Christian Reflection on the Peterson-Dawkins Debate

## Introduction: Two Kinds of Minds

In a recent dialogue moderated by Alex O'Connor, Jordan Peterson and Richard Dawkins engaged in one of the most substantive public conversations about the relationship between science and religion, fact and myth, that we have seen in years. The exchange was civil, exploratory, and genuinely illuminating—not because the participants reached agreement, but because they articulated their differences with unusual clarity.

#### Jordan Peterson

Clinical psychologist and author of *We Who Wrestle with God*, Peterson approaches biblical texts as repositories of profound psychological and archetypal truth that have been refined across millennia to match the contours of human memory and motivation.

#### Richard Dawkins

Evolutionary biologist and author of *The Selfish Gene* and *The God Delusion*, Dawkins approaches the world as a scientist interested in facts—things that are true regardless of whether humans exist to observe them.

The debate crystallized around a single accusation Dawkins leveled at Peterson:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"Dr. Peterson, you're drunk on symbols. What I care about is the truth value. I see no truth value in the claims of Christianity—the virgin birth, the resurrection. Do you believe in any of those?"

Peterson's response was characteristically indirect—not because he was evading the question, but because he genuinely believes the question itself reflects a category error:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"I think that Jordan prioritizes myth and I prioritize fact. I'm not interested in dragons. I'm interested in reality... But my sense is that those two pathways have to unify. Now it's not like I know how to rectify that."

This essay will explore the key moments of the debate, identify the genuine points of convergence and divergence, and offer a Christian reflection on what both men got right—and what both may have missed.

✦ ✦

## Part I: The "Cultural Christian" Confession

Peterson pressed Dawkins on a statement he had made in an earlier interview: that he considered himself a "cultural Christian." What did he mean by that?

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"Virtually nothing. I meant no more than that I'm brought up in a Christian culture. I went to Christian schools. I therefore know my way around the Bible. I know my way around the Book of Common Prayer. I know the hymns. That's all. I don't value Christianity as a truth system at all."

But Peterson wasn't satisfied. He pushed further:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"Do you think that there are any marked differences between cultural traditions that would enable you to rank order them in terms of their ethical validity? For example, we could contrast mainstream UK Christianity with Islamic fundamentalism..."

Dawkins conceded the point:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"There is a hierarchy... In the case of Islam, I dislike any religion which punishes apostasy with death, that throws gay people off high buildings, that practices clitoridectomy... that seems to me to place Islam on a lower level than Christianity. But that's not to say anything very positive about Christianity."

Peterson's response cut to the heart of the matter:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"It might be to say something positive about Christianity... You might ask yourself, what did Christianity get right that led it away from those particular presumptions and towards something that you regard as more ethically appropriate? This isn't a trivial question."

**The Key Insight:** Dawkins acknowledges that Christianity has produced a more humane culture than some alternatives. But he refuses to attribute this to any truth in Christian claims. Peterson argues that the ethical fruit cannot be separated from the theological root—that Christianity's comparative moral success is evidence that it has grasped something true about reality.

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part II: Did Cain Exist? The Literalist Challenge

The most heated exchange came when Dawkins pressed Peterson on the historical reality of biblical figures. He had counted 356 references to Cain in Peterson's book *We Who Wrestle with God*:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"You're obsessed with Cain because Cain is symbolic of evil... You don't believe Cain actually existed, I presume?"

Peterson's answer reveals the gulf between their approaches:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"I think the pattern that Cain represents is an eternal pattern... I think of Cain as emblematic of the patterns of conflict that rip people apart in the world of history."

Dawkins pounced:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"That's different. There are Cain types who exist. But Cain himself—did he exist, or did he not exist? I care about facts."

Peterson offered an analogy that illuminates his position:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"It's a silly question. It's like asking whether Raskolnikov existed in Crime and Punishment. You can say, well, there was no such specific person as Raskolnikov. But that's not a helpful question, because the reason that Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment is a masterpiece is because Raskolnikov was everywhere in Russia when Dostoevsky wrote Crime and Punishment. Raskolnikov is hyper-real, not not-real."

#### The Fundamental Disagreement

Dawkins sees "Did Cain exist?" as a simple factual question with a yes-or-no answer. Peterson sees it as a category error—like asking whether "predator" exists when what you mean is "are there lions and tigers?"
For Dawkins, truth means correspondence with historical and physical fact. For Peterson, truth also includes patterns that recur eternally in human experience—patterns that are "hyper-real" precisely because they manifest in countless individuals across millennia.

The same tension emerged when Dawkins pressed Peterson on the virgin birth and resurrection. Peterson's response was to acknowledge mystery:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"There are elements of the text that I don't feel qualified to comment on. My experience has been that the more deeply I look into these texts, the more I learn. That doesn't mean I can proclaim full knowledge of what the texts proclaim."

Dawkins found this evasive. But Peterson was making a serious epistemological point: some questions cannot be answered in the terms in which they are asked. The virgin birth may be a fact in the Dawkins sense, or it may not be—but its *meaning* is independent of that question, and the meaning is what Peterson cares about.

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part III: Memes and Archetypes—A Surprising Convergence

The most productive moment in the debate came when moderator Alex O'Connor asked about the relationship between Dawkins' concept of "memes" and Jung's concept of "archetypes."

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"A meme is a virus of the mind. It's something that spreads because it spreads, because it spreads. It's something that spreads by imitation... An archetype is quite different, because an archetype is something which all humans have as a virtue of being human, something that's built in. So it's not something that spreads as an epidemic. It's something that we all have anyway."

Peterson offered a way to connect the two:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"Things catch because they have an emotional resonance. They attract people's interest... As the idea is more related to the action of underlying biological motivational structures, it becomes more and more expression of something that's instinctual and archetypal. Jung defined an archetype essentially as the manifestation of an instinct in image and then also in behavior."

Dawkins was intrigued:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"The idea that the archetype could be a reason why some memes spread—that seems to me to be plausible, if you believe in archetypes at all."

### The Dragon as Abstracted Predator

Peterson then offered a striking example: the dragon. Why do dragons appear in virtually every human culture, despite being fictional?

JORDAN PETERSON:

"Is there such a thing as a predator? Of course. What's the category of predator? Bear, eagle, if you're a primate—fire. Is there a worse predator than a serpentine, flying, fire-breathing reptile? Is that not the imagistic equivalent of 'predator'? So in what way, if predator is real, isn't dragon real?"

Dawkins was unmoved:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"I don't know why you say dragon. We have lions, we have tigers, we have saber-tooths..."

But then came the breakthrough. Peterson introduced the Baldwin effect—a biological mechanism by which learned behaviors can become genetically encoded over generations:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"Could you imagine a scenario where a meme had sufficient functional, adaptive significance so that the individuals who acted it out gained a reproductive edge?"

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"Yes."

JORDAN PETERSON:

"So then you could imagine a situation where there was a Baldwin effect between the meme and the genome?"

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"Yes."

This led to a remarkable moment of agreement:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"That's very interesting—that suggests that Jungian archetypes might be genetically assimilated via the Baldwin effect."

**The Convergence:** Dawkins, the materialist, acknowledged that archetypes—deeply embedded patterns of human psychology—might have a biological basis through the Baldwin effect. Peterson's "mythological" approach and Dawkins' "scientific" approach had found common ground in evolutionary biology.

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part IV: The Bronze Serpent and the Resurrection

Perhaps the most remarkable moment in the debate came when Peterson attempted to explain what he finds meaningful in the resurrection by way of an Old Testament story that Christ himself invoked.
In John 3:14-15, Jesus says: "And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up: that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have eternal life."
Peterson unpacked the story from Numbers 21:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"The Israelites are doing their usual fractious foolishness... and God sends among his suffering subjects poisonous snakes to bite them. The leaders go to Moses and say, 'You've got a pipeline to God—maybe you could ask him to call off the serpents.'
"And God says something very strange. He doesn't say, 'To hell with the Israelites—more snakes is what they need.' And he doesn't say, 'I produced the snakes, so I'll get rid of them.' He says, 'Have the Israelites gather their bronze, make a giant stake, put a serpent on it, and if they go look at it, the serpent's poison won't harm them.'
"I read that and I thought: that's exactly what psychotherapists discovered when they converged on the utility of exposure therapy. It's the pharmakon—a little of the poison that hurts you cures you... If you get people to voluntarily confront the things that are poisoning them, they become braver and more well-adapted."

Then Peterson drew the connection to Christ:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"Christ identifies with that bronze serpent. What's the most poisonous thing you could possibly face? The most unjust possible painful death and the ultimate confrontation with malevolence—that's what's dramatized in the passion story.
"The idea is that if we were courageous enough to look death in the face unflinchingly, and if we spent our time putting our finger on the source of evil itself, it would revitalize ourselves to a degree that would be unimaginable."

Dawkins was unimpressed:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"Well, it doesn't impress me... I don't understand why you would say that has divine inspiration."

**What Peterson Sees:** A profound concordance between an obscure Old Testament ritual, Christ's self-identification with that ritual, and modern psychological discoveries about trauma and healing. The probability that these connections are accidental seems vanishingly small to him.
**What Dawkins Sees:** A literary interpretation that may be clever but has nothing to do with factual truth. The question remains: did Jesus really die and rise again?

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part V: The Unity of Truth

The deepest philosophical disagreement emerged when Peterson articulated his core conviction:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"I think that at bottom, truth is unified, and what that's going to mean eventually is that the world of value and the world of fact coincide in some manner that we don't yet understand. And I think that that union, the fact of that union, is equivalent to what's being described as divine order across millennia.
"You either believe that the world of truth is unified in the final analysis, or you don't. Those are the options. And if it's not unified, then there's a contradiction between value and fact... I don't believe that."

Dawkins pushed back:

RICHARD DAWKINS:

"You implied there's no difference between whether the text is divinely inspired or whether it evolved in progression during a series of manuscripts. Now I think that's genuinely interesting, but it's a huge difference. It's not the same thing. Either it was divinely inspired or it wasn't."

Peterson's response was striking:

JORDAN PETERSON:

"It's the same thing if it's fundamentally reflective of—and accurately reflective of—the implicit logos or order. And I think it is."

This is Peterson's most controversial claim: that there is no ultimate difference between "divinely inspired" and "evolved to reflect deep truth." If the biblical texts accurately capture the structure of reality—including the moral and psychological structure—then they are divinely inspired in the only sense that matters, regardless of their mechanism of production.

#### The Theological Question

Is Peterson right that evolved truth and revealed truth are the same thing? This depends on what we mean by "divine." If God is the logos—the ordering principle of reality—and if the biblical texts have been refined over millennia to conform to that logos, then in some sense they are divinely inspired even if no prophet ever heard a voice from heaven.
But traditional Christianity makes stronger claims: that God has acted in history, that Jesus was bodily resurrected, that these are facts in the Dawkins sense. Peterson seems unwilling or unable to affirm these claims directly.

✦ ✦ ✦

## Part VI: A Christian Response

### What Peterson Gets Right

Peterson is correct that the biblical texts are not merely collections of factual claims. They are narratives, poems, laws, proverbs, prophecies—literary forms that communicate truth in ways that resist reduction to propositional statements. The story of Cain and Abel is true in a way that transcends the question of whether two specific individuals with those names ever existed.
Peterson is also correct that modern science cannot be separated from its Christian roots. The assumptions that the universe is orderly, that this order is intelligible to human minds, that truth-seeking is good—these are not derivable from science itself. They are pre-scientific commitments that emerged from the Judeo-Christian tradition.
And Peterson is correct that something profound is happening in the biblical texts that rewards deep study. The concordance between the bronze serpent and Christ's crucifixion is genuinely remarkable. The elaboration of the concept of sacrifice across the biblical narrative is genuinely impressive. These are not the products of naive or primitive minds.

### What Peterson Gets Wrong (or Incomplete)

But Peterson's reluctance to affirm Christianity's factual claims is problematic. Paul is explicit: "If Christ be not risen, then is our preaching vain, and your faith is also vain" (1 Corinthians 15:14). The resurrection is not merely a powerful symbol of psychological transformation. It is a claim about what happened in history—a claim that, if false, makes Christianity a noble lie rather than the truth.
Peterson treats "fact" and "value" as separate domains that must somehow be unified. But Christianity claims they were never separate. God is both the creator of facts and the source of values. The logos that orders the cosmos is the same logos that became flesh and dwelt among us. There is no gap to bridge because there was never a gap.
Peterson's approach also risks reducing Christianity to a useful fiction—a story that "works" psychologically without being true metaphysically. This is precisely what Dawkins suspects Peterson of doing, and Peterson's evasions on questions like the virgin birth do nothing to dispel that suspicion.

### What Dawkins Gets Right

Dawkins is correct that facts matter. Christianity is not merely a collection of inspiring stories. It makes claims about what actually happened: God created the world, Israel was chosen, Jesus was born, crucified, and raised. If these things did not happen, Christianity is false, however psychologically useful its narratives might be.
Dawkins is also correct to press for clarity. Peterson's reluctance to answer direct questions—"Do you believe Jesus was born of a virgin?"—is frustrating because it suggests either that he doesn't know what he believes or that he's unwilling to say so. Neither reflects well on him as a truth-seeker.

### What Dawkins Gets Wrong

But Dawkins' dismissal of symbolic and mythological truth is too hasty. The fact that dragons don't exist in the zoological sense doesn't mean the concept of "dragon" is meaningless. It is an abstraction of predation, and abstractions can be true in ways that are more important than particular instances.
Dawkins also underestimates the explanatory power of the biblical narratives. When Peterson points out that the concept of sacrifice underlies community formation, or that the hero myth corresponds to the structure of the mammalian nervous system, these are not trivial observations. They suggest that the biblical writers grasped something about human nature that modern science is only now rediscovering.
Most importantly, Dawkins fails to reckon with the implications of his own "cultural Christianity." If Christianity has produced a more humane civilization than its alternatives—and Dawkins admits it has—this demands explanation. Dawkins treats it as a coincidence. A Christian would say it is evidence that Christianity has grasped something true about reality.

### The Christian Synthesis

The Christian position is that both Peterson and Dawkins are partially right, and their partial truths are unified in Christ.
Dawkins is right that facts matter. Jesus really lived, really died, really rose. These are not merely symbols. They are events that occurred in space and time, witnessed by people who went to their deaths rather than deny what they had seen.
Peterson is right that patterns matter. The biblical narratives encode deep truths about human psychology and social organization that have been refined over millennia. The story of Cain and Abel is true not because Cain was a historical individual but because the pattern of envious resentment leading to violence is eternally recurrent.
The Christian claim is that both are true simultaneously. Jesus is both a historical person who walked the roads of Galilee AND the eternal logos, the pattern of patterns, the archetype of archetypes. The resurrection is both a fact—something that happened on a particular Sunday morning in a particular garden outside Jerusalem—AND the ultimate symbol of transformation, the victory of life over death that makes all other transformations possible.

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. The same was in the beginning with God. All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made... And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us, (and we beheld his glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father,) full of grace and truth."
— John 1:1-3, 14

The logos that Peterson sees encoded in the biblical narratives is not an abstraction. It is a person—the person of Christ. And that person entered history, not as a symbol, but as a man who could be touched, who ate fish after his resurrection, who showed Thomas his wounds.
This is the scandal of Christianity: not that it makes mythological claims (many religions do that), but that it makes factual claims about a myth becoming fact. As C.S. Lewis put it: "The heart of Christianity is a myth which is also a fact."

## Questions for Discussion

1. Dawkins accuses Peterson of being "drunk on symbols." Is this a fair criticism? Can someone be so focused on symbolic meaning that they lose sight of factual truth? Conversely, can someone be so focused on facts that they miss deeper meanings?
2. Peterson says that asking whether Cain existed is "a silly question—like asking whether Raskolnikov existed in Crime and Punishment." Do you agree? Is there a meaningful difference between a fictional character and a figure like Cain whose existence is affirmed by Scripture?
3. Dawkins identifies as a "cultural Christian" while rejecting Christianity's truth claims. Is this position coherent? Can you benefit from a culture's fruits while denying the roots that produced them?
4. Peterson claims that "there's no difference between whether the text is divinely inspired or whether it evolved to reflect deep truth." What do you think of this claim? Does it matter how the biblical texts came to be, as long as they are true?
5. The debate revealed surprising agreement on the Baldwin effect—the idea that learned behaviors can become genetically encoded. If Jungian archetypes have a biological basis, does this strengthen or weaken the case for their divine origin?
6. Peterson connected the bronze serpent in Numbers 21 to exposure therapy and to Christ's crucifixion. Do you find this connection compelling? What does it suggest about the unity of truth across Scripture?
7. Dawkins says he is "interested in things that were true before there were any humans and will be true long after humans are extinct." Is this the right criterion for truth? Are there truths that depend on the existence of persons?
8. How should Christians engage with thoughtful skeptics like Dawkins? What can we learn from Peterson's approach? What might we do differently?
9. Paul says, "If Christ be not risen, then is our preaching vain" (1 Corinthians 15:14). Does Christianity stand or fall on the factual truth of the resurrection? Can the symbolic meaning of the resurrection be separated from its historical reality?
10. The debate ended with both men acknowledging they have "different kinds of minds." Is this an insurmountable barrier to understanding, or is it possible for the scientific and mythological approaches to be unified? What would that unification look like?

### Analysis of the Dawkins-Peterson Discussion

The conversation between Richard Dawkins and Jordan Peterson, moderated by Alex O'Connor, revolves around core tensions between scientific empiricism and mythological/psychological interpretation of religious texts, particularly the Bible. Dawkins champions factual, evidence-based truth (e.g., verifiable claims like the virgin birth or resurrection), while Peterson emphasizes metaphorical, archetypal, and functional truths (e.g., stories as adaptive patterns for human behavior and society). They touch on memes vs. archetypes, the evolution of ideas, cultural Christianity, sacrifice, dragons as predator abstractions, and the Baldwin effect. The tone is respectful but frustrated—Dawkins presses for literal answers, Peterson circles symbolically, and they find tentative common ground on evolutionary mechanisms for ideas.

#### Strengths and Weaknesses of Dawkins' Position

Dawkins' stance is rooted in scientific materialism: truth must be empirical, predictive, and falsifiable. He dismisses religious claims without evidence (e.g., "I see no truth value in the claims of Christianity") and prioritizes "facts" over "symbols" or "myths." He uses quantum physics as a benchmark—deeply mysterious but validated by precise predictions (e.g., equivalent to measuring North America's width to a hair's breadth).
**Strengths**:

- **Clarity and Rigor**: Dawkins demands direct answers to factual questions (e.g., "Did Jesus have a father?"), exposing evasions and keeping the discussion grounded. This prevents woolly interpretations from masquerading as truth.
- **Predictive Power Emphasis**: He highlights science's tangible achievements (e.g., landing spacecraft on comets, evolutionary arms races between predators and prey), contrasting them with the Bible's lack of verifiable prophecies or discoveries.
- **Cultural Critique**: As a "cultural Christian," he acknowledges Christianity's mild ethical advantages (e.g., over Islamic fundamentalism's punishments) without endorsing its doctrines, showing nuance in separating heritage from belief.
- **Meme Concept**: His idea of memes as "mind viruses" spreading via imitation provides a Darwinian framework for cultural evolution, which even Peterson finds compatible with archetypes.

**Weaknesses**:

- **Narrow Definition of Truth**: By equating truth solely to empirical facts, Dawkins undervalues non-literal utilities (e.g., myths as psychological tools for resilience or community-building). He admits myths are "vaguely interesting" but secondary, potentially missing how they shape human motivation and ethics.
- **Dismissiveness of Depth**: He brushes off Peterson's symbolic interpretations (e.g., "I'm not interested in dragons. I'm interested in reality") without fully engaging their biological or adaptive merits, coming across as reductive.
- **Overlooks Preconditions for Science**: He concedes science has unscientific axioms (e.g., valuing truth, prioritizing investigations) but doesn't explore how Judeo-Christian assumptions (e.g., a unified, intelligible cosmos) might have enabled science's emergence in Europe—a point Peterson presses.
- **Limited Engagement with Evolution of Ideas**: While he invented memes, he resists Peterson's hierarchical view (e.g., memes as efflorescences of deeper archetypes), potentially underestimating how cultural evolution mirrors biological processes.

#### Strengths and Weaknesses of Peterson's Position

Peterson interprets the Bible psychologically and mythologically: stories like Cain and Abel or the bronze serpent represent eternal patterns (e.g., sibling rivalry, voluntary confrontation with fear). He sees unity between fact and value, with myths as adaptive "memes" evolving hierarchically (e.g., dragons as abstracted predators). Sacrifice, for him, underpins community and maturation, akin to delayed gratification.
**Strengths**:

- **Integrative Depth**: Peterson connects biology, psychology, and mythology (e.g., the hypothalamus mediating exploration vs. satiation, mirroring hero myths; the Baldwin effect linking memes to genetic assimilation). This offers a holistic view: myths aren't just fictions but functional for survival and ethics (e.g., prioritizing perceptions via stories).
- **Adaptive Utility**: He argues myths have "predictive power" through cultural success (e.g., Christianity "brought Rome to its knees"; voluntary exposure therapy echoing the bronze serpent). This frames religion as evolutionary—ideas "evolve" to fit human memory and needs.
- **Hierarchical Thinking**: His meme-archetype link (memes as surface expressions of instinctual depths) bridges Dawkins' ideas to broader history (e.g., Eliade's religious motifs), showing how transient fads (backwards baseball hats) tie to eternal motifs (god battles).
- **Defense of Metaphor**: He evades literalism productively at times (e.g., Cain as "hyper-real" like Dostoevsky's Raskolnikov), highlighting how stories reveal human patterns without needing historical facticity.

**Weaknesses**:

- **Evasiveness on Literals**: Peterson dodges direct questions (e.g., on virgin birth: "I don't know how to mediate the fact-value dichotomy"), frustrating empirical interlocutors. This can seem like obfuscation, as O'Connor notes—he admits not penetrating mysteries but claims shattering insights.
- **Verbosity and Circling**: His responses wander symbolically (e.g., long bronze serpent tangent), diluting focus. Dawkins calls him "drunk on symbols," and it risks prioritizing aesthetics over substance.
- **Over-Reliance on Analogy**: Equating divine inspiration with evolutionary emergence (e.g., "no difference" if reflective of logos) blurs boundaries, potentially dragging the divine "down to the mundane" (as O'Connor says). Claims like sacrifice as cortical maturation are insightful but stretch without empirical backing.
- **Cultural Bias**: He idealizes Judeo-Christian axioms (e.g., enabling science) but underplays barbarism in biblical texts (e.g., sacrifice as "unpleasant," per Dawkins). His optimism about myth's unity with fact assumes a "straight, narrow path" without proving it.

Overall, the debate highlights a classic rift: Dawkins excels in precision but risks sterility; Peterson in profundity but risks vagueness. They converge on memes/archetypes evolving via Baldwin-like effects, suggesting potential synthesis—cultural ideas becoming "instinctual" through selection.
