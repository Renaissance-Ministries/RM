# Three Conversations at One Table

## A Fellowship Meeting Analysis: Engagement, Mormonism Revisited, and the Trinitarian Geometry of the Cross

**Fellowship Discussion Essay | April 26, 2026**

**Source:** Sunday Zoom fellowship, April 26, 2026. Participants: Thomas Abshier, Michael Sherman, Charlie Gutierrez, Susan Gutierrez, Leonard Hofheins, Isak Gutierrez, Armond Boulware. Discussion arose from circulation of the [Mormonism fellowship essay](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/Fellowship_essays/260425-mormonism_claude_third_draft.md) (April 25, 2026) and the [phenomenology of the Cross essay](https://github.com/Renaissance-Ministries/RM/blob/main/Fellowship_essays/260425-phenomenology_of_the_cross_standalone.md).

To the Fellowship —

This morning's meeting was three conversations rather than one, and I want to honor each of them by giving each its own treatment. Mike Sherman joined us — my friend of sixty years, a lifelong searcher whom I had referenced in last week's essay on the Kingdom of God but had not, to my embarrassment, sent the essay to. He pressed us hard on the methodology of religious engagement. Leonard Hofheins, the closest among us to active Mormonism though now planted in the Denver Snuffer / Covenant Christian movement, took up the question of *which* Mormonism we had actually critiqued and whether his own position was even on the table. And Susan Gutierrez, after the rest had departed, returned to Addendum III of yesterday's essay with a framing of the Cross that was not opposed to mine but adjacent to it — and that adjacency, I believe, is the most theologically generative thing that came out of the morning.

Let me work through each in turn.

---

## I. Mike Sherman's Challenge — The Methodology of Engagement

Mike came in early and stayed an hour, and his challenge was the one a lifelong seeker would press: *if you have a vision for a Christian civic order, how does that vision avoid replicating the structural pathology of the Iranian theocracy?* He acknowledged my answer — that the substance differs even when the framework formally resembles — but pushed further into the question of *method*. How do you engage other religions, other denominations, other belief systems, without playing winner-take-all?

His position, summarized fairly, has three pillars:

**1. Map the precepts.** Before engaging anyone, identify where your worldview and theirs overlap. Different conversations require different starting points: a Mormon, a Sunni Muslim, a Buddhist, a Hindu — each demands a different first move because each shares a different subset of common ground. The Christian and the Mormon already share the Old and New Testaments. The Christian and the Muslim already share the Old and New Testaments and add the Quran. The Christian and the Buddhist share less doctrinally but may share much practically — care for strangers, compassion for the suffering, the discipline of contemplation. The map tells you where to start.

**2. Start with full-hearted agreement, climb upward.** Like a rock climber finding three secure limbs before reaching for a fourth, build from points of unanimous shared conviction toward the points of divergence. Never lead with the disagreement. Never frame the conversation as winner-loser. The aim is not to defeat but to *enjoin*.

**3. "Belief" without question is the pathology.** Mike's most provocative move was to characterize *belief itself* as the problem — the moment a position becomes immune to revision, it becomes a wall, a tribe, a jersey-color, a justification for violence. He resists "belief" in favor of percentage-based confidence: even where he is 99.9% certain, he leaves 0.1% room for the possibility he is wrong, and he holds that residual humility as the precondition for genuine inquiry.

This is a thoughtful position, and it deserves a thoughtful response.

### What is true in it

Mike is right that the *map-the-precepts, start-from-agreement, climb-toward-divergence* method is, in nearly all human encounters, the more fruitful pastoral approach. Christ did this with the Samaritan woman at the well: He did not lead with her five husbands. He led with her thirst. The thirst was the agreement; the husbands were the climb. Paul did this on Mars Hill: he did not lead with the resurrection. He led with the unknown god the Athenians already worshipped, and only then climbed toward the One who *raised this Man from the dead*. The method is biblical and it works.

Mike is also right that *tribalism dressed in theology* is a pathology — and Christianity has produced its share of it. The Thirty Years' War, the witch trials, denominational wars over secondary doctrines: Mike's century-of-Christian-on-Christian-bloodshed indictment is not unfair, and we should not be defensive about it. Where Christians have killed other Christians over jersey colors rather than substance, we are *more* at fault than less, because we knew better. *"By this shall all men know that ye are my disciples, if ye have love one to another"* (John 13:35) judges us before it judges any outsider.

And Mike is right that the *aim* is enjoinment, not defeat. We do not preach Christ in order to win an argument. We preach Christ because He *is* the truth into whose communion we are inviting every soul we meet. *"Compel them to come in"* (Luke 14:23) — but the compulsion is the urgency of love, not the threat of force.

### What is incomplete in it

Where Mike's framing is incomplete is at the point where the method runs out. The map-and-climb approach assumes that *all genuine disagreement reduces to a not-yet-discovered shared precept*. That assumption is true within a great deal of inter-Christian dialogue, much inter-religious dialogue, and most ordinary ethical conversation. But it is not true at the deepest level. There are points where two systems do not merely emphasize different parts of a shared map — there are points where the maps are of different territories.

The map-and-climb method assumes the ladder always reaches the top. But on certain irreducible questions — *Is Jesus God? Did He rise bodily? Is salvation by His finished work or by my own progressive earning? Is there one God or are there many? Is a creature ever a creator?* — the answer is yes or no. There is no overlap-percentage that makes "Jesus is God" and "Jesus progressed to godhood" both 80% true. They are, structurally, mutually exclusive. One set of beings is held in being by the One who is *I AM*. The other set thinks it is becoming an *I AM* itself. These are not different climbs up the same ladder. They are different ladders against different walls.

Mike's residual-humility principle ("leave 0.1% open") is, in a sense, my position too — I would never claim 100% certainty about my own apprehension of the truth. But the 0.1% residual cannot extend to the *content* of the gospel without dissolving the gospel. I can say "I might be wrong about my interpretation of Romans 9," and I can say "I might be wrong about the date of Daniel's authorship," and I can even say "I might be wrong about the proper mode of baptism." But I cannot say, with intellectual coherence, "I might be wrong that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God." That sentence, said by a Christian, is no longer a Christian sentence. *"He that believeth not the Son shall not see life; but the wrath of God abideth on him"* (John 3:36) does not negotiate to 99.9%.

So Mike's method is a sound *first-order strategy* for engagement — and we should adopt it. But it is not a *final theology*, and it cannot resolve the irreducible question of which Christ is being preached and whether He is the Christ He claimed to be.

### The synthesis

Here is how I would put it back to Mike if we had another hour:

The method is right, and we adopt it gladly. Map the precepts; start where we agree; climb together toward the harder ground. *And* — at some point on the climb, every honest seeker arrives at a question that cannot be subdivided into smaller agreements. Did Jesus rise from the dead? Is He God in the flesh? Is His finished work the only ground of my standing before the Father? At that ledge, the map runs out and the question is binary. Mike's lifelong searching has been, I believe, an honoring of the climb. The question I would ask him, with all the love sixty years can hold, is whether he has yet been willing to reach for the ledge — or whether the searching itself has become a way of avoiding the moment when the climb stops being climbing and becomes a choice.

We do not climb forever. The climb is meant to bring us to a Person.

## II. Leonard's Position — Mormonism Beyond Salt Lake

Leonard's contribution clarified something I had not adequately understood. The Mormonism essay was framed around Stanley, the Salt Lake LDS stake president, and Leonard's first move was to remind us that *Stanley's Mormonism is one of approximately 130 schismatic descendants of Joseph Smith's original movement* — and Leonard does not belong to that one. Leonard is in the Covenant Christian movement, planted in Denver Snuffer's teaching, and his position is sufficiently distinct from Salt Lake LDS that the Mormonism essay only obliquely addressed him.

Three things Leonard surfaced are worth the fellowship's careful attention.

### 1. Salt Lake LDS as institutional apostasy from its own founding claims

Leonard's most striking critique was *internal* to the Mormon tradition. He argued — with examples — that the modern Salt Lake institution has departed from its own founding sources. Examples he raised: the recent prohibition by Russell Nelson against using the word "Mormon" (despite the Book of Mormon being the cornerstone scripture of the movement); the lawsuit by the Salt Lake corporation against John Dehlin for using "Mormon" in his podcast title; the wealth concentration in the Ensign Peak Advisors portfolio (~$200 billion); the steady alteration of the temple endowment over generations.

This is not a critique I would have made from outside, and I am grateful Leonard made it from inside. If Leonard is right — and he has the firsthand evidence the rest of us lack — then the Salt Lake institution is not the faithful continuation of Joseph Smith's movement but a corporatized successor that has substituted institutional power for the original spiritual claim. This is a serious charge, and one that the Mormonism essay's Section II.5 (Canon and the Restoration Claim) only addresses obliquely.

We should sharpen the essay's framing accordingly: the critique of Salt Lake LDS is not merely a Christian critique from outside. It is *also* a critique that paleo-Mormons like Leonard make from inside, on the grounds that the modern institution has betrayed its own scripture and its own founding prophet. That convergence — outsiders and insiders reaching some of the same conclusions for some of the same reasons — is worth naming explicitly.

### 2. The Bible-correction claim

Where Leonard and I came nearest to substantive disagreement was on the question of whether the Bible requires correction. Leonard's position, as best I could parse it, is that the Bible was *originally true* but *subsequently mistranslated* in places, and that Joseph Smith was commanded by the Lord to produce a corrected version (the Joseph Smith Translation, or JST). Leonard maintains that the Covenant Christian movement is the only group in nearly two centuries since Joseph Smith to have completed and published this corrective work.

I pressed on this because it is load-bearing. If the JST corrects substantive doctrine, then the Bible *as historically received* is insufficient for the church's life — and the church needs a 19th-century American supplement to function. If the JST corrects only minor matters of phrasing, then it is not load-bearing and the question is one of textual scholarship rather than authority.

Leonard's answer oscillated, perhaps because the question is genuinely tense. He said the JST is *"very helpful to know the mind and will of God"* — which is a substantive claim. But he also said, in effect, that the JST was optional rather than required: that those who love their existing Bible should feel free to stick with it. The first claim makes the JST authoritative; the second makes it optional. Both cannot be simultaneously true in the way Leonard wants them to be.

I want to engage this charitably. There *are* textual variants in the Hebrew and Greek manuscripts that scholarship has illuminated over centuries — the Bible itself is a product of careful textual criticism, and faithful Christians can and do disagree about the best rendering of a particular verse. If the JST were merely Joseph Smith's contribution to *that* conversation — one inspired commentator's reading, weighed alongside other inspired commentators' readings — there would be no theological difficulty. Christians have always been free to weigh interpretive traditions.

But that is not the JST's claim about itself. The JST claims *prophetic authority* equal to or greater than the apostolic text it corrects. And there is the difficulty. The Bible's own self-witness — *"All scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness"* (2 Timothy 3:16), and *"the faith which was once delivered unto the saints"* (Jude 1:3) — does not leave room for a 19th-century prophet to *re-deliver* the faith. It was delivered once. The text we have is the text the Holy Spirit has preserved through centuries of careful copying, translation, and church reception. Joseph Smith's claim to *correct* it is not an incremental scholarly contribution. It is a reopening of the canon — and the canon, Christianity has consistently held, is closed.

This is the genuine point of disagreement between Leonard and the rest of us, and it should be named clearly: *the canon question is the load-bearing question*. Either the apostles delivered the faith once for all (the historic Christian position), or they delivered it provisionally pending later prophetic correction (the Mormon position in any of its forms). These cannot both be true.

### 3. Denver Snuffer and the claim of face-to-face contact

The most delicate matter in Leonard's testimony was his account of Denver Snuffer. Leonard described Snuffer not as a prophet in the institutional sense, but as a man who has had direct face-to-face encounters with Christ — comparable, Leonard explicitly said, to Moses on Sinai or Paul on the Damascus road.

This is a serious claim, and I want to engage it with the seriousness it deserves rather than dismissing it.

There are precedents in church history for individuals claiming direct revelatory experience of Christ. Some have been genuine (Paul, certainly; the apostle John on Patmos). Some have been mystical traditions of various depths within historic Christianity (Teresa of Avila, Julian of Norwich). And some have been the founding visions of new movements — Joseph Smith, Ellen White, Muhammad — whose claims are doctrinally consequential in proportion to their authority over their followers.

The biblical test is not whether the experience felt real to the person who had it. The biblical test is the *content* of what was received and whether it accords with *"the faith which was once delivered unto the saints."* Paul submitted his Damascus-road revelation to the apostles in Jerusalem (Galatians 1:18-2:10) precisely so the church could verify that what he was preaching was the same gospel the original apostles preached. The verification was not an insult to Paul. It was the proper procedure for any prophetic claim.

So the question I would put to Leonard, with full respect, is this: *what is the content of what Snuffer claims to have received, and how does it compare to the faith once delivered?* If the content squares fully with the apostles' deposit, then Snuffer is at most a teacher who is preaching what we already have — useful, perhaps, but not adding to the canon. If the content departs from the apostles' deposit at any structurally significant point, then *"though we, or an angel from heaven, preach any other gospel unto you than that which we have preached unto you, let him be accursed"* (Galatians 1:8). Paul's foreclosure is unconditional.

Leonard, you know I love you. The question is not whether Snuffer is sincere, or whether his life is upright, or whether his teachings produce some spiritual fruit. The question is whether the Christ he meets face-to-face is the Christ of the apostolic deposit. That is the *only* question, and it is one you and I should be willing to examine together.

### Susan's procedural insight

Susan offered a procedural move that I think is exactly right. She suggested that *before* we engage the periphery of Leonard's claims (the JST corrections, Snuffer's teachings, the Mormon community), we should establish whether the *root* — the credibility of Joseph Smith's founding visions — is sound. Because everything downstream depends on that root.

The argument she gestured toward is biblically grounded: *no man hath seen God at any time* (John 1:18; cf. 1 Timothy 6:16, 1 John 4:12). If the canonical witness is that no man has seen the Father, then Joseph Smith's first vision — in which he claimed to see *both* the Father and the Son in a wooded grove — runs into immediate Scriptural contradiction. And Scripture provides the alternative explanation: *"Satan himself is transformed into an angel of light"* (2 Corinthians 11:14). A vision that contradicts Scripture is not necessarily a fabrication; it may be a genuine experience of *something else*.

This is the conversation Leonard and I should have had years ago, and it is the one we should have now. Not whether the Bible was mistranslated. Not whether Snuffer is faithful. But whether the founding vision of Mormonism, as Joseph Smith described it, is consistent with what Scripture says is possible. That is the load-bearing question, and Susan named it correctly.

### What Leonard added that we should keep

I want to be specific about what Leonard's testimony genuinely added to my understanding, regardless of where the doctrinal disagreements settle:

- **The Salt Lake institution is not synonymous with Mormonism.** Future critiques should specify which Mormonism is being addressed.
- **There is internal Mormon dissent of substantial theological seriousness.** People like Leonard, Snuffer, Dehlin, and the Covenant Christian movement are not silent dissidents; they are working out a paleo-Mormon theology in real time.
- **The wealth and corporate structure of Salt Lake LDS deserves more scrutiny than the original essay gave it.** When the President of the institution holds personal authority over $200 billion of assets in a sole corporation, the "captives to be rescued" framing of the original essay is, if anything, understated.
- **The Joseph Smith claim of pre-Smith Christian apostasy is not unique to Salt Lake LDS.** It is the founding-claim of every branch of the Restoration movement, including Leonard's. It is, therefore, the load-bearing claim that has to be addressed in any conversation with any Mormon of any kind.

## III. The Trinitarian Geometry of the Cross — Synthesizing Susan's Framing with the Phenomenology View

After the others departed, Susan and I returned to Addendum III of yesterday's essay — the phenomenology of the Cross. Susan offered a framing that was not opposed to mine but adjacent to it, and the integration of the two views, I believe, is the most theologically generative thing the morning produced. Let me articulate her view as I understand it, then mine, and then the synthesis.

### Susan's framing: the Father's withdrawal of life-source

Susan's account focuses on the **Trinitarian geometry of the event** — the relations between Father, Son, and Spirit, and what changed in those relations during the hours of the cross.

In her telling: God the Father is *the source of life* — the one in whose presence all life subsists. The Son, though God Himself, lived His earthly life in continuous communion with the Father, drawing life from the Father in the way human beings *should* but cannot because of sin. When the Son took on the totality of human sin in the body, the Father — who *cannot be in the presence of sin* — withdrew. The withdrawal was not punitive in the petty sense; it was *constitutive* — the Father's holiness simply cannot occupy the same metaphysical space as sin. And so the Son, who had been continuously sustained by the Father, was *cut off from the life-source* for the duration of the bearing.

This is what *"My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"* (Matthew 27:46) is reporting. It is the Son experiencing, for the first time in eternity, the *absence* of the Father — the experience of being cut off from the life of God. That cutting-off *is* the experience of the wrath. And then He died — bearing the death we deserved, not merely in physical termination but in the *severance from the source* that physical death represents.

### Susan's framing extended: what the Resurrection then means

In Susan's account, what makes the resurrection theologically necessary is that the Son endured the severance *without sinning*. He did not, in the moment of being cut off, curse God. He did not abandon faith. He bore the abandonment in trust — *"into thy hands I commend my spirit"* (Luke 23:46) — and so the Father, who had withdrawn life, was able to *return it* in vindication. The resurrection is not merely the reversal of death. It is the Father's restoration of the life-source to the Son who endured the severance faithfully — and, by extension, the opening of that restored life to all who are in the Son.

This is, I believe, a view that has deep roots in Reformed and patristic theology. The Father's *forsaking* of the Son at Calvary is not an innovation in Susan's account; it is what Calvin meant when he wrote of Christ bearing the punishment we deserved, and what the older theologians meant when they spoke of Christ bearing Hell in our place. Hell, on this view, is *cutting-off-from-the-Father* — and the Son endured it that we might not have to.

### My framing: the absorption of the recoil

The phenomenology view I argued in Addendum III takes a different vantage point — not the relation between Father and Son, but the relation between *God's nature and sin* and the question of where the recoil is borne.

In that framing: God's nature is constituted such that sin produces an absolute recoil — the wrath is what God *is* in the presence of sin. That recoil has to be experienced somewhere. Either the sinner experiences it (in eternal separation), or it is absorbed by the One Being whose absorption could be sufficient — God in flesh, on the cross. The Son, fully God, took the totality of human sin into His own divine consciousness and bore the recoil of His own nature against that sin within His own being. The wrath is *absorbed*, and once absorbed, discharged — *"It is finished"* — and there is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Him.

### Why these two views are not in conflict

Here is where the synthesis becomes generative. Susan's view and mine are looking at the same event from different vantage points, and each illuminates what the other emphasizes less.

**Susan's view supplies the Trinitarian geometry that mine left underspecified.** My phenomenology framing speaks of the Son absorbing the recoil "in His own divine consciousness," but it does not adequately specify *what changed in the eternal communion of Father and Son for that absorption to occur*. Susan's view names it: the Father withdrew the life-source, the Son experienced the cutting-off, and the cry of forsakenness is the report from inside that Trinitarian event. Without Susan's framing, my view risks abstracting the absorption into a single divine person operating without reference to the others. *That is not what the New Testament describes.* The forsaking is between Persons.

**My view supplies the ontological mechanism that Susan's left underspecified.** Susan's framing names *that* the Father withdrew, but does not fully account for *why* withdrawal constitutes the bearing of the wrath. The phenomenology view answers: because the wrath is the response of God's nature to sin, and the Father's withdrawal is the active form of that response *as it is brought to bear on the Son who has taken on the sin*. The cutting-off is not arbitrary punishment. It is the *ontological* outworking of the divine nature against the sin the Son has assumed. Without my framing, Susan's view risks making the forsaking look like a juridical decision the Father made rather than the structural response of holiness to sin.

**Together, the two views describe a single event from inside and from outside.** Susan describes the event from inside the Trinitarian relations — what happened *between* Father and Son. I describe it from inside the divine nature's response to sin — what happened *because of* who God is in the presence of what we have done. Both vantage points are real. Both are scriptural. And both are necessary if we are going to articulate the Cross with adequate depth.

### The fuller synthesis

Let me attempt the synthesis in one paragraph and then unfold it.

*On the Cross, the Son took into Himself the totality of human sin. The Father — whose nature recoils absolutely from sin and who therefore cannot abide in the presence of what the Son had assumed — withdrew the life-source that had eternally sustained the Son in their indissoluble communion. The Son, in the body of His flesh, experienced the withdrawal as both forsakenness and as the white-hot heat of the divine nature's response to the sin He bore. The cutting-off and the absorption are the same event seen from two angles: the Trinitarian-relational angle (the Father withdraws; the Son is forsaken) and the ontological-phenomenological angle (the recoil is borne; the wrath is discharged). Because the Son bore both — the relational severance and the ontological recoil — without sinning, the Father vindicated Him in the resurrection, and through Him opens to all who are in Him both the restoration of the life-source (the Spirit applied to the believer's heart) and the discharge of the recoil (the believer stands in Christ where the wrath has already had its event).*

Three things follow from this synthesis that I believe matter pastorally and apologetically:

**First, the believer's standing has both a relational and an ontological dimension.** Relationally, in Christ, the Father no longer withdraws from us — He has restored the life-source through the Spirit. Ontologically, in Christ, the recoil against our sin has already been borne — it cannot fall on us a second time. *Both* are true. Pastoral preaching that names only the relational ("God is your Father; come home") risks softening what was actually borne. Pastoral preaching that names only the ontological ("the wrath is discharged") risks abstracting the gospel into a transaction. The fullness is the relational restoration *grounded in* the ontological discharge.

**Second, the Trinitarian geometry rules out modalism.** If the Father did not actually withdraw and the Son did not actually experience forsakenness, then the cry from the cross is theatrical rather than real — and we lose the ground of the Son's *real* identification with us in our forsakenness. But if the withdrawal is real, then the Father and the Son are genuinely distinct Persons capable of being in distinguishable relational states. This is what Trinitarian orthodoxy has always maintained. The cross is the deepest evidence that Father and Son are not interchangeable masks of a single divine actor; they are eternally distinct Persons in eternal communion, and the rupture-and-restoration of that communion is what redemption actually consists of.

**Third, this view illuminates why exclusively divine Christology is not optional.** A Christ who is not God cannot bear the recoil; that was the argument of the phenomenology essay. But equally — a Christ who is not in eternal Trinitarian communion with the Father from before the foundation of the world cannot *be forsaken*, because there is nothing to be forsaken from. Both halves of the argument require full Trinitarian Christology. The Mormon spirit-brother Christ, the Arian Christ, the modalist Christ, the Christ of liberal theology — none of them can be the Christ of Calvary, because none of them is in the right Trinitarian position to bear what was borne. Susan's framing and mine, taken together, foreclose every sub-Trinitarian Christology by making explicit *what was happening between the Persons* during the hours of the bearing.

### A possible third installment

The April 18 essay argued *that* the Cross is the unique solution. The April 25 essay argued *what the wrath actually is*. This morning's conversation suggests a third installment: *what the Trinitarian geometry of redemption actually looks like* — the eternal communion, the rupture under sin, the absorption and the forsakenness as one event seen from two angles, and the resurrection as the Father's restoration of the life-source. I will draft it when the time is right. For now, I want to record this morning's conversation as the seed.

## IV. What This Meeting Taught Me

Three things I leave the meeting holding more firmly than I held them before:

**1. Mike's method is right at the first order, and inadequate at the deepest order, and we need to hold both honestly.** Map the precepts. Climb from agreement. Honor the searcher. *And* do not pretend that every disagreement reduces to a not-yet-discovered overlap. Some questions are binary. The question of *which Christ* is one of them. We owe it to Mike — and to every searcher of his lifelong sincerity — to walk the climb with him, *and* to be honest with him about the ledge.

**2. The Mormon question is not one question but three.** It is a question about the Salt Lake corporation (which Leonard rightly critiques from inside). It is a question about Joseph Smith's founding visions (which Susan rightly identifies as the load-bearing root). And it is a question about ongoing prophetic claims (Snuffer being the present test case). Future engagement with Mormonism — in any of its 130 forms — should distinguish all three rather than collapsing them.

**3. The Cross is deeper than any single framing can hold, and the fellowship is exactly where the fuller framing emerges.** Susan and I came to the meeting with adjacent but unintegrated views. We left with — I believe — a synthesis neither of us would have reached alone. *"Iron sharpeneth iron; so a man sharpeneth the countenance of his friend"* (Proverbs 27:17). This is what the fellowship is for. This is why we meet. And this is why every conversation that happens at this table, however meandering it appears in the moment, is feeding work that matters.

## V. A Note on Mike

I want to close with a personal word, knowing he will read this.

Mike, sixty years of friendship is a gift God has given both of us, and I do not take it lightly. Your searching has been faithful in a way I respect deeply — you have never settled for a comfortable answer, never let yourself be conscripted by a tribe, never allowed your intellect to be shut down by a wall. That is genuinely admirable, and the fellowship is better for your presence at this table.

I want to say one thing more, and I say it because of the friendship rather than around it.

The searching is not the destination. At some point, every honest searcher arrives at the question that cannot be subdivided into smaller agreements: *who is Jesus, and what did He do?* You and I have had this conversation in pieces over decades. I want you to know that the door is open whenever you want to have it whole. Not as winner-and-loser. Not as a defeat of your lifelong inquiry. As the moment in the climb when the climbing brings us to a Person — the Person you have been searching for, whether or not you have yet recognized Him by that name.

We will keep meeting. I will keep loving you. And I will keep praying that the God who searches the hearts of every searcher will, in His time, finish the climb you have been faithfully making.

Thomas
